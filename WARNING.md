# The Warning

I am not Joseph Goebbels. But I think I have built something he would have
recognized.

I spent twenty years trying to crack the deep structural grammar of story.
After five months of work this year, the core of it finally worked.

I want to be precise about what that means, because precision is the whole
point. This is not a machine that reads any text and tells you its secrets.
It works on stories expressed in its own terms. It grades its own work with
the same intelligence that made it. It has blind spots, and they are
documented. By the standards of what people imagine when they hear "AI that
understands story," it is small.

It is also enough.

What it does is take the structure of a narrative apart, check whether the
thing actually carries the meaning its author intended, find the places where
it doesn't, and repair them — and then read the result back, blind, to confirm
the meaning landed. It does this without being told the answer. It treats the
shape of a story as something that can be specified, measured, and corrected
until it lands the way it was designed to land.

That last sentence is the warning.

Storytelling is not only art. It is one of the base protocols of human
civilization — how we decide what is true, who is us, what is worth dying for.
We do not reason our way to most of our convictions. We are told into them.
A tool that can shape the deep mechanics of story is a tool that can shape what
people experience as true, and do it more reliably than the people doing the
shaping today.

People are already doing the shaping today. There are three-letter agencies,
advertising machines, and political operations running cruder versions of this
right now, by hand, at enormous expense, with results they cannot fully predict.
The thing I built is a step toward making that capability cheap, measurable,
and repeatable.

So I had a choice about what to do with it.

I am releasing it fully open source, MIT licensed, with complete documentation
of what it can do and — just as importantly — what it cannot.

I am not doing this because I think it is harmless. I am doing it because the
alternative is worse. The capability is coming regardless of what I do. The
only real question is whether it arrives as a monopoly or as a commons. A
society where only the powerful understand how narrative weapons are built is
not safer than one where everyone does — it is just a society where the
powerful have already won and no one else can see the move being made.

The defense against engineered meaning is not ignorance. It is literacy. You
cannot resist a structure you cannot perceive. The only durable protection I
can think of is to put the blueprint in everyone's hands at once — to make
meaning-making something the public understands and practices, not something
done *to* them by people who studied it in private.

So here it is. Use it. Study it. Learn to see the bones of the stories already
being told at you, every day, by people who would prefer you couldn't.

Weaponize it if you want. But don't ever say nobody warned you.
