The dark was total. Bosola had known darkness before — the hold of a galley, the stinking belly of a prison where even rats navigated by memory — but the Cardinal's palace at Rome offered a darkness of a different quality, architectural, deliberate, the kind that money builds into stone. He pressed his back against the cold wall of the gallery and listened.

He had made his compact with the Cardinal not an hour past. The old churchman had laid it out with that particular administrative calm that made murder sound like bookkeeping: the doors would be locked, the courtiers warned to ignore any cry, and in the solitude thus arranged, Bosola would do what Bosola did. What Bosola had always done. The instrument's work. But the Cardinal did not know — could not know — that the instrument had slipped its handle. That Bosola's blade, when it fell tonight, was meant for the Cardinal himself.

He breathed. He waited. The gallery stretched before him in perfect blackness, and somewhere ahead — or behind, or beside, in the geometry that darkness dissolves — a footstep sounded.

Not the Cardinal's step. He knew that tread, had catalogued it across months of attendance: deliberate, unhurried, the step of a man who expected floors to bear him. This was lighter. Hesitant. The step of a man entering a place he had no certain right to be.

Bosola's hand found the hilt. His mind, which had been running the Cardinal's death like a rehearsed speech — the approach, the accusation, the blade — adjusted with the swiftness of long practice. Someone was here who should not be here. In this palace, on this night, that could only mean an obstacle or an enemy.

The footsteps came closer. Bosola could smell something now — road-dust, the faint sourness of a man who had travelled hard and recently. Not a courtier, then. Not a servant.

He moved.

The body was close — closer than he had calculated. His left hand found cloth, a doublet, and in the same instant his right drove the blade forward. The steel met resistance, then didn't. The man gasped — not a scream, a gasp, the sound of someone who had expected to find a door and found instead the end of everything — and Bosola felt the weight come against him, a body losing its architecture, becoming mere matter in his arms.

He lowered the man to the floor. The breathing was wrong already, wet, with a whistle beneath it that Bosola recognized from the Duchess's prison, from a dozen acts he could not wash from his hands. He had struck true. He had struck, as always, precisely where the work required.

"Who—" Bosola's voice came out rough. He had not planned to speak. "Who are you?"

The answer was a cough, then a word, then the word again, and the word unmade him.

"Antonio—" the dying man said. His own name. Offering it like a credential at a gate. "Antonio Bologna."

The dark split open inside Bosola's skull. Not with light — there was no light, there would be no light — but with the sudden, annihilating comprehension of what his hand had done. Antonio. Antonio Bologna. The Duchess's husband. The man he had meant to find, to warn, to arm, to set beside him in the work of vengeance against the brothers who had strangled her. The one man in all of Italy whose life Bosola needed, and Bosola had put a blade through his lung in a black corridor because he could not tell one set of footsteps from another.

"No," Bosola said. The word was useless. He said it again. "No. No."

He was on his knees now. His hands found Antonio's face — he could feel the features, the bones of a face he had watched across years at Amalfi, the steward's face, the secret husband's face, a face he had reported on to Ferdinand with such diligent precision that it might as well have been his own blade, even then, drawing the line from the Duchess's bedchamber to the Duchess's coffin. And now this. The final report. The final instrument's work, carried out on the one man the instrument had chosen freely.

Antonio's hand found Bosola's wrist. The grip was weak but deliberatein the way a man is deliberate when he has only seconds left in which to be deliberate about anything.

"Bosola," Antonio said. He had recognized the voice, or the smell of him, or the particular quality of violence — it did not matter how. The name arrived in his mouth with a terrible clarity, and Bosola heard in it the whole arc of Antonio's knowledge landing at once: the intelligencer, the spy, the man who had fed their secret to Ferdinand piece by piece, and now, in the dark, the man who had killed him while meaning to save him. Antonio saw it. Bosola could feel him seeing it — the grip on his wrist tightened, then loosened, then tightened again, as if the dying man were testing whether comprehension could be held in the hand.

"I came to make peace," Antonio whispered. "With the Cardinal. I thought — if I came to him directly — some reconciliation—"

The words were slowing. Bosola pressed his hand against the wound, stupidly, as if pressure could undo steel. The blood was warm and copious and it would not stop because wounds do not stop when the man who made them regrets them.

"I would have joined you," Bosola said. His voice cracked on the word. "I would have — I came here tonight to kill him. The Cardinal. For her. For what they did to her. And you — I would have had you beside me — I would have—"

Antonio's breath caught. Something shifted in his face — Bosola felt it under his palm, the muscles of the jaw working, the terrible labor of a man assembling understanding out of the last seconds available to him. The recognition was complete. Antonio saw it all: that Bosola had turned, that Bosola meant revenge, that the very act Antonio had undertaken — reconciliation, the gentle path, the path of a man who observed more than he initiated — had walked him directly into the blade of the one ally he did not know he had. The reconciliation and the vengeance had met in the dark and the reconciliation had died of it.

"Let me not think of it," Antonio said. His voice was barely there now, a thread of sound in the enormous dark. "The weakness of the thing—"

He meant, Bosola understood, the weakness of arriving too late to one's own life. Of understanding, in the final breath, the shape of everything — the marriage, the flight, the parting at the crossroads where the Duchess had sent him away with their eldest son, the long exile, the decision to return, the footstep in the dark gallery — and being powerless to alter a single line of it. Antonio saw. He saw clearly. And the seeing was worth nothing at all.

The hand on Bosola's wrist released.

Another set of footsteps, these ones faster, and a voice Bosola knew: Delio, Antonio's friend, calling softly into the dark. "Antonio? Antonio, are you—"

Delio stumbled upon them. Bosola heard the sharp intake of breath, the moment of incomprehension, and then the sound a man makes when he kneels beside the body of his friend and finds it emptied.

"He is dead," Bosola said. He heard his own voice as if from a great distance — flat, reportorial, the intelligencer's voice delivering its final intelligence. "I have killed him. I who would have died for him."

Delio said nothing. There was nothing to say. The dark held all three of them — the dead man who had come to make peace, the killer who had come to make war, and the friend who had come too late for either — and the dark would not answer for any of it.

Bosola rose. Antonio's blood was on his hands, his sleeves, his knees where he had knelt. It was no different from the Duchess's death, he thought. It was exactly the same. The instrument performs its function. The instrument does not choose. Except that he had chosen — he had chosen, tonight, for the first time, and the choice had killed the man he chose.

He left Antonio on the cold floor of the Cardinal's gallery and moved deeper into the palace, toward the room where the Cardinal waited, and his hands did not shake, because an instrument's hands never shake, even when the instrument is broken past all mending.
