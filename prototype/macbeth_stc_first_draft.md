A bleeding captain, half-carried by two soldiers, was telling the king what had happened.

Duncan stood among his retinue on a low rise above the road south of Forres, wrapped against the cold in a cloak too fine for a field report. He was not a martial king. His hands were a scholar's hands, pale and uncalloused, and he held them clasped before him as though receiving a homily rather than a dispatch from slaughter. Around him his lords listened — old Lennox, young Ross still mud-spattered from his own ride — and the king's two sons, Malcolm and Donalbain, stood close enough to hear. Malcolm had been there, at the battle's edge. He had nearly been taken. He knew what the captain's wounds meant.

"Brave Macbeth," the captain said — the word came out almost laughing, delirious — "well he deserves that name." He described it: the rebel Macdonwald's forces thick as summer flies, the outcome uncertain, and then Macbeth carving through the press of bodies like a man affronted by the existence of opposition. He had not paused. He had not negotiated. He had unseamed Macdonwald from the nave to the chops and fixed his head upon the battlements. The captain said this with something between awe and nausea, then swayed on his feet and had to be held upright.

Duncan touched the man's shoulder. "Go get him surgeons," he said, and the gentleness in it was absolute — a king who could not look at a wound without wanting it healed, who turned first to the injured man and only second to the victory. This was Duncan's way. He governed by gratitude.

Ross arrived with the rest of it: the Thane of Cawdor had turned traitor, joined the Norwegian assault, and that too had broken against Macbeth's blade. Two armies defeated in a single day. Scotland's borders held because one man had held them.

"No more that Thane of Cawdor shall deceive our bosom interest," Duncan said, and his voice carried the only hardness it would ever carry — the hardness of a man betrayed, not of a man who punished easily. "Go pronounce his present death, and with his former title greet Macbeth."

Cawdor's title stripped and given, just like that. Duncan's generosity ran ahead of ceremony, ahead even of prudence. He gave because giving was what a good king did, and he could not imagine that a gift might be a danger — that to heap honours on a man already brave enough to unseam another from nave to chops was to build a ladder he might one day climb too high.

The news would travel. It would reach Macbeth on the road home, and before it reached him, other news — stranger news, spoken by stranger mouths — would reach him first.

But that is ahead.

What matters now: a letter. Macbeth wrote it almost immediately, as soon as the witches vanished from the heath and Ross's confirmation of the Cawdor title made the impossible half-possible. He wrote to his wife. Not to the king, not to Banquo's family, not to any ally or commander — to her. *"This have I thought good to deliver thee, my dearest partner of greatness, that thou mightst not lose the dues of rejoicing by being ignorant of what greatness is promised thee."*

Dearest partner of greatness. The phrase tells everything. She was not his ornament, not his dependent, not his consolation after battle. She was the other half of whatever he was, and the first thing ambition wanted — before it wanted a crown, before it wanted anything — was to share itself with her.

At Inverness, Lady Macbeth would read that letter standing alone in a room she governed as completely as her husband governed a battlefield. She would read it and understand not only what he had been promised but what he could not, by his nature, seize alone. She knew him. She knew the mixture: the ambition absolute, the will not quite equal to it. She loved what she knew.

But that, too, is ahead.

Here is the status quo, intact and entire: Scotland has a good king. The king has a loyal kinsman, his greatest warrior, newly honoured with a second title. The warrior has a wife who is his equal and his confidant. The kingdom is at peace because the battle has been won. Every bond — sovereign to subject, husband to wife, host to guest — is whole.

Everything the world is about to lose, it possesses now.

The heath was wrong before they saw it.

Macbeth and Banquo had been riding since morning, the battle's exhaustion settling into their bones like cold water, and neither had spoken for some time. The moor stretched flat and featureless under a sky the colour of old pewter, and the air had thickened — not fog exactly, but a refusal of clarity, as though the landscape had decided not to commit to being seen. Macbeth's horse shied once, then again, at nothing he could identify.

"So foul and fair a day I have not seen," he said, half to himself.

He did not know he was quoting.

Banquo saw them first. Three figures standing where a moment ago there had been nothing — or where perhaps they had always been and the eye had simply declined to register them. They were women, or wore women's shapes, but the shapes fit badly: skin like bark left in rain, lips that moved before sound emerged, fingers too many-jointed. Banquo pulled up short.

"What are these," he said, "so withered and so wild in their attire, that look not like the inhabitants of the earth and yet are on it?"

Macbeth said nothing. He was looking at them, and something in his chest had tightened — not fear, not yet, but the premonitory constriction of a man who suspects he is about to hear something he has already thought.

The first spoke: "All hail, Macbeth! Hail to thee, Thane of Glamis."

His title. His father's title before him, his by inheritance, the fact of his life as it already stood. He knew this. He had always known this. But hearing it from that mouth made it sound less like a fact and more like the first step of an argument.

The second: "All hail, Macbeth! Hail to thee, Thane of Cawdor."

Cawdor. Not his title. Cawdor was another man's — a traitor's, yes, but a living traitor's, or so Macbeth believed. He had not yet received Ross's message. He did not know that Duncan had already stripped Cawdor's honours and bestowed them. The word landed like a stone thrown into water he had thought was solid ground.

The third: "All hail, Macbeth, that shalt be king hereafter!"

King.

The word went through him. Not into his ears and through his understanding, but through him, through the animal body that had unseamed Macdonwald twelve hours ago and had not flinched. He felt it in his hands. He felt it in the place behind his sternum where decisions lived before the mind could name them. King. The thing he had never said aloud, the thought he had not permitted himself to think in words — and here it was, spoken by a creature that should not exist, on a heath that smelled of rain and peat and something older than either.

He stood still. Banquo was watching him.

"Good sir, why do you start and seem to fear things that do sound so fair?" Banquo asked, and there was genuine puzzlement in it — Banquo, who had fought beside him all day and never seen him flinch, watching him flinch now at a greeting.

Macbeth could not answer. The word *king* had opened something in him that he could not, in this moment, close, and the effort of not letting it show on his face consumed everything he had.

Banquo, braver in this than Macbeth or simply less implicated, turned to the figures and demanded his own fortune. "If you can look into the seeds of time and say which grain will grow and which will not, speak then to me, who neither beg nor fear your favours nor your hate."

They answered in their tripled way: lesser than Macbeth, and greater. Not so happy, yet much happier. And then the thing that would matter most, though neither man could know it yet: *Thou shalt get kings, though thou be none.*

Banquo's children. Banquo's line. Kings — not Banquo himself, but his descendants, stretching out to the crack of doom. The crown would pass through Banquo's blood.

Which meant, if Macbeth heard it correctly — and he heard it with the terrible precision of a man already calculating — that whatever kingship the prophecy promised him, it promised him alone. Not his line. Not his house. A single generation's grasp, and then the throne would belong to the sons of the man standing next to him.

"Stay, you imperfect speakers, tell me more," Macbeth said, and his voice had changed — it was the voice of a general now, commanding, urgent. He had questions. Cawdor lived; by what right did they name him Cawdor? And king — *king* — by what means, upon what knowledge, from what authority? "Speak, I charge you."

They vanished. Not departed — vanished, as breath vanishes from a cold pane. The heath was empty. The air thinned. Macbeth and Banquo stood alone with their horses and the enormity of what had been said to them.

"The earth hath bubbles, as the water has," Banquo said slowly, "and these are of them."

Macbeth was still staring at the place where they had stood. "Your children shall be kings," he said.

"You shall be king," Banquo answered.

They looked at each other. The sentence hung between them — *you shall be king* — and in the space of that look, something shifted in the geometry of their friendship. Not broken. Not yet. But the prophecy had made them, for the first time in their lives, not only companions but competitors, recipients of futures that could not both be fulfilled without the other's meaning something different than it seemed.

Macbeth would have spoken — would have said what, he did not know; the thoughts were multiplying too fast, crowding behind his teeth — but hoofbeats interrupted. Ross, riding hard from the south, still carrying the mud of his earlier errand and now carrying another message.

"The king hath happily received, Macbeth, the news of thy success," Ross said, reining up. And then, as though reading from a document too simple to require emphasis: "He bade me, from him, call thee Thane of Cawdor."

Thane of Cawdor.

The second prophecy. True. Already true, already accomplished, before Macbeth had done anything to bring it about — the thing the creatures on the heath had named was the thing the king had already done. The sequence was exact: Glamis, which he was. Cawdor, which he now learned he had become. And the third — the third —

"The Thane of Cawdor lives," Macbeth managed. "Why do you dress me in borrowed robes?"

Ross explained. The treason, the sentence, the title transferred. The facts were plain, bureaucratic, the machinery of a grateful king rewarding loyalty. But Macbeth was no longer hearing administration. He was hearing confirmation. He was hearing the universe testify that the impossible was not impossible, that three ragged figures on a heath had known what the king's own messenger had not yet delivered, and that if two-thirds of an impossible thing had come true within the hour, then the final third —

Banquo, beside him, murmured something cautionary. "And oftentimes, to win us to our harm, the instruments of darkness tell us truths, win us with honest trifles, to betray us in deepest consequence."

Macbeth heard him. He heard every word, and the words were wise, and the wisdom meant nothing against the roaring in his blood. Two truths had been told, as happy prologues to the swelling act of the imperial theme. The imperial theme. He was already calling it that, inside himself, already granting it the language of inevitability.

And yet — and this was the thing that frightened him more than the vanishing women, more than the impossible knowledge — the thought that accompanied the prophecy's confirmation was not joy. It was not even ambition, cleanly felt. It was something darker, a suggestion that had arrived fully formed in the space between *Cawdor* and *king*, and the suggestion was murder.

He had not decided anything. He had not been asked to decide anything. Duncan lived, Duncan reigned, Duncan had just given him a new title out of pure generosity. And yet the image was there — the image of what it would take to make the third prophecy true by his own hand — and its presence horrified him. His hair stood on end. His heart knocked against his ribs in a rhythm that had nothing to do with exertion. The murder was a thought he had not chosen to think, and he could not stop thinking it.

"If chance will have me king," he said quietly, almost to himself, "why, chance may crown me without my stir."

It was the first negotiation. Not a refusal — a deferral. Let the prophecy fulfil itself, as the Cawdor half had fulfilled itself. Let it come without his hand in it. He could wait. He could be patient. He could let fate do its work.

He almost believed it.

They rode on toward Forres, toward the king who had just honoured him, and Macbeth said little for the rest of the journey. Banquo watched him — not suspiciously, not yet, but with the careful attention of a man who had heard the same prophecy and drawn a different lesson from it. Banquo had been promised a dynasty and felt nothing he needed to act on. Macbeth had been promised a crown and was already at war with himself over what the promise required.

The heath was behind them. The heath would never be behind them.

Forres. The king's hall. Torches and banners and the smell of roasted meat, and Duncan standing among his lords with the unguarded warmth of a man who believed the world repaid goodness with goodness.

Macbeth had composed the letter before he arrived. Somewhere between the heath and the castle gates, while Banquo rode beside him in their newly complicated silence, his mind had split in two — one half managing the approach, the salutations, the business of being Macbeth-the-loyal-thane arriving to greet his sovereign, and the other half writing. Choosing words. Arranging the impossible into sentences his wife could hold. *They met me in the day of success, and I have learned by the perfectest report they have more in them than mortal knowledge.* He would send it tonight. She would need to know. She, of all people in the breathing world, was the one whose mind could meet his own without flinching, and this — this required a mind that would not flinch.

But first: the king.

Duncan embraced him. That was the thing Macbeth would remember later, when remembering became a species of torment — that Duncan embraced him, physically, with both arms, as a father might embrace a son returned from danger. The king's hands on his shoulders, the king's eyes bright with an emotion that Macbeth recognised, with a sickening lurch, as love.

"O worthiest cousin!" Duncan said, and meant it — that was the worst part, that Duncan always meant it, that there was no distance between the king's words and the king's feeling, no statecraft in his affection. "The sin of my ingratitude even now was heavy on me. Thou art so far before that swiftest wing of recompense is slow to overtake thee. Would thou hadst less deserved, that the proportion both of thanks and payment might have been mine!"

Macbeth answered as he was expected to answer. The service and the loyalty he owed were their own reward; he had done only what duty demanded; the honour was the king's to give and the subject's to receive. The words came easily. They were true — or had been true this morning, when the world was still the world he understood, when kingship was Duncan's and loyalty was Macbeth's and the distance between the two was as permanent as geography. The words were still true now, in his mouth, in this room, in the torchlight. They were simply no longer the only true thing.

Duncan turned to Banquo and praised him too — embraced him too, with that same reckless generosity, that inability to hold back. Banquo received it gracefully, and Macbeth watched the exchange and thought: *He promised your children the crown. You are standing in front of the man whose throne your sons will inherit, and your face shows nothing.* The thought was unworthy, and he put it aside, and it stayed where he put it for almost three seconds before returning.

Then Duncan did the thing that changed the architecture of everything.

"Sons, kinsmen, thanes," the king announced, and the hall quieted, "know that we do establish our estate upon our eldest, Malcolm" — and here he reached for the young man standing at his right — "whom we name hereafter the Prince of Cumberland."

Prince of Cumberland. Heir designate. The title that named the succession and sealed it in the sight of every lord in Scotland. Malcolm — Duncan's son, Duncan's blood — standing there accepting the weight of it with a boy's solemnity, and the court murmuring approval, and Macbeth's face arranged in the appropriate expression of a loyal thane witnessing the natural order affirm itself.

Except the natural order had, on a heath three hours ago, told him something different.

He applauded. He smiled. He felt the thing behind his sternum — the thing the third prophecy had woken — contract into something smaller and harder and more specific. Prince of Cumberland. That was a step on which he must fall down, or else o'erleap, for in his way it lay. The thought arrived in that language — theatrical, structured, as though some part of his mind were already composing the drama of what he might become.

*Stars, hide your fires,* he thought. *Let not light see my black and deep desires.*

Duncan was speaking again. Duncan was always speaking, always giving — giving praise, giving titles, giving his plans for the evening. He would ride to Inverness. He would honour Macbeth's castle with his presence. He would sleep under Macbeth's roof, guest to his host, king to his thane, trusting man to trusted man. The announcement carried no suspicion because Duncan was incapable of it; the king lived in a world where loyalty, once demonstrated on the battlefield, was permanent. Macbeth had unseamed his enemies. Macbeth had bled for Scotland. What more proof could a man require?

"I'll be myself the harbinger and make joyful the hearing of my wife with your approach," Macbeth said, and the sentence performed two functions — courtier's promise to his king, and something else, something only he knew, a notification to himself that the letter must go ahead of him, that she must read it before he arrived, that when he walked through his own door he needed to walk into a room where someone had already done the thinking he could not do alone.

He had written the letter in his head on the ride. He would write it on paper within the hour, seal it, send it by the fastest rider in his household. He already knew the words. He had told her about the witches, about the prophecy, about Cawdor's title arriving like a signature on a contract he had not signed. He had told her what the third promise was. He had told her because she was the person he told things to — because the marriage was the place where Macbeth thought aloud, where the interior of him had a listener it trusted absolutely. *This have I thought good to deliver thee, my dearest partner of greatness, that thou mightst not lose the dues of rejoicing by being ignorant of what greatness is promised thee.*

Partner of greatness. Not *wife*. Not *love*. Partner. The word he chose without choosing it, because it was the word that was true. They were partners. They had been partners since the beginning — in ambition, in understanding, in the shared private knowledge of what they were capable of when they moved together. He did not yet know what he was asking her to partner in. He knew only that he could not hold the prophecy alone.

Duncan clasped his hand again as they parted. The king's grip was warm, dry, unhesitating. Duncan looked at him with the face of a man who saw exactly what was in front of him — a war hero, a newly honoured thane, a loyal kinsman — and could not imagine what was behind it.

"He is full so valiant," Duncan told the court, watching Macbeth go, "and in his commendations I am fed; it is a banquet to me."

Macbeth walked out into the courtyard, into the evening air that smelled of horses and frost and the last of the autumn light. He had a new title. He had his king's love. He had a letter to write to his wife. He had a castle to prepare for a royal visit. He had, by every measure that Scotland used to weigh a man, everything a man could want.

He stood in the courtyard and felt the distance between what he had and what had been promised, and the distance was exactly the size of a crown, and the crown was exactly the size of the darkness gathering behind his ribs.

He called for a rider. The letter would go tonight.

Inverness. The castle. Late afternoon light through narrow windows, and Lady Macbeth alone in the chamber she kept for her own use — the room where she read, where she managed the household accounts, where the work of being the Thane of Glamis's wife happened in private before it appeared in public as effortlessness.

The rider had come an hour before sunset. She had recognised the seal before she recognised the handwriting, and then she recognised both, and then she was alone with the letter and the door closed and the light still good enough to read by.

*They met me in the day of success, and I have learned by the perfectest report they have more in them than mortal knowledge.*

She read the sentence twice. Then she read the entire letter, beginning to end, without stopping, the way one swallows medicine — all at once, because hesitation would make it worse. Then she read it again, slowly, and this time every word had weight, and the weight was different from what the words appeared to carry.

He had met three women on a heath. They had called him Glamis, which he was. They had called him Cawdor, which — *and this she must attend to most carefully* — he now was, by the king's own decree, the previous Cawdor having proved traitor and been stripped of everything a traitor deserves to lose. They had called him king hereafter.

King hereafter.

She set the letter down on the table. She picked it up again. She held it near the window where the light was strongest and read the passage a third time, not because the words had changed but because the room had — the air in it, the quality of the silence, the way the walls seemed simultaneously closer and farther away, as though the chamber had recognised before she did that it was no longer the room of a thane's wife but the room of something else, something not yet named, something that required naming.

*This have I thought good to deliver thee, my dearest partner of greatness, that thou mightst not lose the dues of rejoicing by being ignorant of what greatness is promised thee.*

Partner of greatness. She knew why he had written partner. She knew why he had written to her at all, before arriving, before sleeping, before doing anything else with the impossible news — because he needed her to have thought it through before he walked through the door. Because he could not do what the letter implied alone, and he knew he could not, and the knowing was not weakness but the particular intelligence of a man who understood his own construction. He was brave beyond question; he had unseamed men from nave to chops and ridden home with their blood still drying on his gauntlets. Courage was not his deficit. But there was a difference between courage on a battlefield, where the thing to be done was clear and the doing of it was sanctioned by every authority a man served under, and courage in the dark, where the thing to be done had no sanction at all, where the only authority was the self and the self was uncertain.

She knew her husband. She knew the precise flavour of his ambition — how it ran hot and then cooled, how it surged toward the thing it wanted and then recoiled from the means of getting it. He was not a coward. He was something more complicated than a coward, and more dangerous, and more in need of management. He would want the crown the way a man dying of thirst wants water — with his whole body, with every nerve and sinew, with a wanting so total it left no room for strategy. And then he would think about what wanting required. And then he would hesitate. And then the hesitation would curdle into something that looked, from the outside, like conscience but was, from the inside, something closer to a failure of will — not *I must not* but *I cannot make myself*.

She could make him. She had always been able to make him. Not through tricks, not through the crude machinery of seduction or manipulation that lesser marriages ran on, but through the simple fact that when she spoke to the center of him, the center answered. They were the same creature, she and Macbeth. They wanted the same things. They saw the same world. The difference was that she could look at the path between wanting and having without blinking, and he could not — not yet — not without her standing beside him, holding his gaze steady, refusing to let him look away.

*Glamis thou art, and Cawdor, and shalt be what thou art promised.*

She said it aloud. The room heard it. She heard it. The words sounded like a decision, and she examined them for doubt and found none — not because doubt was impossible but because she had decided, in the space between the second and third reading of the letter, that doubt was a luxury she would not permit. Not in herself. Not tonight.

*Yet do I fear thy nature. It is too full o' the milk of human kindness to catch the nearest way.*

The nearest way. She would not say the word yet, not even in the privacy of her own mind, not until she had to — but the shape of it was there, the outline, the terrible simplicity of it. There was a king. There was a prophecy. There was a space between the two that could be closed by a single act, and the act was the act she would not name, and her husband would ride through the gates within hours carrying the same knowledge and the same unnamed act inside him like a stone in his chest.

He would need her to have already decided. He would need to walk into a room where the question had been answered, where the only remaining business was the doing. Because if she left the question open — if she met him with her own uncertainty, her own hesitation, her own version of the milk of human kindness — then neither of them would do anything, and the prophecy would remain a prophecy, and they would grow old in this castle with the knowledge that greatness had been promised and they had been too gentle to seize it.

She would not be gentle. Not tonight.

*Come, you spirits that tend on mortal thoughts,* she whispered to the empty room, to the gathering dark outside the window, to whatever listened when a woman spoke to the unspeakable. *Unsex me here, and fill me from the crown to the toe top-full of direst cruelty. Make thick my blood. Stop up the access and passage to remorse, that no compunctious visitings of nature shake my fell purpose, nor keep peace between the effect and it.*

The words came out of her like something that had been waiting. She did not know if anything heard them. She did not care. The prayer was not to the spirits; the prayer was to herself — a command issued from the part of her that could do this to the part that might resist. She was building something inside her own chest, a structure of iron and intention, and she was building it before he arrived because he would lean on it, and it had to hold.

A servant knocked. A messenger. She composed herself — Lady Macbeth, chatelaine, gracious, expecting nothing — and admitted him.

The king was coming. Tonight. Here. To Inverness. To sleep under her roof, to eat at her table, to rest his trusting body in a bed she would prepare.

She dismissed the messenger. She stood very still for a moment, feeling the coincidence — if it was coincidence, if anything was coincidence now, in a world where witches spoke truth and prophecies came half-true within hours — the perfect, annihilating coincidence of Duncan delivering himself to the place where the act could be done. The raven himself was hoarse that croaked the fatal entrance of Duncan under her battlements.

*Come, thick night, and pall thee in the dunnest smoke of hell, that my keen knife see not the wound it makes, nor heaven peep through the blanket of the dark to cry "Hold, hold!"*

She was ready. She was as ready as a human being could be for a thing no human being should be ready for. The letter was still on the table, his handwriting in the fading light, and she folded it and placed it inside her bodice, against her skin, where it would stay warm with her warmth, where it was partnership made paper, conspiracy made ink.

Then she heard the horses.

He came through the door the way she knew he would — large, wind-bitten, smelling of road dust and horse sweat, his eyes finding hers immediately, the way they always did, before he looked at anything else in a room. She saw the question in him before he spoke. She saw the want and the fear and the space between them, and she stepped into that space the way she had always stepped into it, with certainty, with the force of a woman who had already finished the argument her husband was still having with himself.

"Great Glamis, worthy Cawdor — greater than both, by the all-hail hereafter."

His face changed. Not visibly — not in a way a servant or a courtier would have noticed — but she was not a servant or a courtier, and she saw it: the flinch, the flare, the complicated mixture of relief and dread that came from being known completely. She had read the letter. She knew. She had already done the thinking. He could see it in her face, in the way she stood, in the quality of her stillness.

"My dearest love," he said. "Duncan comes here tonight."

"And when goes hence?"

"Tomorrow, as he purposes."

"O, never shall sun that morrow see!"

She watched the words land. She watched him receive them — not as surprise, because he had known what she would say, had known it from the moment he wrote the letter, had sent the letter precisely so that she would say exactly this — but as reality, as the thing spoken aloud in a room between two people becoming a thing that existed in the world. His face was the face of a man standing at the edge of a cliff who has been told, by the person he trusts most, to jump.

"Your face, my thane, is as a book where men may read strange matters," she said, moving closer, taking his hands — hands that had killed on battlefields, hands that had been clasped by a king who loved him. "To beguile the time, look like the time. Bear welcome in your eye, your hand, your tongue. Look like the innocent flower, but be the serpent under it."

He was quiet. She could feel the resistance in him — not refusal, not yet, but the thick, sluggish reluctance of a man whose body knew what his mind had not yet admitted. He wanted to be persuaded. He wanted someone else to carry the weight of the decision so that the decision felt less like his. This, too, she understood. This, too, she could provide.

"He that's coming must be provided for," she said, and the sentence operated on two levels — hostess preparing for a royal guest, wife instructing a husband in murder — and she held both levels in her voice without letting either one crack. "And you shall put this night's great business into my dispatch."

"We will speak further," he said.

It was not a no. It was the sound a man makes when the last wall between intention and action is still standing but the foundation has already given way. *We will speak further.* As if speaking would change anything. As if the letter had not already been written, the prophecy already half-fulfilled, the king already on the road to the castle where the thing would happen. She looked at him, and she saw that he saw it too — that the distance between speaking further and doing the thing they would not yet name was not a distance at all but a formality, a last breath before the dive.

"Only look up clear," she told him. "Leave all the rest to me."

He looked at her. She held his gaze. The letter was warm against her skin. The castle was being prepared for a king's arrival, and she would oversee every detail — the rooms, the wine, the torches, the bedding — with the meticulousness of a woman who understood that this was the last evening of the world as it was, and that the morning would belong to the world as she and her husband were about to make it.

He left to wash the road off himself, and she stood alone in the chamber for a moment longer, her hand pressed flat against the letter beneath her dress, and she felt the future sitting in the room with her like a fourth presence — not the spirits she had invoked, not the witches her husband had described, but the thing itself, the act, enormous and still and waiting for the dark.

The castle received its king the way a mouth receives a morsel.

Duncan rode through the gates of Inverness in the last hour of useful light, when the sky was the colour of a bruise fading to yellow at its western edge and the swallows were cutting their final arcs above the battlements. He rode with Malcolm at his side and Banquo half a length behind, and the household guard flanking them in decent order, and the torches already lit along the courtyard walls because Lady Macbeth had seen to it — had seen to everything, the torches and the tables and the fresh rushes on the floors and the particular quality of welcome that makes a guest feel not merely received but anticipated, desired, essential.

"This castle hath a pleasant seat," Duncan said, reining up, lifting his face to the air. He meant it. He was a man who noticed pleasantness, who attended to the quality of the wind and the behaviour of birds and the way a house presented itself to a traveller at the end of a long ride. It was one of the things that made him a good king and a poor judge of danger — the same attention to beauty that let him appreciate the martlet nesting in the castle's frieze made him incapable of looking at a well-lit courtyard and seeing anything other than hospitality. "The air nimbly and sweetly recommends itself unto our gentle senses."

Banquo agreed. He pointed out the nesting birds, the jutting corners where the martlets had built and bred, the way the creatures chose their homes by instinct, by some animal recognition of safety. The heaven's breath smelt wooingly here, he said, and he was right — the air was good, the evening was mild, the castle was handsome against the darkening sky — and neither he nor Duncan nor any man in the arriving party looked at the walls of Inverness and saw what they were riding into. They saw stone and torchlight and the promise of a bed after a long day's travel. The stone saw them differently.

Lady Macbeth met them in the courtyard. She had changed — a better dress, her hair rebound, the transformation from the woman who had whispered to spirits in a darkening room into the woman who curtseyed before a king with warmth so practised it was indistinguishable from sincerity. She was magnificent. She was every inch the grateful subject, the honoured hostess, the wife whose husband's new title had overwhelmed her with a joy she could barely contain.

"All our service, in every point twice done and then done double, were poor and single business to contend against those honours deep and broad wherewith your Majesty loads our house."

Duncan took her hands. He spoke of the trouble he brought with him — the inconvenience of a royal visit, the disruption to a household — and his words were an apology wrapped in a compliment, because Duncan's words were always that: generous, self-deprecating, the language of a man who believed that power was a thing best exercised gently. He loved Macbeth, he said. He would continue to honour him. He asked to be brought to his host, and Lady Macbeth led him inside with her hand light on his arm, and the castle closed around him the way a story closes around its subject — completely, irreversibly, with the ending already written in the architecture.

Macbeth was not in the courtyard. Macbeth was inside, in a corridor between the great hall and the kitchens, in a place where the noise of preparation — servants carrying platters, the clatter of pewter, the sound of a household performing itself at full capacity — provided a kind of privacy. He had washed. He had changed. He had done everything a host does when a king arrives, and then he had stopped moving and stood very still in the corridor, and the stillness was the stillness of a man whose body had run out of tasks to perform and whose mind had therefore been left alone with itself.

He should have been in the courtyard. He knew this. His wife knew this. The household knew this. His absence was a small wrong note in an otherwise flawless performance, and it would be noticed only by the person who knew what the absence meant, and that person was currently leading the king to his chambers with a smile that could have charmed birds from trees.

The supper was laid. The wine was poured. The great hall was dressed for a king's meal, and the king was settling into his rooms, and somewhere in the castle's middle distance Macbeth stood in a corridor and conducted the argument he had been conducting since the heath, since the letter, since his wife's face when she told him the sun would never see that morrow.

The argument went like this:

If the act could end with itself — if the assassination could trammel up the consequence, could be the be-all and the end-all, here, just here, upon this bank and shoal of time — then it would be worth the risk of whatever came after this life. But it would not end with itself. These things never did. The lesson was taught in blood, always — you struck the blow, and the blow taught others how to strike, and the blade you wielded became the blade wielded against you. That was justice. Not heaven's justice, not yet, though heaven's justice waited too — but the plain, earthly, mechanical justice of precedent. He would teach the art of murder to his own court, and the court would learn, and the lesson would come back to him as it had come to Cawdor, as it had come to every man who thought violence could be performed once and then put away like a tool.

And Duncan. Duncan, who was here in double trust — as kinsman and as king, as the man who had just promoted him and as the guest now sleeping under his roof. Duncan, who had borne his faculties so meek, who had been so clear in his great office that his virtues would plead like angels, trumpet-tongued, against the deep damnation of his taking-off. Pity, like a naked newborn babe striding the blast, would ride upon the winds and blow the horrid deed in every eye, and tears would drown the wind, and there would be nothing left of Macbeth's name but the thing he had done in the dark to a sleeping man who trusted him.

He had no spur to prick the sides of his intent. Only ambition, which o'erleapt itself and fell on the other side.

He found her in the passage outside the great hall. The supper was underway; Duncan was inside, eating and drinking and praising his hosts. The noise of the feast carried through the doors — laughter, the low rumble of men talking, the sound of a court at ease. She had left the table — left the king — to find him, because she had noticed his absence the way she noticed everything, and her face when she saw him standing in the corridor was the face of a woman who already knew what he was going to say.

"We will proceed no further in this business," he said. "He hath honoured me of late, and I have bought golden opinions from all sorts of people, which would be enjoyed in their newest gloss, not cast aside so soon."

He watched her hear it. He watched her receive the words the way a wall receives a blow — absorbing the force, holding, and then returning something harder than what had been given. Her chin lifted. Her eyes changed. The warmth she had performed for Duncan vanished as though it had never existed, and what remained was the other thing, the thing beneath the hostess, the thing that had spoken to spirits in an empty room and meant every word.

"Was the hope drunk wherein you dressed yourself?" she said. "Hath it slept since? And wakes it now to look so green and pale at what it did so freely? From this time such I account thy love."

The words were precise, surgical. She knew where to cut. She was not arguing with his reasoning — his reasoning was sound, she did not bother to contest it — she was arguing with his self, with the structure of the man who stood before her, and the argument was: you are less than what you promised. You are less than what you wrote in that letter. You are less than what I prepared myself to be on your behalf.

"Art thou afeard to be the same in thine own act and valour as thou art in desire?"

He said what a man says when the accusation is unfair but accurate: "I dare do all that may become a man. Who dares do more is none."

And she said the thing that unmade him.

She said she had nursed a child. She said she knew the tenderness of it — the infant at her breast, the small mouth, the terrible vulnerability of the bond — and she said that she would, while it was smiling in her face, have plucked her nipple from its boneless gums and dashed its brains out, had she so sworn as he had sworn to this. She said it quietly, and she said it looking directly into his eyes, and the quietness was worse than shouting, because shouting could be dismissed as passion, and this was not passion. This was architecture. This was a woman showing her husband the interior of her own commitment and asking him to match it.

The corridor was empty. The feast continued on the other side of the doors. Duncan was inside, alive, full of wine and trust, surrounded by men who served him, sleeping tonight in a bed prepared by the woman who was, at this moment, standing in a passageway with her husband and constructing the machinery of his murder.

"If we should fail?" Macbeth said.

The question was not a question. It was the last piece of resistance leaving his body — the final objection offered not because he believed it but because he needed it answered so that the answering would do the work his own will could not. She heard it for what it was. She smiled — not warmly, not cruelly, but with the focused intensity of a woman completing a negotiation whose outcome had never truly been in doubt.

"We fail? But screw your courage to the sticking place and we'll not fail."

Then she told him the plan. The chamberlains who slept outside Duncan's door — she would ply them with wine until memory and reason became a limbeck only. When they lay in swinish sleep, what could not she and Macbeth then perform upon the unguarded Duncan? And when it was done, the blood would go upon the sleeping grooms, and the crime would wear their faces, not the faces of the host and hostess. The plan was simple. The plan was elegant. The plan was a small, clean machine designed by a woman who had spent the hours since the letter arrived thinking about nothing else.

He looked at her. The corridor was dim. Through the doors, Duncan's laughter — the king was telling a story, or being told one, and the court was performing its pleasure — rose and fell like breath. Macbeth looked at his wife and saw the thing she had built for him: the structure, the path, the way through. She had done the thinking. She had done the deciding. She had done everything except the act itself, and the act was his, had always been his, would always be his — but she had cleared the ground around it so thoroughly that the act now stood alone in his mind, stripped of every complication, every objection, every competing claim, and what remained was simple. A sleeping man. A knife. A crown.

"Bring forth men-children only," he said, and his voice had changed — the reluctance burned away, the hesitation cauterised, and in its place something that felt like resolve but tasted like surrender. "For thy undaunted mettle should compose nothing but males."

She waited. She had learned when to press and when to let him arrive on his own. The distance between where he had been — *we will proceed no further* — and where he was going was not a distance she could carry him across. He had to walk it himself. She had shown him the bridge, and now she stood at the far side and waited for his step.

"I am settled," he said.

The words fell into the corridor like stones into water — heavy, final, sending ripples outward through time in every direction, forward into the night that was coming and the morning that would follow and the years of blood and madness that neither of them could see yet but that were already gathering, already forming, already inevitable from the moment the sentence was spoken.

"I am settled, and bend up each corporal agent to this terrible feat."

He straightened. Something in his posture changed — not confidence, exactly, but the particular rigidity of a man who has stopped deliberating and started moving, who has converted the energy of indecision into the energy of action, who has decided that the only thing worse than doing the terrible thing is standing in a corridor forever deciding whether to do it. His body knew what to do with commitment. His body had always known. It was hesitation that unmade him, and hesitation was over.

"Away," he said, "and mock the time with fairest show. False face must hide what the false heart doth know."

They went in to supper. They returned to the feast, to the king, to the table where Duncan sat warm and fed and trusting, and they were perfect — every gesture correct, every word calibrated, every smile offered and received with the precision of two people performing a play within a play. The court saw a loyal thane and his gracious wife entertaining their king. The court saw nothing else. There was nothing else to see — not yet — not until the torches were doused and the household slept and the night did the work the night had been given to do.

Duncan retired to his chambers. He commended his hosts. He sent a diamond to Lady Macbeth — a gift for the hostess, a token of a king's pleasure — and the diamond was carried through corridors that were already darkening, past doors that were already closing, into the hands of a woman who received it with gratitude and set it aside with the indifference of someone who had been promised something far more valuable than a stone.

The castle settled. The fires burned low. The guards were posted — and plied with wine, as promised, as planned. The night thickened around Inverness the way a fist closes around a throat, slowly and then all at once, and inside the castle's walls a king slept the sleep of a man who had never been given reason to doubt the loyalty of the people who sheltered him.

Macbeth did not sleep. Lady Macbeth did not sleep. They waited, together and apart, in the strange suspended time between the decision and the act — the last hours of the world as it was, ticking away in the dark like a heartbeat slowing toward silence.

The corridor was empty, and then the corridor was not empty, because something hung in the air before him — a dagger, handle toward his hand, floating at the precise height a man would hold a weapon if a man were holding a weapon, which no man was.

Macbeth stopped. The castle was silent around him — the silence of a household fully asleep, of guards drugged past dreaming, of a night that had been carefully, methodically emptied of witnesses. His wife had done her work. The chamberlains were insensible, soaked in wine and posset, their breathing the thick wet breathing of men who would not wake until they were woken. Everything was prepared. Everything was ready. And between Macbeth and the chamber where Duncan slept, a dagger hung in the darkness like a question the darkness itself was asking.

He reached for it. His fingers closed on nothing. The blade remained — vivid, present, its handle still inclined toward him, still offering itself with the patience of an object that had all night and knew it. He could see it as clearly as he could see the one he carried, the real one, the one whose weight he felt against his hip, whose steel was actual and cold and would do actual, cold work before this night was over. But the floating blade was not actual. It was not cold. It was something else — something his mind had made because his mind could not contain what was about to happen in a single thought and had therefore split it into a thing seen and a thing carried, a thing desired and a thing done.

"Is this a dagger which I see before me, the handle toward my hand?"

His voice was barely sound. He was speaking to no one — to the corridor, to the darkness, to the part of himself that had produced this apparition and was now watching him respond to it. The dagger hung. He reached again, and again his hand passed through it, and the blade did not waver, did not dissolve, did not have the decency to vanish and leave him alone with the plain mechanics of what he was about to do.

"Come, let me clutch thee. I have thee not, and yet I see thee still."

It began to move. It drifted ahead of him, down the corridor, toward the chamber — marshalling him, leading him, as though the night itself had appointed a guide for this particular journey and the guide was the image of the act he had not yet committed. And now there was blood on it — blood on a blade that did not exist, gouts of it appearing on the steel and on the handle, blood that was not yet shed appearing on a weapon that was not yet drawn, and Macbeth understood that his eyes were being made the fools of his other senses, or else worth all the rest, and it did not matter which, because both conclusions led to the same door at the end of the same corridor.

He closed his eyes. He opened them. The dagger was gone.

The corridor was just a corridor. The stones were just stones. The night was just a night — but it was the kind of night where the earth itself seemed dead, where wicked dreams abused the curtained sleep, where witchcraft celebrated pale Hecate's offerings, and where murder moved like a ghost toward its object. He heard nothing. The castle gave him nothing — no footstep, no creak of timber, no startled voice from behind a door. The world had been made silent for him, and the silence was permission, and the permission was the last thing he needed and the last thing he wanted.

He drew the real dagger. It had weight. It had an edge. It was not a vision; it would not vanish when he reached for it. He held it and walked toward the chamber door, and his feet made no sound on the stone — not because he was careful, though he was careful, but because the night itself seemed to muffle him, to absorb him into its fabric, to make him part of its own dark machinery.

"Hear it not, Duncan," he whispered, "for it is a knell that summons thee to heaven or to hell."

He opened the door.

The chamber was warm. A fire had been laid — his wife's doing, or the servants', part of the meticulous hospitality that had welcomed a king into a house designed to destroy him — and the coals still breathed a dim orange light that barely reached the bed but reached it enough. Duncan slept on his back. His face was visible. His breathing was the slow, measured breathing of a man who had eaten well and drunk well and been given no reason, not one reason in the whole long generous course of his life, to fear the people who sheltered him.

He looked like Macbeth's father. Not in feature — in repose. In the particular defencelessness of an older man sleeping, the lines of authority softened, the mouth slightly open, the hands resting on the coverlet with the fingers loosely curled. He looked like what he was: a man who trusted the world because the world had largely been trustworthy, and who slept deeply in other men's castles because he believed that kinship and hospitality and the plain bonds of loyalty between a king and his thane were structures that held.

Macbeth stood over him with the dagger in his hand.

The moment lasted. It was not hesitation — hesitation had been burned out of him in the corridor with his wife, cauterised and sealed, and what was left was not doubt but something worse than doubt: clarity. He saw exactly what he was doing. He saw the sleeping face and the blade and the firelight and the room and the night and the chain of events that had brought him here — the heath, the prophecy, the letter, the look on his wife's face, the settled resolve in the passage outside the feast — and he saw it all with a terrible precision that did not stop his hand but accompanied it, witnessed it, would remember it forever.

He killed Duncan in his sleep.

The act was fast. The act was the work of a man who had killed many men on battlefields and whose body knew the mechanics of killing even when his mind could not contain them. Two strokes. The king made a sound — not a word, not a cry, something smaller and more terrible than either — and then the sound stopped, and the breathing stopped, and the warm room was suddenly the warmest and the quietest place in Scotland, and Macbeth stood in it with blood on his hands and a dead king in the bed and the rest of his life beginning.

He looked at his hands.

The blood was black in the dim light. It covered his fingers, his palms, the undersides of his wrists where the act had brought him close enough to be anointed by it. He could feel it cooling — the particular sensation of another man's blood losing its warmth against living skin — and the sensation was not like anything he had felt on a battlefield, because on a battlefield blood was ambient, was everywhere, was the medium through which a man moved and fought and survived, and here it was specific, was Duncan's, was a king's blood on a kinsman's hands in a room where the fire was dying and the silence was so complete that he could hear his own pulse in his ears.

He heard a voice. Not a voice in the room — a voice in the fabric of the night, in the walls, in the space between waking and whatever lay beyond waking. It said: *Sleep no more. Macbeth does murder sleep.* He could not tell if the voice was inside his skull or outside it. He could not tell if it was the voice of the house or the voice of the dead man or the voice of his own mind cracking under the weight of what it had just done. It said: *Glamis hath murdered sleep, and therefore Cawdor shall sleep no more. Macbeth shall sleep no more.*

He carried the daggers out of the room. He carried them because he had forgotten to leave them, because the plan — his wife's clean, elegant plan — required the daggers to be left with the grooms, required the blood to be on the grooms' hands, required the scene to be composed like a painting whose meaning pointed away from the painter, and he had failed to compose it, had walked out of the room with the weapons still in his grip because his hands had locked around them and would not let go.

She was waiting. She was in the corridor outside their chambers, and her face when she saw him was the face of a woman who had been counting seconds, who had heard the owl shriek and the crickets cry and had catalogued every sound the night produced while her husband did the thing she had prepared him to do. She looked at him. She looked at his hands. She looked at the daggers.

"My husband —"

"I have done the deed," he said. "Didst thou not hear a noise?"

His voice was strange to him. It came from a distance, from somewhere outside the man who stood in the corridor with blood drying on his skin. She heard the strangeness too — heard it and set it aside, because there were practical matters to attend to, because the plan required steps and the steps required sequence and the sequence required someone whose mind was still functional, and hers was. Hers still was.

"I heard the owl scream and the crickets cry. Did not you speak?"

"When?"

"Now."

"As I descended?"

They were two people standing in a dark corridor performing a conversation that neither of them would ever be able to forget, and the conversation had the terrible intimacy of a shared crime — the closeness of two minds that had crossed a threshold together and now stood on the other side looking back at the world they had left, unable to return to it, unable to see each other without also seeing what they had done.

He told her about the voice. Sleep no more. Macbeth shall sleep no more. He told her about the grooms — how two of them had woken briefly, had stirred and mumbled prayers in their drugged half-sleep, had said "Amen" and "God bless us" before sinking back into unconsciousness, and how he had stood over them with Duncan's blood still wet on his hands and tried to say Amen and could not. The word would not come. His throat had refused it — had closed against the syllable the way a body closes against poison, because the word was holy and his mouth was no longer a place where holy words could live.

"Consider it not so deeply," she said, and the words were steady, but the steadiness cost her something — he could hear the effort in it, the controlled exhalation behind the control. She was managing him. She was managing the aftermath the way she had managed the preparation: with precision, with clarity, with the focused attention of a woman whose plan was still in motion and who could not afford to let its principal actor collapse in a corridor.

Then she saw the daggers.

"Why did you bring these from the place? They must lie there. Go, carry them back and smear the sleepy grooms with blood."

"I'll go no more," he said. "I am afraid to think what I have done. Look on't again I dare not."

She looked at him. The look was long, and it contained everything the marriage had been and everything it was becoming — the shared ambition that had unified them, the conspiracy that had bound them, and now the fracture that was already beginning, the hairline crack between the woman who could still function and the man who could not, between the partner who had done the act and the partner who would have to finish it. She took the daggers from his hands.

"Infirm of purpose," she said. "Give me the daggers."

She went. She went back down the corridor toward the chamber where Duncan lay, and she carried the daggers with the competence of a woman performing a task that had been made necessary by someone else's failure, and she was gone for what felt like hours but was minutes, and in those minutes Macbeth stood alone in the corridor and looked at his hands and could not stop looking at his hands.

The blood was on them. It would not come off. He knew it would come off — water and time and the plain mechanics of washing would remove the substance from his skin — but the knowledge was abstract, distant, belonging to a version of himself that still believed in the relationship between cause and effect, between action and remedy, between a stain and its removal. The man standing in the corridor did not believe in remedies. The man standing in the corridor looked at his hands and saw them dyeing the entire ocean red — all the seas incarnadine, the green turned to red, the multitudinous seas made one uniform colour by the blood of a single sleeping king.

"Will all great Neptune's ocean wash this blood clean from my hand? No. This my hand will rather the multitudinous seas incarnadine, making the green one red."

She returned. Her hands were bloody now — she had done what he could not, had placed the daggers with the grooms, had painted them with Duncan's blood, had composed the scene the way it needed to be composed — and her hands were the same colour as his, and for a moment they stood together in the corridor with matching stains, the last moment of the conspiracy's equality, the last instant in which they were doing the same thing in the same way for the same reason.

"My hands are of your colour," she said, "but I shame to wear a heart so white."

A knocking. Somewhere in the castle — at the gate, the south gate, persistent, the sound of someone demanding entry — a knocking that cut through the silence of the night like a blade through cloth. The sound was ordinary. The sound was the most ordinary thing in the world — a fist against wood, a visitor arriving, the plain domestic fact of someone wanting in — and it was the most terrible sound Macbeth had ever heard, because it belonged to the world that was still going on outside the walls of Inverness, the world that did not yet know what had happened inside them, the world that would find out in the morning and would never look at him again without seeing what he had done.

"Wake Duncan with thy knocking," he whispered. "I would thou couldst."

They went to wash. They went to their chambers and they washed their hands and they changed their nightclothes and they performed the small desperate rituals of concealment — water, clean linen, the careful rearrangement of surfaces — and the knocking continued, continued, would not stop, and the night that had been so silent was now full of the sound of someone wanting in, and the someone wanted in, and the gate would have to be opened, and the morning would have to come, and the morning came.

The morning came, and the morning was Macduff.

He had arrived at first light — summoned, expected, a lord of Scotland arriving to attend his king — and the porter had opened the gate with the bleary resentment of a man roused from drunken sleep, and Macduff had walked into the courtyard of Inverness in the grey early light and seen nothing wrong. The castle was quiet. The morning was cold. Macbeth met him in the courtyard — washed, dressed, the host performing the host's duties — and walked him toward the king's chambers, because the king had asked to be called early, and it was time.

Macduff went in. Macbeth waited.

The sound that came from the chamber was not a shout. It was the sound a man makes when the world he understood is replaced, in an instant, by a world he cannot — a sound wrenched from the body before the mind has organised it into language. Then the language came: "O horror, horror, horror! Tongue nor heart cannot conceive nor name thee!"

The castle woke. The household poured into corridors and stairways and the space outside the king's chamber, and the news moved through the building like fire through dry timber — the king was dead, the king was murdered, the king lay in his bed with his blood on the sheets and his grooms beside him stained with the evidence of their crime. Macbeth went in. He saw what he had done — saw it in daylight, saw it framed by other men's horror, saw the body of the king he had killed displayed to him as though he were discovering it for the first time — and the performance required of him was the most precise performance of his life.

He killed the grooms. He killed them immediately, before they could wake fully, before they could speak, before they could be questioned — two swift strokes, the efficient violence of a soldier removing a problem — and when the household stared at him he said it was fury. Loyal rage. The sight of his king murdered by the very men appointed to guard him had overwhelmed his judgement, and he had acted before thinking, and who among them would not have done the same?

Some believed him. Some — Macduff's eyes, steady across the bloody chamber — did not.

Lady Macbeth fainted. She fainted at precisely the right moment, with precisely the right mixture of overwhelmed femininity and horrified distress, and the faint drew every eye in the room away from the dead grooms and toward the fallen hostess, and by the time she was carried to her chambers the conversation had moved past the question of why Macbeth had killed the only witnesses and onto the question of what happened next.

Malcolm and Donalbain stood in the corridor outside their father's chamber and understood what their understanding could bear. Their father was dead. Their father had been murdered under the roof of a man who called himself kinsman, and the men who had supposedly done the murder were themselves already dead — killed by that same kinsman in a burst of fury so immediate and so convenient that it left no mouth alive to contradict any version of events anyone might care to construct.

They were young, but they were not stupid.

"What will you do?" Malcolm said to his brother, and the question was not the question of a grieving son but the question of a prince who had looked around the chamber and counted the swords and measured the distances between himself and the doors and understood, with the cold clarity that terror sometimes provides, that the safest place for the sons of a murdered king was anywhere other than where the king had been murdered.

"I'll to Ireland," Donalbain said. His voice was low. They stood close together in the corridor while the household swirled around them — servants weeping, lords conferring, Macbeth's voice carrying from the chamber with the steady authority of a man who had taken command of the crisis because someone had to and because commanding crises was what he did — and the two princes spoke to each other with the compressed urgency of men planning a retreat under fire.

"The near in blood, the nearer bloody," Donalbain said, and the sentence was a whole education in the politics of succession, delivered in six words by a boy who had just become a man in the worst possible way.

Malcolm nodded. He did not look back at the chamber. He did not look at Macbeth. He looked at his brother, and an agreement passed between them that required no further language — the agreement of two people who understood that grief was a luxury they could not afford until they were alive enough to feel it, and that being alive required distance, and that distance required speed.

They separated before noon. They separated without announcement, without farewell, without the public ritual of princely departure that would have been their right and their danger. Malcolm rode south with a small escort and did not stop riding until he had crossed into England, where the court would receive him as the displaced heir of a murdered king and where he could wait and watch and survive. Donalbain turned west, toward the Irish Sea, toward a different kind of exile in a different kingdom. They split the compass between them — east and west, England and Ireland — so that whatever net might be cast for the sons of Duncan could not close around both at once.

Their flight was noted. Their flight was interpreted. In the corridors of Inverness and in the halls of the lords who gathered in the days that followed, the absence of Duncan's sons became a fact as useful as it was suspicious — useful to the man who needed the throne empty, suspicious to anyone who paused to wonder why two princes would flee the scene of their father's murder unless they had reason to fear the justice that would follow. The interpretation settled into place with the neatness of a key in a lock: the princes had fled because the princes were guilty. The grooms had been their instruments. The thing explained itself.

And so the crown moved.

It moved with the machinery of necessity — Scotland could not be kingless, the lords could not be lordless, the country that had just fought a war against Norway and its own rebels could not be left to drift without a hand on the tiller — and the machinery of necessity pointed, as though magnetised, toward the thane whose loyalty had saved the kingdom, whose sword had won the war, whose roof had sheltered the murdered king, and whose outraged blade had punished the murderers before they could draw breath to lie. Macbeth was kinsman to Duncan. Macbeth was Thane of Glamis and Cawdor. Macbeth was the most powerful lord remaining on Scottish soil, and Scottish soil needed a king, and the lords of Scotland gathered and the thing was done.

He was crowned at Scone. The ancient stone, the ancient words, the ancient rituals that transformed a lord into a sovereign — all of it performed with the solemnity that Scotland required and the speed that circumstance demanded. Lady Macbeth stood beside him. She stood where a queen stands when a king is made, and her face was composed into an expression of appropriate gravity — grief for the murdered Duncan, resolve for the burdens of the crown, the steady presence of a wife supporting a husband called to an office he had not sought — and the expression was flawless, and the composure was total, and if her hands remembered the weight of the daggers she had carried back to the chamber, if her skin remembered the sensation of another man's blood applied with deliberate precision to the faces of sleeping grooms, nothing in her bearing betrayed it.

They had what they wanted. The prophecy had delivered exactly what it promised — king hereafter — and the delivery had cost a single night's work, two daggers, and the life of an old man sleeping in a bed he had been invited to sleep in. The crown sat on Macbeth's head. The throne held Macbeth's weight. The court assembled around Macbeth's authority. Scotland called Macbeth king, and Macbeth was king, and the word was true in every public sense of the word, confirmed by ritual and precedent and the practical acquiescence of every lord who bent his knee at Scone.

The marriage, in these first days, still held. It held the way a structure holds after the foundation has shifted — visibly intact, functionally sound, bearing weight — because the secret that bound them was still shared equally, still carried between two people who had planned it together and executed it together and who understood that their survival depended on their unity. They moved through the court as king and queen, and the partnership that had conceived the murder now administered its consequences, and if there were silences between them that had not existed before — pauses in conversation that lasted a breath too long, moments where one looked at the other and the look contained something that had not been there when they were merely thane and thane's wife — the silences were small, were manageable, were the ordinary cost of extraordinary action.

But Macbeth did not sleep. He had not slept since the night of the murder — not truly, not the deep restorative sleep of a man whose conscience permits unconsciousness — and the voice he had heard in the corridor at Inverness had spoken the truth, as the voices in this story always spoke the truth: *Macbeth shall sleep no more.* He lay in the king's bed in the king's chambers and he stared at the king's ceiling and he heard the sounds of a castle settling around him, and every sound was a footstep, and every footstep was discovery, and every discovery was the end of everything he had seized. The crown sat on his head by day, and by night it sat on the bedpost and watched him not sleeping, and the not-sleeping was the first payment on a debt whose full price he had not yet been told.

And there was the other matter. The other part of the prophecy — the part he had not written home about, the part that sat in his mind like a stone in a shoe, present at every step, impossible to ignore, impossible to resolve. *Thou shalt get kings, though thou be none,* the Sisters had said to Banquo. Banquo, whose son still lived. Banquo, whose line would inherit what Macbeth had murdered to possess. Banquo, who had been on the heath that day, who had heard the prophecy, who had watched its first two-thirds come true with the mechanical precision of a clock striking the hours, and who now stood in Macbeth's court with the knowledge of what had been promised and the intelligence to wonder how the promising had been helped along.

The new king sat on his new throne and looked out at his new court and saw, among the faces of the lords who called him sovereign, the face of the man whose children would replace him. The prophecy had given Macbeth the crown. The same prophecy had told him the crown was borrowed.

The costs were accruing. They had not yet come due.

Banquo watched.

He stood where a lord of Scotland stands when a king is made — among the thanes, among the witnesses, in the ranks of men whose presence lends the ceremony its authority and whose bent knees lend the new sovereign his legitimacy — and he watched Macbeth receive the crown at Scone, and he said nothing, and his face was the face of a loyal subject witnessing a solemn and necessary act, and behind that face his mind was doing the work his mouth could not.

He had been on the heath. He had heard what Macbeth had heard, standing in the same wind, squinting through the same fog, listening to the same three voices deliver their promises with the calm precision of women reading from a ledger. *All hail, Macbeth, that shalt be king hereafter.* He had heard that. He had heard, too, the part that was his — *thou shalt get kings, though thou be none* — and he had carried both halves of the prophecy through the weeks that followed the way a man carries two stones in the same pocket, aware of both, weighing both, unable to set either down.

The first stone: Glamis. That was already true when the Sisters spoke it. It was merely a name for what Macbeth already was, a proof of knowledge rather than a prediction, and it had cost Banquo no sleep.

The second stone: Cawdor. That had arrived within the hour, carried by Ross like a dispatch from the future, and Banquo had watched Macbeth receive it and had seen the thing move across his friend's face — not surprise, exactly, but the expression of a man who has been told something impossible and then watched the impossible walk calmly through his front door and sit down at his table. The prophecy had proved itself. It had converted its own improbability into evidence.

And now the third. King hereafter. Here. Now. The stone of Scone beneath him, the crown settling onto his head with the weight of ritual and the sanction of the assembled lords, and Macbeth was king, and the prophecy had completed itself with the same mechanical inevitability that had governed its first two movements, and Banquo stood in the crowd of witnesses and felt the full shape of the pattern lock into place beneath his feet like the floor of a building he had not known he was standing in.

The old priest spoke the words. The lords knelt. Banquo knelt with them — his knees bent, his head bowed, his body performing the gestures that loyalty required — and from that angle, looking at the flagstones, he let his thoughts run where his eyes could not be seen to follow.

Duncan had been murdered in Macbeth's castle. In Macbeth's care. Under Macbeth's roof, where the king had come as guest and kinsman, trusting the ancient obligations of hospitality the way a man trusts the ground beneath his feet — absolutely, without examination, because the alternative was unthinkable. The grooms had been blamed. The grooms had been killed. The grooms had been killed by Macbeth himself, in a fury so swift and so complete that it had silenced every tongue that might have told a different story, and the swiftness had been explained as grief, as outrage, as the natural response of a loyal thane confronted with the murder of his sovereign, and the explanation had been accepted because accepting it was easier than examining it and because examining it led to places that the lords of Scotland did not wish to go.

The princes had fled. That, too, had been explained — guilt, complicity, the panicked flight of young men caught in a conspiracy that had unraveled — and that explanation, too, had been accepted, and the acceptance had been general, and the crown had moved to the man it was always going to move to, the man the prophecy had named, the man whose sword had silenced the only witnesses, the man in whose house the murder had been committed.

Banquo rose from his knees with the other lords. He stood. He looked at the new king.

He had ridden beside this man through battle. He had shared fires with him on campaign, shared silences, shared the particular intimacy of soldiers who have seen each other afraid and have not spoken of it afterward. He knew Macbeth's face the way a man knows the face of a companion of years — every expression catalogued, every tic of the jaw and shift of the eyes filed away in the deep unconscious library of long familiarity. And he looked at Macbeth now, crowned, robed, elevated, receiving the obeisance of Scotland's lords with the steady gravity of a man who had been called to an office he had not sought, and Banquo saw — not on the surface, not in any single feature or gesture that could be named and pointed to, but in the aggregate, in the total composition of the man — something he recognized.

He recognized it because he had been watching for it. He had been watching since the heath, since the fog, since the three voices had spoken and vanished and left their words sitting in the air like smoke. He had been watching Macbeth the way a man watches a horizon where he expects weather — not staring, not announcing his attention, but attending, quietly and steadily, to the direction from which the change would come.

And the change had come. And it looked exactly like what it was.

*Thou hast it now,* Banquo thought, and the thought was not an accusation but a recognition, the quiet internal click of a conclusion arriving at the place where the evidence had been pointing all along. *King, Cawdor, Glamis, all, as the weird women promised. And I fear thou played'st most foully for it.*

He did not say this. He said nothing. He stood among the lords and he watched the ceremony conclude and he joined the murmur of acclamation that followed the crowning, and his voice was indistinguishable from the voices around him, loyal and grave and appropriate to the occasion, and the thought stayed where thoughts stay — behind the face, below the tongue, in the private country where a man keeps the things he knows but cannot prove and cannot speak and cannot unknow.

Lady Macbeth stood beside the new king. Banquo looked at her, too — briefly, carefully, the way one looks at the second figure in a painting after studying the first — and she was perfect. Her composure was flawless. Her grief for Duncan was visible and measured and exactly proportionate to her new station — a queen mourning her husband's predecessor, bearing the weight of succession with appropriate solemnity. There was nothing in her bearing to confirm what Banquo suspected, nothing in her expression to betray the work that he believed her hands had done or helped to do. She was simply a wife standing beside a husband who had been crowned, and the standing was dignified, and the dignity was total.

But Banquo had been on the heath. And Banquo knew that the prophecy had not been an observation but an engine — that it had not described what would happen but had set in motion the wanting that would make it happen — and he knew, with the certainty of a man who has watched a friend change, that the crown sitting on Macbeth's head had not arrived there by the machinery of fate alone. Hands had helped. Human hands, in a dark room, on a night when a good king slept in a bed he had been invited to sleep in.

He thought of Fleance. His son. The boy who was also part of the prophecy — *thou shalt get kings* — and the thought of his son in a kingdom ruled by a man who had murdered his way to the throne produced in Banquo a sensation he had not felt on any battlefield: a cold, patient fear that settled into his chest and stayed there, not as panic but as awareness, the way a soldier becomes aware of an archer on a wall he has not yet found cover from.

The ceremony ended. The lords dispersed. The new court began to form around the new king — the gravitational rearrangement that follows any transfer of power, the drawing-in of some men and the quiet withdrawal of others, the recalibration of loyalties and distances that is the real work of a coronation, performed not on the stone at Scone but in the corridors and courtyards afterward, in the small conversations and the sidelong glances and the calculations that every thane was making about where he stood and where he needed to stand.

Banquo made his calculations, too. He calculated that he knew something no other lord in Scotland knew — that he had stood on a heath with the man who was now king and had heard the future spoken aloud by voices that had no reason to lie — and he calculated that this knowledge made him valuable, and he calculated that this knowledge made him dangerous, and he calculated that in a court ruled by a man who had killed to get what the prophecy promised, being dangerous was not a thing that led to long life.

He kept his counsel. He held his tongue. He watched.

The new king held court, and the new queen stood beside him, and Scotland bent its knee, and the false victory shone in the torchlight like gold, and Banquo stood among the lords who called Macbeth sovereign and carried inside himself the quiet, corrosive knowledge that the man he was kneeling to was a murderer, and that the prophecy that had made this murderer a king had also promised that the king's crown would pass not to his own children but to Banquo's, and that this promise — if the new king remembered it, and the new king would remember it, because the new king remembered everything the Sisters had said — made Banquo and his son the only men in Scotland whose future the prophecy had set against the throne.

He thought of Fleance again. He thought of his boy.

He did not sleep well that night either.

He had given the order the way a man signs a letter — with the steady hand of someone who has already composed the thing in his mind and is merely completing the physical act that makes it real. The murderers were not lords. They were not thanes. They were men whose names did not matter and whose faces he would not remember, men who had been found in the corridors where such men are always found when a king needs something done that a king cannot be seen doing, and he had spoken to them in a room where no one else was present, and the words he had used were careful and precise and left no ambiguity about what was required or who it was required of.

Banquo. And Fleance. The father and the son.

Because the prophecy had not ended with the crown. The prophecy had continued past the crown, past the moment that should have been the conclusion, past the point where a man who had done what Macbeth had done should have been permitted to stop — and it had named Banquo's issue as the inheritors of everything Macbeth had seized. *Thou shalt get kings, though thou be none.* He had heard those words on the same heath, in the same wind, standing close enough to Banquo to have touched his arm, and the words had entered him and lodged there the way a splinter lodges in flesh — painless at first, then increasingly impossible to ignore, then the only thing he could feel.

He had a crown and no son to wear it after him. He had murdered a king to sit on a throne that the same voices who promised it to him had promised would pass to another man's children. He had filed his soul, made his eternal jewel over to the common enemy of man, and for what? To make the seeds of Banquo kings. To be the instrument of another line's ascension. To have done the killing and borne the guilt and lost the sleep and felt the thing inside him that had once been loyalty and honor curdle into something he could not name — for Banquo's sons.

No.

The murderers understood. They were men who understood such things without requiring them to be explained in language that could be repeated, and they had gone, and Macbeth had returned to the business of being king — the audiences, the councils, the endless administration of a kingdom that did not know it was being governed by the man who had killed its previous governor — and he had waited for the thing to be done the way a man waits for a physician's report, knowing the answer will arrive and knowing the answer will change nothing because the decision has already been made.

The banquet was his idea. A feast for the lords, a gathering of the court, the formal performance of sovereignty that every new king must stage — not merely to feed his thanes but to be seen feeding them, to demonstrate the generosity and the command and the easy confidence of a man whose right to the throne is so secure that he can afford to celebrate it. Lady Macbeth had approved. She had more than approved; she had organized, arranged, ensured that every detail was correct, that the hall was prepared, that the seating was appropriate, that the queen's role in the evening — the welcoming, the graciousness, the warmth that a wife provides to soften the authority of a husband — was executed with the same precision she brought to everything. They had discussed it. They had discussed it as partners, as co-architects of the new court, and in the discussing there had been a moment — brief, almost imperceptible — when Macbeth had felt the old unity between them, the conspiratorial closeness that had carried them through the night of Duncan's murder, the sense of two minds working as one toward a single objective.

But he had not told her about Banquo. He had not told her about the murderers or the order or the thing that was being done even as they spoke about table arrangements and the proper vintage for the first toast. He had kept it from her. Not because he did not trust her — he trusted her as he trusted no one else alive — but because the keeping felt necessary, felt like a wall he was building between the thing he was becoming and the person who had helped him become it, and the wall was not to protect her from knowledge but to protect the last space between them where she could still look at him and see the man she had married rather than the man he was now.

*Be innocent of the knowledge, dearest chuck, till thou applaud the deed.*

He had said that to her. He had said it with something that might have been tenderness, and she had looked at him — a look he had catalogued without examining, a look that contained a question she did not ask — and the moment had passed, and the preparations had continued, and the evening had arrived.

The murderer appeared at the door of the banqueting hall with blood on his face.

Macbeth saw him before anyone else did. He was standing near the entrance, receiving the last of the arriving lords, performing the rituals of welcome that the occasion demanded — the handclasps, the inclinations, the murmured courtesies — and his eye caught the figure at the threshold the way a soldier's eye catches movement on a perimeter, automatically, before the conscious mind has named what it is seeing. The man's face was in shadow but the blood was visible, dark against the skin, and Macbeth excused himself from the conversation he was conducting and moved toward the door with the unhurried deliberation of a king attending to business, and he spoke to the man in the corridor where the torchlight did not reach.

"There's blood upon thy face."

The answer came in the low, flattened tone of a man delivering a report. Banquo was dead. His throat was cut — twenty gashes in his head, any one of which would have been enough. The deed was done. The father was dispatched.

But. The word arrived in the murderer's account like a stone dropped into still water, and Macbeth felt the ripples before the man finished speaking. Fleance had escaped. The boy had been beside his father when the attack came, and in the darkness and the confusion of the assault the boy had bolted, and the murderers had not caught him, and Fleance was gone — into the night, into the countryside, out of their reach. The son had survived.

Macbeth stood in the corridor and felt the thing he had been building shift beneath him. The father was dead and the son was alive, and the son was the one the prophecy had named, the son was the one whose line would wear the crown, and the son was now somewhere in the dark with every reason in the world to run and to remember and to return. The fit was on him again — the constriction, the sense of walls drawing in — and he mastered it the way he had learned to master such things, by clenching the jaw and pressing the thought down beneath the surface where it could not be seen on his face, and he dismissed the murderer and returned to the feast.

The hall was full. The lords were seated. The torches burned. Lady Macbeth presided from the head of the table with the composure that was her particular genius — the ability to occupy a room completely, to make the performance of authority look effortless, to smile and welcome and pour and attend while behind her eyes the machinery of calculation ran without pause. She caught his gaze as he entered and held it for a moment — a wife's glance, quick and assessing, reading his face for the information he had not given her in words — and he gave her nothing, and she accepted the nothing with the grace of a woman accustomed to receiving it, and the feast continued.

He moved toward his seat. The lords had left it empty for him — the chair at the center of the table, the king's place, the position from which a sovereign surveys his court and sees his power reflected back to him in the faces of the men who serve him. He looked at the chair.

Banquo was sitting in it.

Not Banquo as he had been — not the living man, not the companion of campaigns and the witness of the heath — but Banquo as he was now, which was dead. The figure in the chair was the figure of a man who had received twenty gashes to the head, and the gashes were visible, and the blood was visible, and the eyes were open and they were looking at Macbeth with the particular fixity of the dead, who have nothing left to look at but the person responsible for their condition, and Macbeth stopped moving and the air went out of his lungs and the words that came from his mouth came without his permission.

"Which of you have done this?"

The lords looked at him. They looked at the empty chair. They looked back at Macbeth. The chair was empty. He could see, even through the horror, that they saw nothing — that the thing in the chair was visible only to him, that the dead man's gaze was directed at him alone, that this was a private visitation being conducted in a public room — and the knowledge that he was seeing what no one else could see did not diminish the seeing but intensified it, because it meant the thing was inside him, was being generated by the same interior machinery that had produced the dagger floating before him on the night of Duncan's murder, and that machinery was not under his control and had not been under his control for some time.

"Thou canst not say I did it," he said to the thing in the chair, and his voice was not the voice of a king addressing his court but the voice of a man addressing his terror, and the lords heard it, and the room shifted. "Never shake thy gory locks at me."

Lady Macbeth was on her feet. He was aware of her the way a drowning man is aware of a hand reaching for him from a boat — distantly, through the water, as a fact that existed in a world he was no longer fully inhabiting. She was moving toward the lords, she was speaking, she was explaining — *My lord is often thus, and hath been from his youth* — and her voice was steady and her explanation was plausible and her management of the moment was, as always, immaculate, and Macbeth heard her and understood that she was saving him and could not make himself care because the thing in the chair was still there and its eyes had not moved.

She came to him. She came close and she spoke low and her words were not the words of a queen addressing a king but the words of a wife addressing a husband who is failing in front of the people he cannot afford to fail in front of, and the words were sharp and controlled and they cut through the fog of his terror with the precision of a surgeon's instrument.

"Are you a man?"

He was. He was a man. He had been a man on the battlefield, a man in the dark of Duncan's chamber, a man when he gave the order that put Banquo in the ground, and he was a man now, except that the man he was now was a man who could see the dead sitting in his chair and could not make them leave. He told her. He told her there was something in the chair, that the thing had blood on it, that the thing was looking at him, and she listened with the expression of a woman whose patience is the only structural support preventing a building from collapsing, and she told him it was nothing, it was the painting of his fear, it was the air-drawn dagger that had led him to Duncan, it was the product of a mind that had been too long without sleep and too long in the company of the things it had done.

"When all's done, you look but on a stool."

He looked. The chair was empty. The dead had gone. The air where Banquo had been was simply air, and the chair was simply a chair, and Macbeth exhaled and felt the terror drain from him with a speed that was itself alarming — not because the relief was unwelcome but because the speed suggested the terror had not gone but merely retreated, had pulled back to the place where it waited, and would return.

He returned to the table. He raised his cup. He proposed the toast that the occasion required — to the health of the company, to the prosperity of Scotland, to the absent Banquo, whose presence was missed — and as the name left his lips the thing was back. In the chair. Blood on its face. Eyes open. Gashes visible. Looking at him.

Macbeth's cup hit the table. The wine spread across the cloth like a stain finding its shape, and he was on his feet and his voice was loud and it was not the voice of a king, it was the voice of a man who has been cornered by something he cannot fight because the thing that has cornered him is himself. He spoke to the ghost. He spoke at the ghost. He told it to speak, to take any shape but the one it was wearing, to be a bear or a tiger or any living thing, and he would face it without trembling — but not this, not the dead man in the chair, not the accusation that sat in the blood and the gashes and the unblinking eyes.

The lords were standing. The lords were alarmed. The feast had broken — the fragile architecture of the evening, the careful construction of normalcy and sovereignty and celebration that Lady Macbeth had built and Macbeth had agreed to inhabit, had cracked and was collapsing, and the lords could see the cracks and they were looking at each other with the expressions of men who are revising their understanding of the ground they are standing on.

Lady Macbeth ended it. She dismissed the company. She did not stand on ceremony — she said so, explicitly, *stand not upon the order of your going, but go at once* — and her voice was the only steady thing in the room, the only instrument still playing in tune while the rest of the orchestra fell apart around it, and the lords went, and the hall emptied, and the torches burned for no one, and Macbeth and his wife were alone.

The silence that followed was the silence of a marriage that has been tested past the point where testing produces useful information and has entered the territory where every test produces only damage. She looked at him. He did not look at her. She had saved him and he knew it and the knowing did not produce gratitude but something closer to resentment — not of her, exactly, but of the fact that he needed saving, that the man who had killed Duncan and ordered Banquo's death and taken the crown of Scotland could not sit through a dinner without being ambushed by his own mind.

He told her about Macduff. He told her that Macduff had not come to the feast, that the thane of Fife had refused the invitation, that the absence was not the absence of a man with other obligations but the absence of a man making a statement, and the statement was legible, and the statement was dangerous. She listened. She had no answer. There was a time — recently, impossibly recently — when she would have had an answer, when her mind would have produced the counter-strategy as quickly and cleanly as it had produced the plan for Duncan's murder, but that time was receding, and the woman who stood in the empty hall with the spilled wine on the table and the vacant chairs pushed back from their places was not the woman who had called on the spirits to unsex her. She was tired. Macbeth could see it. The tiredness was in her eyes and in the set of her mouth and in the fractional delay between his words and her responses, and it frightened him in a way that Banquo's ghost had not, because the ghost was a product of his own disintegration and he understood disintegration, but his wife's tiredness was a product of hers, and hers he could not control.

He would go to the Weird Sisters. He said it aloud, not as a proposal but as a decision already made — *I will tomorrow, and betimes I will, to the weird sisters* — and the saying of it completed the distance that the evening had opened between them. He was going to the witches, not to his wife. He was seeking counsel from the heath, not from the marriage. The partnership that had conceived and executed Duncan's murder — the partnership that was the play's moral engine, the shared project of ambition that had bound them into a single conspiratorial unit — was separating, and the separation was visible in the room, in the space between the man who was already thinking about the cave and the woman who was standing among the wreckage of his public composure with nothing left to do but clear the table.

She went to bed. He did not. He had not slept — truly slept, the deep and restorative sleep that repairs a man's connection to himself — since the night he had killed Duncan, and the not-sleeping had become its own landscape, a terrain he inhabited permanently now, a country of dim corridors and watched shadows and thoughts that circled without arriving. He moved through the castle in the hours after the banquet the way a man moves through a house he no longer recognizes as his own — aware of the walls, aware of the doors, unable to feel that any of it belonged to him or that he belonged to any of it.

*I am in blood stepped in so far that, should I wade no more, returning were as tedious as go o'er.*

The cave was in a place that should not have had a cave — a seam in the hillside that looked, in the torchlight, less like a natural formation than like a wound in the earth that had never healed — and the Weird Sisters were inside it, and they were expecting him, and the fact that they were expecting him did not surprise him because he had stopped being surprised by them on the heath the first time, when they had known his name before he spoke it and had given him the future before he asked for it.

They showed him things. A cauldron. Apparitions that rose from the steam like thoughts taking form — an armed head, a bloody child, a crowned child bearing a tree — and each apparition spoke, and each spoke with the compressed authority of a verdict delivered from a height that did not require explanation or appeal. *Beware Macduff. Beware the thane of Fife.* That was the first, and it confirmed what he already knew, what Macduff's empty chair at the banquet had already told him in the language of political silence: the thane of Fife was the threat, the one who had not come, the one whose absence was a presence more dangerous than any lord who had attended and drunk his wine and watched him shatter.

Then the second apparition, the bloody child, and its words were different — not a warning but a reassurance, and the reassurance had the texture of armor being buckled into place: *None of woman born shall harm Macbeth.* He heard it and felt the words settle into the place where fear had been, displacing the fear not by answering it but by making it irrelevant, because if no man born of woman could harm him then Macduff could not harm him, and the threat was not a threat, and the warning was already answered by the protection that followed it. He should have heard the construction. He should have heard the way the language left a seam — *of woman born* — the way a locksmith should notice a lock that has been designed to be picked. But the words were what he wanted to hear, and a man who has not slept since he killed his king and who has watched his friend's ghost sit in his chair and bleed at his table is not a man equipped to parse the conditional clauses of supernatural assurances.

The third apparition told him he would not be vanquished until Birnam Wood came to Dunsinane Hill. He almost laughed. Wood does not walk. Trees do not uproot themselves and march. The protection was total, was fantastical in its completeness, was a guarantee so absolute that it could only be violated by an impossibility, and Macbeth accepted it the way a man dying of thirst accepts water — without examining its source, without questioning its purity, without considering the possibility that the hand offering it is the same hand that created the thirst.

He asked about Banquo's line. He should not have asked. He knew, even as the words were forming, that the asking was a mistake, that the answer would not be the answer he wanted, that the prophecy's original shape — *thou shalt get kings* — had not been revised by Banquo's death because prophecies are not revised by the actions of the men they describe. The cauldron showed him a line of kings stretching out to the crack of doom, each one wearing the crown and each one bearing Banquo's face, and the last held a glass that reflected still more, an infinity of succession that did not include Macbeth's name or Macbeth's blood or anything of Macbeth at all except the crime that had made the succession possible. He had killed to wear the crown. The crown would pass to the dead man's children. The equation was complete and it was the same equation it had always been, and no amount of murder had altered a single variable.

The Witches were gone. The cave was a cave. The heath was the heath. He stood in the dark with the smell of peat smoke and something older — something animal and mineral and wrong — clinging to his clothes, and he made the decision about Macduff's family the way a man makes a decision after he has stopped believing that decisions have moral content: quickly, cleanly, without the deliberation that implies the existence of an alternative.

Macduff was in England. He had learned this — from Ross, from the networks of intelligence that a king maintains the way a body maintains blood flow, automatically, as a condition of continued function — and the knowledge had arranged itself in his mind with a clarity that was almost architectural. Macduff was in England. Macduff was with Malcolm. Macduff had gone to the son of the king that Macbeth had murdered, and the going was not the journey of a man seeking exile but the journey of a man seeking an army, and the army would come, and when it came it would come for Macbeth, and the only question was what could be done before it arrived.

Macduff's castle. Macduff's wife. Macduff's children.

He gave the order. He gave it to the same species of men who had killed Banquo — men without names, men without faces that would be remembered, men who existed in the space between a king's intention and its execution — and the order was not complicated and did not require explanation. The castle at Fife. Everyone in it. The wife, the children, the servants, anyone found within the walls. All of them.

He did not feel the order leave him. That was the thing — not the horror of giving it, which would have been a sign that the man who gave it was still the man who had hesitated before Duncan's chamber, but the absence of horror, the flatness, the sense of performing an administrative action that differed from other administrative actions only in its content and not in its moral weight. He had crossed something. Not a line — lines imply that the territory on either side is different, and the territory felt the same. He had crossed a threshold so gradually that the crossing had been imperceptible, and now he was on the other side, and the other side looked exactly like the place he had left, except that on this side a man could order the murder of children and feel nothing, and the nothing was not numbness but competence, was the professional detachment of a man who has learned to do his work without being distracted by its nature.

The news came back to him the way all such news comes back to kings: filtered, compressed, delivered in language designed to confirm completion without dwelling on method. It was done. The castle had been entered. Lady Macduff was dead. The boy — Macduff's son, a child whose age Macbeth did not know and did not ask — was dead. The household was dead. Fife was a ruin with no one left in it to ruin.

Macbeth received the report and added it to the ledger he was keeping inside himself — the running account of what he had done and what it had cost and what it had purchased — and the ledger did not balance and had never balanced and would never balance, and he understood this, and the understanding changed nothing.

In the castle, in the nights that followed, Lady Macbeth walked in her sleep.

He did not see it himself — not at first. A gentlewoman told him. Then a doctor was summoned, and the doctor watched, and the doctor reported, and the reports were careful and indirect and couched in the language of medical discretion, but the substance was legible: his wife rose from her bed in the dark hours and moved through the castle with her eyes open and her mind closed, and she washed her hands. She washed them ceaselessly, compulsively, rubbing them together under water that was not there, scrubbing at stains that existed only in the theater of her sleeping mind, and she spoke while she washed — fragments, half-sentences, references to blood and to the smell of blood and to the impossibility of removing the smell from skin that had been cleaned and cleaned and cleaned and would not come clean.

*All the perfumes of Arabia will not sweeten this little hand.*

The doctor told him there was nothing medicine could do. The disease was beyond his practice. The patient needed the divine, not the physician — a minister of grace, not a minister of herbs. Macbeth listened and understood that the doctor was telling him his wife was dying of what they had done together, that the conspiracy which had been their shared project had become her sole inheritance, that the guilt he had outrun — or outgrown, or simply outlasted — had found in her a host willing to receive it and was consuming her from the interior with the methodical patience of a fire that has found dry wood.

He could not reach her. That was the fact that sat at the center of everything else — not that she was suffering, not that she was walking in her sleep, not that the blood she could not wash from her hands was Duncan's blood and Banquo's blood and the blood of a woman and child in Fife whose murders she did not even know about — but that the distance between them had become a space he could not cross. The partnership was over. The marriage that had conceived the murder of a king and executed it with a single shared will had separated into its component parts — a man who could not stop killing and a woman who could not stop washing — and the components did not speak to each other anymore, not really, not in the language they had invented between them on the night she had read his letter and called on the spirits to fill her with cruelty.

He armed himself. He fortified Dunsinane. He received the reports of Malcolm's army gathering in England with Macduff at its head and ten thousand men behind them, and he received them with the flat calm of a man holding two contradictory certainties in his mind at the same time: the certainty that they were coming for him, and the certainty that none of woman born could harm him and that he would not fall until Birnam Wood moved. The protections were absolute. The protections were impossible to violate. The protections had been spoken by the same voices that had spoken the crown into his future, and the crown had come true, and therefore the protections would come true, and therefore he was safe, and therefore the army did not matter, and therefore he could stand in his castle and wait for them the way a man behind a wall waits for a storm — not without awareness of the wind, but without fear of it.

The walls of Dunsinane were thick. The garrison was prepared. The king stood in his armor in a castle full of soldiers who served him because they had no choice and feared him because fear was the only currency he had left to spend, and his wife wandered the corridors at night washing blood from hands that had been clean for months, and the army was coming, and the wood stood where wood stands, rooted and unmoving, and Macbeth waited — trapped between a prophecy that promised him safety and a world that was assembling, with increasing precision, the instruments of his destruction.

The heath knew him now. That was the difference between this visit and the first — the first time, the place had been a surprise, a rupture in the ordinary landscape of a soldier's march home, and the Weird Sisters had appeared as strangers delivering intelligence to a man who had not requested it. This time he sought them. This time the seeking was the confession that the crown was not enough, that the throne he had purchased with Duncan's blood and defended with Banquo's was a structure that required its own architect to return, again, to the foundation and ask whether the foundation would hold.

He found them where he knew he would find them, in a place the land should not have contained — a wound in the hillside, a seam that opened into firelight and smoke and the sound of a language that predated the one he spoke. The cauldron was already working. They were already expecting him. He did not announce himself, and they did not require announcement; they simply turned, the three of them, with a simultaneity that suggested a single mind distributed across three bodies, and the eldest — or the one he thought of as the eldest, though age was not a category that applied to them in any way he could verify — spoke first.

*By the pricking of my thumbs, something wicked this way comes.*

Something wicked. He heard it. He registered it. He did not dispute it.

He demanded answers. He did not ask — asking was the posture of a petitioner, and he had come not as a petitioner but as a king, a man who had earned the right to command even the supernatural by the simple fact that he had done what they had told him he would do — and his demands had the quality of a man shouting into a well, hearing his own voice return to him altered by the depth and the dark and the water at the bottom that he could not see. *I conjure you, by that which you profess, answer me.* He did not care what instruments they used. He did not care what the answering cost — whether it tore apart the natural order, whether it flattened churches, whether it tumbled castles and palaces and tangled navigations and blew down corn and bent trees — he wanted to know, and the wanting had the force of something beyond desire, something muscular and structural, a need that had replaced every other need the way a river replaces everything that was in its path before the flood.

They showed him.

The first apparition rose from the cauldron's mouth like a thought extruding itself into the visible world: an armed head, helmeted, severed at the neck, its eyes open and its mouth already forming words before the steam had finished giving it shape. It spoke with an authority that did not belong to a severed head or to any single voice but to the thing behind the voices, the mechanism that generated prophecy the way a mill generates flour — mechanically, indifferently, without regard for who would eat the bread.

*Macbeth. Macbeth. Macbeth. Beware Macduff. Beware the thane of Fife.*

He started forward. He knew this already — had known it since the banquet, since the empty chair, since the absence that spoke louder than any attendance — but hearing it spoken by a mouth that had no body behind it was different from knowing it, was the difference between suspecting a wall is load-bearing and watching the engineer tap it and nod. Macduff was the threat. Macduff in England, Macduff with Malcolm, Macduff gathering the instruments of his undoing — yes. He opened his mouth to ask more, to demand the specifics of the danger and the specifics of the remedy, but the head was already sinking, and the second apparition was already rising, and the cauldron's economy did not permit interruption.

A bloody child. Newborn or near it, slick with the fluids of recent arrival, its eyes too knowing for something so recently made. It spoke, and what it spoke was a door opening in a wall he had believed was solid:

*Be bloody, bold, and resolute. Laugh to scorn the power of man, for none of woman born shall harm Macbeth.*

The words entered him the way heat enters iron — not gradually but all at once, a transformation of state that left the original material unrecognizable. None of woman born. He turned the phrase over and found no seam in it, no gap, no conditional, only the absolute: every man is born of woman, therefore no man can harm him, therefore Macduff cannot harm him, therefore the first apparition's warning was already answered by the second's assurance, and the danger was not a danger, and the fear could be set down like a weight he had been carrying for no reason. He felt his body ease. He felt something that might, in another man, have been called relief but which in him was closer to confirmation — the confirmation that the universe he had entered when he killed Duncan was a universe that would continue to protect him, that the same forces which had given him the crown would not permit the crown to be taken, that the prophecy was not a trap but a covenant and the covenant was holding.

He should have stopped. The bloody child had given him enough — had given him invulnerability in a sentence, had made him proof against every sword and every army and every thane of Fife who had ever lived or would ever live. But the cauldron was still working, and the third apparition was already emerging, and Macbeth was a man who had learned that you do not leave a table while it is still being served, because the dish you refuse might be the one that matters.

A crowned child, holding a tree. A tree in a child's hand, like a scepter made of living wood, and the child's crown was gold, and its face was calm with the calm of something that knows the future because it has already been there.

*Be lion-mettled, proud, and take no care who chafes, who frets, or where conspirers are. Macbeth shall never vanquished be until Great Birnam Wood to high Dunsinane Hill shall come against him.*

He almost laughed. He did laugh — a sound that came out of him sideways, not with joy but with the giddy vertigo of a man who has been told that the laws of physics have been suspended on his behalf. Wood does not walk. Trees do not uproot themselves and advance on castles. He was being told that he would live until the impossible happened, which was another way of saying he would live forever, which was another way of saying that the murders — Duncan's, Banquo's, and the ones still to come — had purchased not just a crown but immortality, and the price had been worth paying, and the ledger balanced after all.

*That will never be,* he said, and his voice was steady for the first time in longer than he could remember — steady with the steadiness of a man standing on ground he trusts. *Who can impress the forest? Bid the tree unfix his earth-bound root?*

But he could not stop. He could not leave the question of Banquo's line unasked, because the question had been living inside him since the first prophecy, since *thou shalt get kings, though thou be none,* and no amount of assurance about his own safety could silence the deeper question: safe for how long, and to what end, if the crown would pass from his head to Banquo's children in the end? He asked. The Witches told him not to seek to know. He insisted — with the insistence of a king, which is to say with the threat of consequences — and they showed him.

A line of kings. Eight of them, each one crowned, each one Banquo's echo, each one wearing the dead man's face with the casual authority of an heir wearing a father's ring. They stretched through the steam in a procession that had the quality of a corridor without end, each figure giving way to the next, and the last held a mirror — a glass that showed still more, an infinite regression of Banquo's blood on Banquo's throne, kings beyond counting, a dynasty that extended past the visible into a future where Macbeth's name was a footnote and Banquo's line was the sentence. And Banquo's ghost stood at the end of the line and smiled — the same smile he had smiled in life, the smile of a man who understood patience, who understood that prophecy operates on a timescale that outlasts the men who try to outrun it — and the ghost pointed at the procession, and the pointing said: *This. All of this. Despite everything you have done. Because of everything you have done.*

The cauldron went dark. The Sisters vanished — not departed but vanished, the way a flame vanishes when you close your eyes and open them again and it is simply not there, as if it had never been. He stood alone in the cave that should not have been a cave, in the wound in the earth, and the smoke smelled of peat and bone and something else, something that might have been the scent of time itself bending around a fixed point, and he understood — not with his mind, which was occupied with the tactical implications of Macduff and Birnam Wood and the protections he had been given, but with whatever faculty operates beneath the mind, in the place where a man's knowledge of himself is stored — that the prophecy had given him exactly what he wanted and that what he wanted was exactly the shape of his destruction, and that these two facts were not contradictions but were the same fact expressed in two different languages, and he did not speak the second language well enough to read the warning.

He left the heath. He carried the prophecies with him the way a soldier carries provisions — as supplies for the campaign ahead, not as gifts, not as comforts, but as the material necessary for continued operation. Macduff was the threat. No man born of woman could harm him. Birnam Wood would not move. These were the coordinates of his safety, the three points that defined the space in which he could continue to be king, and he arranged them in his mind with the precision of a man who has been trained to read terrain, and the terrain told him he was safe, and he believed the terrain because the alternative was to believe that the same forces that had made him king had also designed his death, and that belief was not a belief a man in his position could afford.

Macduff left in the night, and the night did not remark upon his leaving.

There was no announcement, no formal withdrawal of allegiance, no letter sealed and dispatched to the new king's court explaining the reasons for departure — reasons that would have required naming the thing that everyone in Scotland knew and no one in Scotland said aloud, which was that the man who wore the crown had taken it from a dead man's head and that the dead man had been sleeping under the killer's roof when the killing was done. Macduff simply was in Fife one evening and was not in Fife the next morning, and the space he left behind him had the quality of a statement more eloquent than any speech he could have composed, because a speech can be argued with and an absence cannot.

He rode south and then south again, through country that was still Scotland but that felt, with every mile, less like the Scotland he had known — or rather, the Scotland he had known felt less like itself with every mile he put between himself and Dunsinane, as if proximity to the usurper's castle was the thing that distorted the landscape and distance was the cure. He crossed into England the way a man crosses from a burning building into open air: not with triumph, not with relief, but with the animal recognition that the air on this side could be breathed and the air on the other side could not.

Malcolm was in England. Malcolm, Duncan's eldest son, who had fled the night of his father's murder with the instinct of a man who understands that the same hand that killed the king would not hesitate to kill the king's heir — Malcolm was at the English court, and Macduff was riding toward him, and the riding was itself the declaration that words would have diluted. He was not going to England to wait. He was not going to England to hide. He was going to England to build the instrument of Macbeth's removal, and the building would require Malcolm's name and English arms and the combined moral weight of every lord in Scotland who had watched the coronation at Scone and known, in the private chamber of his own judgment, that the crown had been placed on the wrong head.

He did not bring his family.

This was the fact that would matter later — would matter more than any other fact in the story of Macduff's life, more than his discovery of Duncan's body, more than his refusal to attend the banquet, more than the confrontation that was still ahead of him on the battlefield — but at the moment of his departure it was simply a tactical decision, the kind a soldier makes when speed is more important than completeness. His wife was in Fife. His children were in Fife. Fife was still Scotland, and Scotland was still Macbeth's, and Macduff rode south without them because he believed — or needed to believe, or could not afford to consider the alternative to believing — that a king, even a king who had murdered his way to the throne, would not touch a man's wife and children. There were laws older than kingship. There were customs that even tyrants observed because the observance was the thing that separated tyranny from something worse, something without a name, something that the world had not yet needed a word for because no one had yet descended to the depth where the word became necessary.

He was wrong about this. But he did not know he was wrong, and the not-knowing was the mercy that allowed him to ride.

Word reached Dunsinane within days. It reached Macbeth not as a report — no messenger knelt before him with the formal phrasing of intelligence delivered to a sovereign — but as an accumulation, a thickening of the air around a fact that had already been suspected and was now confirmed. Macduff was gone. Macduff was in England. Macduff was with Malcolm.

Macbeth received the news in the castle that had been Duncan's last lodging and was now his permanent residence, and he received it the way a man receives confirmation of a diagnosis he has already made: without surprise, without the shock that accompanies genuinely new information, but with the grim settling of a body adjusting to a weight it had been preparing to carry. The armed head in the cauldron had said *beware Macduff.* The bloody child had said *none of woman born shall harm Macbeth.* These two statements lived inside him simultaneously, the warning and the protection, the disease and the cure administered in the same dose, and Macduff's flight to England was simply the warning made geographical — the threat now had a location, a direction, a trajectory that pointed back toward Scotland like an arrow being drawn.

He stood at a window. The castle's stone was cold in the way that Scottish stone is always cold, a cold that does not respond to fires or bodies or the passage of seasons but simply persists, geological and indifferent, and the view from the window was the view of a kingdom that belonged to him by every measure except the ones that mattered — by law, by anointing, by the submission of the lords who had attended his coronation — and yet felt, in moments like this one, like a thing he was holding rather than a thing he possessed, the difference being that a thing you hold can be taken from your hands and a thing you possess cannot.

His wife was somewhere in the castle. He did not seek her. This was new — this failure to seek her, this absence of the impulse that had once sent him to her with every piece of intelligence, every prophecy, every fear and every ambition, the way a man brings raw material to the craftsman who knows how to shape it. The letter he had written after the first prophecy, the letter that had carried the Witches' words from the battlefield to her hands in Inverness, felt now like a document from another marriage, another lifetime, a communication between two people who no longer existed in the forms that had produced it. He had stopped telling her things. He could not have said when the stopping began — whether it was after the banquet, after the ghost, after the moment when he looked across the hall and saw in her face not the iron partner who had smeared blood on the grooms' hands but a woman trying to manage a situation that had outgrown her capacity to manage it — but the stopping was now complete, and the completeness had the quality of a door that had been closed so gradually that neither of them had heard the latch.

Macduff was in England. The armed head had been right. The bloody child had also been right. Both truths held.

And in Fife, in the castle that Macduff had left behind him with the speed of a man outrunning a fire, his wife waited with his children in rooms that still smelled of his occupation — his books, his dogs, the particular quality of air that a man's presence leaves in the spaces he has inhabited — and she did not yet know, could not yet know, that her husband's flight had made her not safer but less safe, had drawn upon her household the specific and annihilating attention of a king who had learned that the only way to answer a threat is to destroy everything the threat has left behind, and that the laws older than kingship — the customs, the protections, the unwritten agreements about wives and children and the sanctity of a man's home — were laws that applied in the Scotland that had existed before, the Scotland of Duncan, the Scotland where fair was fair and foul was foul, and that in the Scotland Macbeth had made, the Scotland where those categories had been inverted so thoroughly that the inversion was now the natural order, those laws had no force, no weight, no purchase on the decisions of a man who had already killed his king and his friend and his sleep and was now, in the arithmetic of his own survival, simply solving for the next variable.

Macduff was in England. His family was in Scotland. And the distance between those two facts was the distance across which the worst thing in the play would travel.

She had been angry before she was afraid, and the anger had lasted longer than it should have, the way a fire lit in a dry season will burn past the point where the thing that started it has been forgotten, sustained now by its own heat rather than by any fresh fuel. Her husband had left. Her husband had left without explanation, without apology, without the elementary courtesy of a lie — a fabricated errand, a diplomatic summons, any of the small fictions a man offers his wife when the truth of his departure is too large or too dangerous to speak aloud — and the leaving itself was the thing she could not forgive, not because she failed to understand its reasons but because understanding the reasons did not make the rooms less empty or the children less fatherless or the nights less long.

Ross had come. Ross had stood in her hall with the careful face of a man delivering a message he wished he did not carry, and he had said the things that men say when they are trying to make a woman's abandonment sound like strategy — *he is noble, wise, judicious* — and she had listened to the words the way one listens to rain on a roof, aware of the sound, aware that it signified something about conditions outside, but unable to extract from it any information that changed the conditions inside, which were that she was alone in Fife with her children and her husband was in England and the distance between those two facts was growing not smaller but larger with every hour that passed.

Ross had left too. Everyone left. That was the pattern she was beginning to understand — that Scotland had become a country people left, that the leaving was the only rational response to what Scotland had become, and that the people who remained were the people who could not leave or who had not yet understood that leaving was necessary, and she did not know which of those categories contained her.

Her son was with her. He was young enough that the world still presented itself to him as a series of questions rather than a series of answers, and the questions he asked had the quality of instruments — small, precise, calibrated to extract from the adults around him the information they were trying to withhold. *Was my father a traitor?* he asked, and the question landed in the room with the weight of something much heavier than a child's curiosity, because the word *traitor* had become, in Macbeth's Scotland, a word that meant whatever the king needed it to mean, a word that could be applied to any man whose loyalty was insufficient or whose absence was inconvenient or whose existence presented a problem that the king preferred to solve permanently.

She told him his father was dead. She did not mean it — or she meant it in the way that a woman means a thing she says in anger, which is to say she meant the feeling underneath the words rather than the words themselves, the feeling being that a man who leaves his wife and children unprotected in a country ruled by a murderer has performed a kind of death, has made himself absent in a way that is functionally indistinguishable from the absence that death produces, and that the distinction between a father who is dead and a father who is in England is a distinction without a practical difference to the children who are in neither place.

The boy did not believe her. He argued with her the way children argue — not with logic exactly, but with the relentless application of a child's understanding of how the world ought to work, an understanding that had not yet been corrupted by evidence of how the world actually worked. If his father were dead, she would weep for him. She was not weeping. Therefore his father was not dead. The syllogism was perfect in its way, airtight within the closed system of a child's reasoning, and she could not bring herself to dismantle it because dismantling it would have required telling him the truth, which was that there were things worse than death and that one of those things was the kind of absence his father had chosen, and that she was not weeping because the tears that come from grief are different from the tears that come from rage and she had passed from one country into the other without noticing the border.

They were talking — mother and son, in the particular mode of conversation that exists only between those two parties, a mode that is simultaneously instruction and performance and negotiation and love — when the world changed.

It changed the way weather changes in Scotland: without adequate warning, without the gradual accumulation of signs that allows preparation, but with the sudden totality of a sky that was light and is now dark and the darkness is not a transition but a replacement, as if the light had never been. A messenger came. Not Ross — Ross was gone, Ross had performed his function and departed — but a man she did not know, a man whose face carried the particular expression of someone who is about to say a thing that cannot be unsaid, and the thing he said was simple and was terrible and was the last piece of information that would have been useful to her if it had arrived an hour earlier but that arrived instead at the exact moment when its usefulness had expired:

*You must leave. Now. Take your children. Go.*

She asked where. The question was reasonable — a woman told to flee requires a destination, a direction, some coordinate in the geography of safety toward which flight can be oriented — but the man had no answer, or his answer was his own departure, which was immediate, which left her standing in her own hall with the knowledge that danger was coming and no knowledge of where safety lived, and the interval between those two pieces of information — the presence of the first and the absence of the second — was the interval in which everything that was about to happen would happen.

She did not leave. She could not have said afterward — if there had been an afterward — whether the failure to leave was a choice or a paralysis, whether she stayed because she decided to stay or because the decision to leave required a capacity for action that the suddenness of the warning had destroyed. She was in her own house. The walls were stone. The doors were oak. These were facts that had meant safety once, in the Scotland that existed before, and her body still believed them even as her mind understood that stone and oak meant nothing to men who had been sent by a king, that the king's authority passed through walls the way light passes through glass, and that the protections she had believed in — the customs, the ancient prohibitions against harming a lord's family in his absence — were protections that belonged to a different country, a country that shared Scotland's geography but not its laws.

They came through the doors. There were several of them — she would not have been able to count them, would not have been able to distinguish one from another, because they had the quality of a single thing rather than a collection of individuals, a single intention distributed across multiple bodies, and the intention was legible in their movement, in the way they occupied her hall as if it had already been emptied, as if she and her son were not present but were instead obstacles between the men and the completion of the task they had been given.

One of them spoke. He called her husband a traitor. The word again — the word that meant nothing and meant everything, the word that Macbeth had made into a weapon that could be aimed at anyone, the word that transformed loyalty into its opposite and made the act of fleeing to England — the act of seeking the legitimate heir, the act of trying to restore the order that the king himself had destroyed — into a crime punishable by the destruction of everything the fleeing man had left behind.

Her son spoke. Her son, who was young enough that the word *traitor* was still a word that required examination rather than a word that demanded submission, who had been arguing about the meaning of the word minutes earlier with the intellectual fearlessness of a child who does not yet understand that words in Macbeth's Scotland had edges, that they could cut, that the men who wielded them did not intend them as propositions to be debated but as sentences to be executed — her son said *thou liest, thou shag-haired villain,* and the words came out of him with the clarity and the conviction and the absolute, unshakable certainty of someone who knew what a traitor was and knew that his father was not one, and the knowing was the thing that killed him, because one of the men — the single intention acting through its nearest body — put a blade into the boy who had spoken, and the boy fell, and the falling was fast and was slow and was the thing that she would see for the rest of her life, which was not long.

He said *run.* The boy said *run* to his mother. He was dying and he told her to run, and the instruction was the last thing he made, the final product of a mind that had been asking questions since the scene began and that ended not with a question but with an imperative, a command issued from a child to his mother in an inversion of every natural order that the play had left standing, because the play had by now inverted all the others — king and subject, host and guest, husband and wife — and this was the last one, the deepest one, the inversion that reached down into the structure of the family itself and turned it inside out: the child protecting the mother, the dying instructing the living, the small body on the floor more courageous than the men with the blades.

She ran. She ran because her son told her to, and because running was the only verb left, and because the body's insistence on its own continuation is the last instinct to surrender, outlasting courage and reason and even love. She ran through rooms that had been her home, past walls that had contained her marriage and her children and her daily life, and the men followed her, and the following was not hurried because it did not need to be, because the castle was finite and the exits were known and the task they had been given was simple and they were going to complete it.

They completed it.

What happened was not shown — it happened in the space between one room and the next, in the darkness that the play's eye declined to enter, as if even the story itself could not bear to look directly at the thing it had made necessary. She was alive and then she was not alive, and the transition between those two states was the act that Macbeth had ordered from Dunsinane with the same administrative efficiency with which a king orders a bridge built or a tax collected — a task assigned, a task executed, a task completed, the completion reported back through the chain of authority to the man who had initiated it, who would receive the report the way he received all reports now, which was with the flat acknowledgment of a man for whom the distinction between ordering a death and eating a meal had become a distinction of degree rather than kind.

In Fife, in the castle that still smelled of Macduff's books and Macduff's dogs, the bodies of his wife and his son lay in rooms that no one would enter for hours, because the men who had done the killing had left and the servants who might have found them were hiding and the lord who should have protected them was in England, building an army, not yet knowing that the thing the army was meant to avenge had already happened, that the cost of his departure had been calculated and extracted in full, and that the laws older than kingship — the laws he had believed in, the laws whose existence had permitted him to ride south without his family — were laws that had died before his wife and son did, had died the night Duncan died, had died in the same bed and by the same hand, and that everything since then had been the consequences of that first death working themselves out through the bodies of the innocent with the mechanical inevitability of a stone rolling downhill, gathering speed, gathering weight, unstoppable now, the bottom not yet reached.

She could not get her hands clean.

This was the thing — the central, irreducible, ungovernable thing — and it had been the thing for longer than she could remember, though remembering had itself become unreliable, had become a faculty that operated on its own schedule and by its own rules, presenting her with information she had not requested at hours when she had not asked for it, withdrawing the ordinary comforts of sequence and chronology and replacing them with a single image, a single sensation, a single fact that repeated itself with the idiot persistence of water dripping on stone: there was blood on her hands, and washing did not remove it, and she had been washing for a very long time.

She was not awake. She would not have said she was asleep either, because the state she occupied was neither of those things but a third condition, a country between the two that had no name in any language she knew, a country whose principal feature was that the barriers between what had happened and what was happening had dissolved, so that the past was not behind her but around her, not a thing she recalled but a thing she inhabited, and the rooms she moved through — the stone corridors of Dunsinane, the stairs she descended with a taper in her hand, the passages that her body navigated with the competence of long residence while her mind was elsewhere, was everywhen, was in every room she had ever occupied — these rooms were also the rooms at Inverness, the rooms of that night, the rooms in which she had waited for her husband to come down the stairs with the daggers and had heard instead the sound of a man discovering that the act he had committed could not be put back inside the body that had committed it, that it had leaked out of him the way the king's blood had leaked out of the king, and that no amount of resolution or will or conjugal authority could push it back in.

She had pushed it back in. That was her role — the role she had assigned herself, the role she had performed with the efficiency of a woman who understood that hesitation was a luxury available only to people who had not yet crossed the threshold she and her husband had crossed together, hand in bloody hand. *A little water clears us of this deed,* she had said, and she had meant it, had believed it with the full force of a mind that had not yet learned what the body would later teach it, which was that water does not clear, that clearance is not a thing water can perform, that the deed and the hands and the water exist in a relationship that is permanent and that no amount of washing will alter.

She washed. Her hands moved against each other in the dark — the taper set down somewhere, its light unnecessary for an activity that had become so practiced it no longer required sight — and the motion was the motion of a woman scrubbing, but there was no basin, no water, no soap, nothing between her skin and her skin except the friction of the act itself, and the act had worn grooves in her, had become the thing her body did when her mind was in the other country, the country of sleep that was not sleep, the country where Duncan still bled and the blood still would not come off and the night still would not end.

*Out,* she said. Her voice was not her voice — or it was her voice stripped of the thing that had made it hers, stripped of the command and the precision and the controlled fury that had been its distinguishing features in the days when she had used it to reshape her husband's will into a weapon, in the days when words had been tools she wielded with the confidence of a craftsman who knows exactly what each instrument is for and exactly how much pressure to apply. Now the words came out of her the way water comes out of a crack in a vessel — not directed, not intended, but forced out by a pressure that had been building for longer than the vessel was designed to contain it.

*Out, damned spot.*

The spot did not go out. The spot had never gone out. The spot was the permanent residue of the night she had taken the daggers from her husband's hands — because he would not go back, because he could not look at what he had done, because the man who had killed a king in his bed could not bear to return to the room where the king lay killed, and so she had gone, she had taken the daggers from his shaking hands and walked back up the stairs and into the room where Duncan lay and she had smeared the sleeping grooms with blood, and the blood had been warm, and the warmth was the thing she could not stop feeling, the warmth of another person's life on her hands, a warmth that should have cooled and dried and flaked away but that had instead sunk into her skin and taken up residence there, a tenant that could not be evicted, a presence that announced itself every time she brought her hands together, which was always, which was every night, which was now.

Two people watched her. She did not know they watched. They stood in the margins of the corridor — the gentlewoman who attended her and the doctor the gentlewoman had summoned — and they watched the way people watch a thing they cannot help and cannot look away from, the way one watches a house burn when the fire is too advanced for water, when the watching is the only relationship available between the observer and the observed. The gentlewoman had seen this before — had seen her lady rise from bed, put on her nightgown, take paper from the locked desk, write upon it, read it, seal it, return to bed, all without waking, all with the mechanical precision of a body executing instructions its conscious mind had not issued — and the seeing had frightened her enough to seek a physician, because the things her lady said during these episodes were things that a gentlewoman should not hear and could not repeat and dared not carry alone.

The doctor listened. He was a man trained in the ailments of the body, in the fevers and the fluxes and the wounds that could be dressed and drained and sewn shut, and what he was witnessing was not an ailment of the body. What he was witnessing was something for which his training had not prepared him, something that belonged to a different discipline — the discipline of the soul, if such a discipline existed, or the discipline of consequence, the study of what happens to a human being who has done a thing that the human being was not built to contain.

She spoke. The words came in fragments, in pieces that did not connect to each other except through the underground logic of the dreaming mind, a logic that was not sequential but associative, each phrase linked to the next not by narrative but by the emotional pressure that forced them out:

*Yet who would have thought the old man to have had so much blood in him?*

The doctor heard it. The gentlewoman heard it. The sentence hung in the corridor the way smoke hangs in a room after the fire has been put out — a remnant, a residue, evidence of the thing that had produced it. The old man. The blood. The quantity of blood. The surprise — *who would have thought* — as if the killing had been an experiment whose results exceeded the experimenter's expectations, as if the body of an old king contained more life than the woman who ordered his death had calculated, and the surplus was the thing she could not account for, the remainder that would not reduce, the excess that had overflowed the plan she had made and now overflowed the mind that had made it.

*The Thane of Fife had a wife,* she said. *Where is she now?*

The question was not a question. The question was a statement dressed in the grammar of interrogation, and what it stated was that she knew — knew about Macduff's wife, knew about Macduff's children, knew that her husband had done this thing without telling her, without consulting her, without the partnership that had characterized their first crime and that had dissolved somewhere in the interval between that crime and this one, dissolved so gradually that she could not identify the moment of dissolution, could not say *here, this is where he stopped needing me, this is where he began to act alone,* because the dissolution had not been a moment but a process, a slow withdrawal of confidence and consultation that mirrored the slow withdrawal of everything else — sleep, peace, the ability to touch her own hands without feeling the warmth of someone else's death.

She had unsexed herself. She had asked for it — had stood in the courtyard at Inverness and called upon the spirits that attend on mortal thoughts to unsex her, to fill her from the crown to the toe top-full of direst cruelty, to stop up the access and passage to remorse, to take from her every soft and human thing that might impede the execution of what she and her husband had decided — and the spirits had obliged. They had taken what she asked them to take. But they had not told her — the terms of the bargain had not included this information — that the things they took would not stay taken, that remorse is not a door that can be permanently sealed but a pressure that builds behind the seal until the seal fails, and that the failure, when it comes, is not a leaking but a rupture, not a gradual return of feeling but an inundation, a flood that drowns everything it touches, and the drowning is not metaphorical but is as real and as fatal as any drowning in any water.

*What, will these hands ne'er be clean?*

The rubbing intensified. Her hands worked against each other with a violence that was no longer washing but something else — punishment, perhaps, or the body's attempt to remove the offending parts by friction alone, to wear away the skin that carried the stain until new skin was reached, clean skin, skin that had never touched a bloody dagger or a dead king's servant or a husband's shaking hands in the dark of a stairwell after midnight. But there was no clean skin underneath. The stain went all the way down.

*Here's the smell of the blood still,* she said. *All the perfumes of Arabia will not sweeten this little hand.*

The doctor heard it and understood — not everything, but enough. Enough to know that what he was witnessing was not a disease that admitted of cure, that the condition afflicting this woman was not a thing that herbs or poultices or any instrument in his physician's bag could address. *Unnatural deeds do breed unnatural troubles,* he would say later, and the word *unnatural* would carry in it everything he had inferred from what he had heard — the old man's blood, the Thane of Fife's wife, the hands that would not come clean — and the inference would sit inside him like a stone he had swallowed, indigestible, impossible to expel, dangerous to carry, because knowing what he now suspected was itself a danger in Macbeth's Scotland, where knowledge was proximity and proximity was risk and the safest position was ignorance.

She sighed. The sigh came from somewhere deeper than the lungs — from the place where the woman who had read her husband's letter and decided, in the space between one sentence and the next, that Duncan would die under her roof, still existed in some residual form, still remembered what it had felt like to be the person who made that decision, and still understood, with a clarity that was available only in this state, only in this country between sleep and waking, that the decision had been the first step in a journey whose destination was this corridor, this darkness, this endless washing of hands that would not come clean.

*To bed, to bed,* she said. *What's done cannot be undone.*

The sentence was the exact inversion of the sentence she had spoken on the night of the murder — *what's done is done* — and the distance between those two sentences was the distance she had traveled, the distance between a woman who believed that action ended when the action was completed and a woman who understood that action never ends, that the consequences of a thing done propagate outward the way ripples propagate outward from a stone dropped into water, and that the propagation continues long after the stone has reached the bottom, long after the hand that dropped it has been withdrawn, long after the person who dropped it has forgotten — or tried to forget — that the stone was ever held, ever released, ever dropped.

She returned to bed. Her body carried her back along the corridors it had memorized, back through the doors it could open without sight, back to the room where she slept alone — because Macbeth no longer slept beside her, no longer slept at all in any way she recognized as sleep, had become a creature of the daytime, of the planning and the dispatching and the ruling and the killing that filled his hours, and the bed they had shared was now her bed, only her bed, a bed in which a woman lay and did not sleep and from which a woman rose and walked and spoke and washed and returned to lie again, night after night, in a cycle that was not going to end well and that everyone in the castle knew was not going to end well but that no one could interrupt because interrupting it would have required a power that no one in Macbeth's Scotland possessed, the power to undo what had been done, to unknow what had been learned, to unspill blood that had already been spilled, and that power did not exist.

The doctor left. He told the gentlewoman to remove from her lady's reach anything sharp, anything by which harm might be done — and the instruction was the closest he could come to naming what he feared without naming it, the closest a careful man could come to saying that the woman he had just watched was not recovering from the thing that afflicted her but was approaching its terminus, that the trajectory of her unraveling had a destination, and that the destination was the only exit available to a person who could not stop washing and could not get clean and could not sleep and could not wake and could not live any longer in the space between those states.

He could not help her. He said so. *More needs she the divine than the physician.* The divine — the priest, the confessor, the professional handler of sin and its consequences — but there was no divine who could help here, because the sin was too large for confession and the consequence was too advanced for absolution and the woman was too far gone for anything except the end that was coming, that was already visible in the rubbing of her hands and the fragments of her speech and the sigh that came from the place beneath the lungs, the place where what was left of Lady Macbeth — of the woman who had been Lady Macbeth, who had been a wife and a partner and a force of will so concentrated that it had bent a warrior to its purpose — was preparing to depart.

She would die soon. The play would not show it — would not show the final act of a woman whose first acts it had shown in such detail, would not permit the audience to watch the end the way it had watched the beginning, because the end was private in a way the beginning had not been, was a transaction between a woman and the weight she carried and the means by which she chose to set it down. The report would come to Macbeth later, delivered in the compressed language of a soldier reporting a fact — *the queen, my lord, is dead* — and the five words would contain no information about how or why or in what room or by what method, would contain only the fact itself, the bare irreducible fact, and the fact would land on a man who had once loved her and who would respond not with tears but with the flattest, most exhausted sentence in the language, a sentence about tomorrow and tomorrow and tomorrow, a sentence that was the precise measure of how far he had traveled from the man who had once written her a letter from a battlefield because he could not bear for her not to know what the witches had said, because she was his dearest partner of greatness, because the news was too large to carry alone.

But that was later. Now — in this moment, in this corridor, in this night — she was still alive. She was in bed. Her hands were still. The taper burned on the table where she had set it, its small flame the only light in a room that smelled of lavender and iron, the lavender from the sachets her gentlewoman placed among the linens and the iron from nowhere, from everywhere, from the place inside her where the blood she could not wash away continued to announce its presence, its permanence, its refusal to be undone.

She lay in the dark and she was alone and the aloneness was complete — complete in the way that only the aloneness of a person who has cut herself off from every source of human connection can be complete, because she had cut herself off, had performed the cutting deliberately and methodically in the service of an ambition that had succeeded beyond any reasonable expectation and that had cost everything it was possible to cost, and the success and the cost existed simultaneously, occupied the same space, breathed the same air, and the air was not sufficient for both, and one of them was going to suffocate, and it was not going to be the cost.

He was in the great hall when the cry went up — not a cry, precisely, but a sound that moved through the castle the way cold moves through stone, transferring itself from room to room, from mouth to mouth, arriving at his ears not as words but as a disturbance in the texture of the air, a shift in the quality of the silence that had become the castle's native atmosphere, the silence of a household that had learned to make as little noise as possible because noise attracted attention and attention, in Dunsinane, was not a thing one wanted to attract.

He was armoring. Not literally — the armor was being brought, the reports from the advancing English force were being delivered by men who entered the room with the careful, sidelong approach of dogs that have been kicked before and expect to be kicked again — but in the deeper sense, the sense in which he had been armoring for weeks, months, since the night the ghost had sat in his chair and grinned at him with a face full of wounds, since the night the witches had shown him a procession of kings that stretched to the crack of doom and every one of them wore Banquo's face. He had been putting on layers — not steel but something harder, something that had no name but that functioned the way steel functions, deflecting blows, absorbing impacts, keeping the soft interior safe from the world's sharp edges. The layers had accumulated. He could feel their weight. He could feel, too, the way they had changed his shape, had made him larger on the outside and smaller on the inside, so that the man who lived behind the armoring occupied less and less space, a shrinking tenant in an expanding fortification, until the fortification was nearly all there was and the tenant was nearly gone.

Seyton entered. Or someone entered — a face, a uniform, a pair of eyes that would not meet his — and the face said something, and the something was five words, and the five words were:

*The queen, my lord, is dead.*

He heard them. He heard them the way a man standing at the bottom of a well hears a voice calling from the top — distant, echoing, arriving after a delay that suggested the words had traveled a great distance to reach him, had passed through intervening layers of stone and air and indifference before landing, finally, on the surface of his comprehension, where they sat without sinking, without penetrating, without doing the thing that such words are supposed to do to the person who receives them.

She was dead. His wife was dead. The woman who had read his letter — the letter he had written from the field because the news was too enormous to carry alone, because she was the only vessel in the world large enough to hold what the witches had poured into him — that woman was dead. The woman who had stood in the courtyard at Inverness and called upon the spirits to unsex her, to fill her with cruelty, to stop up every passage to remorse — the spirits had obliged, and then they had collected what was owed, and the collection had been thorough, and now she was dead, and the thoroughness was complete.

He should feel something. He understood this in the abstract, the way a man who has lost sensation in his hands understands in the abstract that the stove is hot — the knowledge is present, the information is available, but the apparatus that converts information into experience has been disconnected, and the disconnection is not temporary but structural, is not a malfunction but a consequence, the consequence of having armored so completely and for so long that the armoring has become the organism and the organism has become vestigial.

*She should have died hereafter.*

That was what came out of him. Not grief. Not rage. Not the sound a man makes when the person he loved most in the world has been taken from him. What came out of him was a scheduling complaint — she should have died later, at a more convenient time, at a time when there would have been space for the dying to occupy, room in the day's itinerary for the business of mourning, a slot between the military dispatches and the fortification reports in which a husband might sit with the fact of his wife's death and feel the thing a husband is supposed to feel. But there was no such slot. The day was full. The English were coming. Birnam Wood stood where it had always stood, ten miles from the castle, and the prophecy said he was safe until it moved, and the prophecy had never lied — had never exactly lied — and so the dying was an inconvenience, a disruption, a thing that had happened at the wrong time, and the wrongness of the timing was the only thing he could feel about it, the only response the armoring permitted.

But something moved beneath the armor. Something shifted in the place where the tenant still lived, the small remaining space where the man who had once written that letter still existed in some residual, attenuated form, and the shifting was not grief — grief requires a self large enough to contain it, and his self was no longer large enough — but it was something adjacent to grief, something in grief's neighborhood, a recognition that the death of the woman in the next room or the room above or wherever she had died — he did not know where, he realized; he did not know where in this castle his wife had died, did not know if she had been in their bed or in the corridor where she walked at night or in some room he had never entered — that this death was the last severance, the final cut in a series of cuts that had been accumulating since the night they had killed Duncan together, hand in bloody hand, and that each subsequent cut had separated him from something — from Banquo, from sleep, from the capacity for fear, from the willingness to consult, from the habit of partnership — and this cut separated him from the last thing, the thing that had been the first thing, the thing that had started everything.

She had been his dearest partner of greatness. He had written that. He had meant it. The words had been true when he wrote them, true in the way that only words written in the heat of a revelation can be true — unguarded, unedited, produced by a mind that has not yet calculated the cost of sincerity. She had been his partner. They had been two people engaged in a single project, and the project had succeeded, and the success had destroyed the partnership the way a fire destroys the house it was lit to warm, and now the house was ash and the fire was still burning and there was nothing left for it to consume except the person standing in front of it, and that person was him, and he was not consumed because he was armored, and the armor held, and the holding was the loneliest thing in the world.

He could hear the sounds of preparation around him — soldiers moving, metal striking metal, the organized clatter of a castle preparing for siege — and the sounds were the sounds of the life he had chosen, the life that remained after everything else had been stripped away, and the life was not a life but an apparatus, a mechanism for the maintenance of power that had no purpose beyond its own continuation, power that protected nothing and served no one and existed only because the alternative to its existence was the acknowledgment that everything — the prophecy, the murder, the crown, the killing and the killing and the killing — had been for nothing, had purchased nothing, had achieved nothing except the transformation of a man who had once been capable of love into a man who could receive the news of his wife's death and respond with a scheduling complaint.

The room was quiet. The messenger had withdrawn. The sounds of preparation continued at a distance, muffled by stone and by the thing that was happening inside him, the thing that was not grief and not rage and not despair but that shared a border with all three, a territory he had never visited and that he was visiting now, alone, in armor that he had put on to protect himself from exactly this, from exactly the thing that was happening, and the armor was holding, the armor was doing its job, and the job was terrible, and the armor was terrible, and the man inside the armor was the most terrible thing of all, because the man inside the armor could not weep.

She was dead. His wife was dead. He would not see her body — would not cross the castle to whatever room contained it, would not look at the face he had looked at across the table at Inverness when Duncan arrived and they had exchanged the glance that contained the entire conspiracy in a single shared second of eye contact, the glance that said *yes* and *tonight* and *together*. He would not touch her hands — the hands that had taken the daggers from him when his own hands would not stop shaking, the hands that had carried the daggers back up the stairs and smeared the grooms and come back down again steady, capable, clean. The hands that had later refused to be clean, that had scrubbed and scrubbed in the darkness while she walked and talked and did not wake. He would not touch those hands. He would stay here, in this room, in this armor, and he would fight the war that was coming, and he would fight it alone, because alone was what he was now, alone was what the project had produced, alone was the final form of the ambition that had begun as a partnership and had narrowed and narrowed until it could be carried by a single person, and that person was him, and the carrying was all that was left.

She should have died hereafter. There would have been a time for such a word. But the time was not now, and the word — whatever word it was, the word for what a man feels when the last human being who knew him as a human being is gone — that word would not be spoken, not now, not ever, because the man who might have spoken it no longer existed, had been replaced by the apparatus, the mechanism, the armored thing that sat on a stolen throne in a besieged castle and received the news of the world's ending with the flat, exhausted patience of a man who had been expecting it, had been expecting it for a long time, and whose only surprise was that it had taken this long to arrive.

Then the messenger came. Another messenger — they were all messengers now, the entire population of the castle had been reduced to the function of carrying news from one room to another, from the battlements to the hall, from the world outside to the shrinking world inside — and this one was pale, and this one was shaking, and this one looked at Macbeth the way a man looks at a thing he is about to break.

"I should report that which I say I saw," the man said, "but know not how to do it."

Macbeth looked at him. The armoring held. The armoring would hold against anything now, because the worst thing had already happened — not the English army, not the siege, but the five words, the queen my lord is dead — and everything after the worst thing was aftermath, was detail, was the ongoing administrative collapse of a world that had already ended. He told the man to say it. Whatever it was. Say it.

The man said it.

Birnam Wood was moving.

He said it differently — said he had looked toward Birnam and the wood had seemed to move, said the trees were coming, said the forest was advancing on the castle — but what he meant, what the words contained when you stripped away the terror and the stammering and the impossibility of what he was describing, was that the prophecy had turned. The thing the apparitions had shown him in the cavern — the armed head, the bloody child, the crowned child carrying a tree — the thing they had promised, the protection they had offered, the impossible condition they had attached to his safety (*Macbeth shall never vanquished be until Great Birnam Wood to high Dunsinane hill shall come against him*) — that condition, which he had received as a guarantee because forests do not walk, because trees do not uproot themselves and march across the Scottish countryside like soldiers — that condition had been met. Was being met. Was meeting itself right now, outside, in the ten miles between the wood and the castle, the distance closing, the trees advancing, the impossible becoming actual in the same way that every impossible thing in this story had become actual: the prophecy fulfilling itself, the witches' words landing where they had always been aimed, and the landing revealing that the words had never meant what he thought they meant, had never been a shield but had always been the blade, and the blade had been traveling toward him since the night on the heath when three shapes in the fog had called him by three names and two of them had been true and the third had been the trap and he had walked into it with his eyes open and his hands reaching and his wife beside him saying *yes* and *tonight* and *together*.

He understood it now. Not gradually, not through stages of reasoning, but all at once, the way a man who has been lost understands all at once that he has been walking in circles — the recognition arriving not as discovery but as confirmation of something he had known for a long time, something the armoring had kept at a distance but that the armoring could no longer keep at a distance because the armoring was the thing the recognition was about. The prophecies were not protections. The prophecies had never been protections. The prophecies were the architecture of the trap — the walls of the corridor down which he had been walking since the heath, the corridor that narrowed and narrowed and that he had mistaken for a passage to safety because the walls were made of reassurance (*none of woman born*, *until Birnam Wood*) and the reassurance had felt solid, had felt load-bearing, had felt like the kind of thing a man could lean his weight against, and he had leaned, and the walls had held, and the holding had been the trick, because the walls were not holding him up — the walls were guiding him here, to this room, to this moment, to the news that the forest was walking and that the guarantee was a lie and that the lie had been visible from the beginning to anyone who was not desperate enough to believe it.

He had been desperate enough. He had been exactly desperate enough. That was the precision of the thing — the witches had calibrated the desperation, had known exactly how much reassurance a man who had murdered his king and his friend and a woman and her children would need to continue functioning, and they had provided exactly that amount, no more, no less, and the provision had worked, had kept him moving, had kept him killing, had kept him armored, and now the provision was being withdrawn and he could see the mechanism for what it was and the seeing did not help because the seeing was part of the mechanism too, was the last stage of the trap, the stage where the animal understands the trap and the understanding changes nothing because the trap has already closed.

He could feel something moving inside the armor. Not grief — grief had failed to arrive and would not arrive now. Not fear — fear had been one of the things the armoring had replaced, one of the sensations the layers had compressed out of existence. What moved inside the armor was something older than grief and simpler than fear: the thing a man feels when he has run out of future, when the road he has been traveling reveals itself to have no further extension, when the next step is the last step and the step after that is empty air. It was not despair. Despair implies the wish for an alternative. This was the absence of alternatives experienced not as suffering but as clarity — cold, total, almost restful — the clarity of a man who no longer needs to calculate because there is nothing left to calculate, no move to consider, no stratagem to devise, no prophecy to interpret, no wife to consult, no future to protect.

The forest was coming. Let it come.

He called for his armor — his actual armor now, the steel, the weight, the thing that went over the body and made it harder to kill. Seyton brought it. Or began to bring it. The buckles were wrong, or Seyton's hands were shaking, or the armor itself resisted, as if the steel knew what it was being asked to do and found the asking absurd — to protect a man who had armored himself so thoroughly on the inside that the outside was redundant, to preserve a body whose tenant had already vacated, to keep alive a king whose kingdom was ten miles of shrinking ground between a walking forest and a castle full of men who served him out of terror and would stop serving him the moment the terror found something more terrible to attach itself to.

He pulled at the straps himself. He shouted — pull it off, no, put it on, bring it — the commands contradicting themselves because the man issuing them was contradicting himself, was two men occupying the same armor, the man who wanted to fight because fighting was the last thing left and the man who wanted to stop because stopping was the only honest response to the recognition that everything — the crown, the killing, the prophecy, the partnership — had been a corridor leading here, to a besieged castle with a dead wife upstairs and a walking forest outside and nothing inside the armor except the residue of a man who had once been Scotland's hero and was now Scotland's disease.

But the fighting man won. The fighting man always won, because the fighting man was the first man, the man who had existed before the prophecy, before the letter, before the partnership, before the witches had given ambition a shape and the shape had consumed everything it touched. The fighting man was the thing at the bottom, the foundation beneath the armoring, the last solid surface, and the fighting man said: arm. Fight. Go out. Meet the forest, meet the army, meet whatever is behind the moving trees and make it kill you face to face, blade to blade, because that at least is something you know how to do, something you were before you were this, and if the prophecies were traps then the traps have sprung and the springing is almost a relief because the waiting is over, the calculating is over, the interpreting is over, and what remains is simple and physical and has the merit of an ending.

He buckled the last strap. He drew his sword. Around him the castle was a hive of noise — men running, metal clashing, the organized panic of a garrison that had just been told the forest was attacking them — and through the noise came the old sound, the sound he knew better than any other sound in the world, the sound of a man preparing to do violence, the sound of breath and steel and the particular silence that lives inside a soldier's chest in the moment before contact, the silence that is not peace but its opposite, the silence of every capacity narrowed to a single point, a single purpose, a single act.

At least they would die with harness on their backs. That was the thing. That was the answer the story had taught him, and it was not the right answer — the right answer was that the prophecies were traps and ambition was a corridor and the corridor led nowhere — but the right answer was useless, was a lesson delivered to a student who no longer had a life in which to apply it, and so he took the wrong answer instead, the answer that said: fight, move, go forward, die in armor rather than in bed, die with a sword in your hand rather than a crown on your head, die as the man you were before you became the thing you are.

He moved toward the door. Behind him, somewhere in the castle, his wife's body lay in whatever room it lay in, and he did not look back, because looking back required a self he no longer possessed, and ahead of him the forest was walking, and the forest was full of men who wanted him dead, and he was going out to meet them, and the meeting would be the last thing he did, and the last thing he did would be the first thing he had been, and there was something in that — not comfort, not redemption, not even irony, but something — and the something carried him through the door and out into the noise and the light and the advancing trees.

He found Macduff in the noise.

Or Macduff found him. It was difficult to tell the difference, because the castle grounds had become a thing without geometry — walls breached, courtyards filling with men wearing branches, the camouflage dropping away as Malcolm's soldiers shed their Birnam boughs and became what they had always been beneath the green disguise, which was an army, which was the end of him — and in the middle of that dissolution of all spatial certainty there was Macduff, and Macduff was not wearing branches, and Macduff was not disguised as anything, and Macduff was looking at him with an expression that Macbeth recognized because he had seen it before, years ago, in his own face, reflected in the polished surface of a shield on a battlefield where he had fought for Duncan, for Scotland, for the thing he had been before he became this — the expression of a man who has found the person he came to kill.

"Turn, hell-hound, turn."

The voice carried no theatricality. No declaration. It was the voice of a man issuing an instruction to a thing that needed killing, the way a man speaks to vermin, the way a man speaks to a disease he intends to cut out of the body it has infected, and the body in this case was Scotland, and the disease was him, and the cutting was about to begin, and Macbeth turned, because turning was what the fighting man did, and the fighting man was the only man left inside the armor.

Macduff's face. He had not looked at Macduff's face closely for — how long? Since the banquet, perhaps. Since the night Banquo's ghost had sat in his chair and Macduff had not come, had sent his absence as a message, had already begun the long work of opposition that had taken him to England, to Malcolm, to the army that was now inside the castle walls. Macduff's face was older than it had been at the banquet. Harder. The face of a man who had received certain news — news about a castle in Fife, news about a wife and a boy and the particular administrative thoroughness with which Macbeth had begun to address the problem of disloyalty — and the receiving had done to Macduff's face what the crown had done to Macbeth's, had stripped it of everything unnecessary, had reduced it to purpose, and the purpose was singular and total and it was standing ten feet away with a sword.

"Of all men else I have avoided thee," Macbeth said, and it was true, he had avoided Macduff in the fighting, had moved through the chaos looking for other opponents, lesser opponents, opponents whose deaths would mean nothing except the prolongation of a battle already lost, because there was a thing inside him that knew — not feared, not suspected, but knew, the way the body knows before the mind that the ground is about to give way — that Macduff was the mechanism by which the last prophecy would resolve itself, Macduff was the instrument the corridor had been guiding him toward, and the avoidance had been the last reflex of a man who did not want to arrive at the place he was already standing.

"But get thee back. My soul is too much charged with blood of thine already."

He meant it. That was the thing that surprised him — he meant it, he did not want to kill Macduff, not because killing had become difficult (killing had become the easiest thing in the world, the only skill he had left, the one competency the corridor had not taken from him) but because Macduff's blood was the one blood that would not add to the armor but would dissolve it, would reach through the layers and touch the thing underneath, the thing he could no longer afford to feel, the thing that lived in the same room as the memory of a woman and a boy in a castle in Fife and the order he had given and the men he had sent and the thoroughness with which the order had been carried out.

Macduff did not get back. Macduff did not speak to the offer. Macduff raised his sword and came forward and the coming forward was its own answer, was the answer Macbeth had known it would be, and the swords met, and the meeting was a sound he knew, the oldest sound, the sound of metal finding metal, and the fighting man inside the armor received the sound like a homecoming.

They fought. The courtyard around them contracted to the space between their bodies — the war, the siege, Malcolm's army, the branches, the castle, all of it receding to the periphery of a world that had shrunk to two men and two swords and the ground between them. Macbeth's body remembered how to do this. His arms remembered. His feet remembered. The fighting was the first language, the one he had spoken before he had learned the language of prophecy, before he had learned the language of conspiracy, before he had learned the language of kingship, which turned out to be the same as the language of murder, which turned out to be the same as the language of loss, and underneath all those acquired languages the first language was still fluent, still precise, still capable of the old articulations — parry, thrust, retreat, advance — and the fluency was the last dignity, the one thing the corridor had not corrupted because the corridor had not needed to, because fighting was not what the corridor was about. The corridor was about ambition, and ambition had used the fighting and discarded it, and now the fighting was all that remained, and the remainder was performing with the terrible competence of a thing that has nothing left to lose.

Between blows, between the spaces where the swords separated and the air rushed in to fill the gap, he spoke. Not to Macduff — Macduff was beyond the reach of speech, Macduff existed entirely inside the purpose his face had announced — but to the prophecy, to the last wall of the corridor, to the final reassurance that still stood between him and the recognition that everything had collapsed.

"Thou losest labor," he said, and he heard his own voice saying it and the voice sounded like a man reciting a prayer he no longer believes but cannot stop reciting because the recitation is the last structure, the last architecture, the last thing holding the shape of the self in place. The words came out, and as they came out he could feel them — not as conviction but as the memory of conviction, the residue of the night in the cavern when the bloody child had risen from the cauldron and spoken and the speaking had been received as a shield and the shield had been leaned against and the leaning had been the trick — and the memory of conviction was not conviction, was its opposite, was the hollow where conviction had been, but he said the words anyway because saying them was the last test, the last experiment, the last time he would hold the prophecy up to the world and see whether the world confirmed it or destroyed it.

Macduff stopped.

Not stopped fighting — his sword was still raised, his body was still coiled in the posture of a man about to strike — but stopped in some other way, stopped the way a man stops when he hears a thing he recognizes, a thing he has been waiting to hear, a thing that changes the geometry of the encounter from uncertain to inevitable. Something moved across Macduff's face. Not triumph — it was too early for triumph, and Macduff was not a man given to premature celebrations. What moved across his face was recognition. The same cold, total recognition that Macbeth had felt when the messenger told him the forest was walking — the recognition that the trap had a shape and the shape was now visible and the visibility changed nothing because the trap had already closed — except Macduff's recognition was the inverse of Macbeth's, was not the recognition of the trapped but the recognition of the one who finds the key that fits the lock he has been standing before, the lock behind which is the thing he came to kill.

"Despair thy charm," Macduff said.

And the voice was level. The voice was the voice of a man delivering information, the way a physician delivers a diagnosis, the way a messenger delivers news, the way a man tells another man a fact that will end him — not with malice but with the precision of a thing that has been carried a long distance for exactly this purpose and is now being set down.

"And let the angel whom thou still hast served tell thee: Macduff was from his mother's womb untimely ripped."

The words arrived.

They arrived the way the messenger's words about the forest had arrived — not gradually, not through stages of comprehension, but all at once, the entire architecture of meaning assembling itself instantaneously in the space between hearing and understanding — and the architecture was a collapse, was the sound of the last wall falling, the last reassurance withdrawing, the last protection revealing itself to have been the last blade, and the blade was not traveling toward him because the blade had already arrived, had arrived in the shape of six words — *from his mother's womb untimely ripped* — and the six words were the lock and the key simultaneously, were the mechanism that closed the trap and the mechanism that explained the trap and the explanation was: *none of woman born* had never meant what he thought it meant, had never meant *no man can kill you*, had never meant *you are safe*, had meant only and exactly what it said, and what it said contained an exception, and the exception was standing in front of him with a sword, and the exception had a name, and the name was Macduff, and Macduff had not been born but had been cut from his mother, had entered the world through a wound rather than a passage, and the distinction — the tiny, technical, surgical distinction between born and removed, between the body's work and the surgeon's knife — that distinction was the hinge on which the prophecy turned, was the gap in the wall through which the blade came, was the space the witches had left open when they built the corridor, the deliberate flaw in the architecture that looked like protection and was designed from the beginning to fail at exactly this point, in exactly this way, against exactly this man.

He felt it — the collapse. Not the armor (the armor held, the steel held, the physical protection remained intact because the physical protection was not the protection that mattered). The inner architecture. The layers. The thing he had built between himself and the recognition that everything he had done since the heath had been done inside a trap, had been performed according to a script he had mistaken for a choice, had been the working-out of a mechanism he had mistaken for a destiny. The layers fell. Not dramatically — no sound, no sensation of impact — but simply, the way a structure falls when the last load-bearing element is removed, the way a wall falls when the foundation beneath it turns out to have been hollow, to have been filled not with stone but with words, and the words were *none of woman born* and the words had been hollow from the beginning and the hollow had been invisible because the words had been wrapped in the shape of reassurance and the reassurance had been the disguise, had been Birnam's branches, had been the camouflage the trap wore to look like safety.

And beneath the collapse — beneath the falling layers, beneath the dismantled architecture, beneath the armor that no longer armored anything — there was the man. Just the man. The man without the prophecy, without the crown, without the partnership (the partnership was dead, was upstairs in whatever room her body lay in, was the thing the *tomorrow* speech had been about), without the reassurance, without the future. The man at the bottom. The fighting man. The first man. The man who had existed before the witches named him and who would exist for approximately the next three minutes and who would spend those three minutes doing the one thing he still knew how to do.

"Accursèd be that tongue that tells me so," he said, and his voice had changed — not louder, not softer, but different in some fundamental register, the register of a man who has stopped negotiating with the world and started simply occupying it, simply standing in it, simply being the thing he is without the mediation of prophecy or ambition or partnership or hope. "For it hath cowed my better part of man."

The better part. He could feel it cowering. The part that had believed. The part that had leaned against the wall and trusted the wall and built a life — no, not a life, a reign, a tyranny, a sequence of murders justified by the wall's solidity — built all of that against the wall, and the wall had fallen, and the building had fallen with it, and the better part of man was the part that understood what the falling meant, which was that none of it had been real, none of the safety, none of the protection, and therefore none of the confidence that had carried him from murder to murder, from Duncan to Banquo to the Macduff family, that confidence had been manufactured by the trap to keep him moving through the corridor, to keep him walking toward this exact moment, this exact courtyard, this exact man with this exact biography, this exact blade.

He could stop. He could drop the sword, kneel, yield, let them drag him before Malcolm and stand trial for what he had done — for the king in his bed, for the friend on the road, for the woman and the child in the castle in Fife — and the standing trial would be its own kind of ending, an ending with words and judgments and the formalities of justice, an ending that acknowledged the human meaning of what he had done and submitted to the human consequences.

He did not stop.

"I will not yield," he said, and the not-yielding was not courage, was not defiance, was not the brave last stand of a hero brought low — it was something simpler and older and less noble than any of those things. It was the fighting man refusing to be anything other than the fighting man, the body refusing to do anything other than what the body knew how to do, the arms and the sword and the feet and the breath committing to the last articulation of the first language because the first language was the only language left and silence was the only alternative and silence was the thing he had been heading toward since the heath and he would arrive at silence soon enough — the silence was three minutes away, maybe less — but he would not arrive at it voluntarily, would not choose it, would not walk into it with his hands empty and his sword on the ground. He would arrive at silence with the sound of metal still ringing in his ears and the weight of a sword still pulling at his shoulder and the old competence still flowing through his muscles, and the arrival would be the last thing he did and the last thing he did would be the first thing he had been and the symmetry of that — the warrior dying as a warrior, the thane ending where the thane began — was not redemption and was not enough and was not the lesson the story had been trying to teach him but it was his, it was the one thing that was authentically his, untouched by prophecy, uncorrupted by ambition, unmediated by the partnership that had made him a king and a murderer and a tyrant and a widower and a man standing in a courtyard with a sword, waiting for the thing the prophecy had always intended to send.

"Lay on, Macduff," he said. "And damned be him that first cries, 'Hold, enough!'"

He raised his sword. Macduff raised his. The distance between them closed.

The fighting was different now. Not better, not worse — different. Stripped of the protection the prophecy had provided, stripped of the certainty that no man of woman born could harm him, the fighting became what fighting actually was: two bodies trying to destroy each other, the outcome uncertain, the skill real but finite, the stamina real but depleting, the sword heavy and getting heavier with each stroke. He could feel his arms tiring. He could feel the years — the years of kingship, the years of sleeplessness, the years of armoring, the years of murdering from a distance through proxies and hired men while the old battlefield muscles softened and the old battlefield reflexes slowed — he could feel all of that now, could feel it the way a man who has been numb feels the cold when the numbness wears off, all at once, everywhere.

Macduff was younger in this. Not in years — Macduff was roughly his contemporary — but in purpose. Macduff's purpose was fresh. Macduff's purpose had been forged in the news from Fife, in the words Ross had carried across the border — *your wife, your children, your servants, all* — and the forging had produced something hard and clean and relentless, something that did not tire because it was not running on stamina but on the fuel that grief becomes when grief is given a target, and the target was here, was in front of him, was tiring, was slowing, was the man who had ordered it, who had sent the murderers, who had killed the wife and the boy and the household, and Macduff's sword carried all of that — the wife, the boy, the household, the news, the grief, the crossing, the army, the branches, the siege, the search through the castle for this one man, this one face, this one neck — and the carrying made the sword faster, made it heavier, made it find the gaps in the armor that the armor had not been designed to close because the armor had

The sword found him.

Not all at once — the body does not surrender its architecture in a single stroke. The sword found him the way weather finds a wall, the way water finds the crack in stone, the way time finds the thing it has been working against: patiently, persistently, through the accumulation of small discoveries — a gap at the shoulder where the plate had shifted, a slowness in the parry that left the left side open for half a breath longer than half a breath should last, a step backward that landed wrong, that put the weight on the heel instead of the ball, that turned the retreat into a stumble and the stumble into an opening and the opening into the place where Macduff's blade arrived.

The first wound was at the arm. It was not fatal. It was informational — a message delivered through steel that the body's defenses had begun to fail, that the old reflexes were no longer fast enough to close the distances the old reflexes needed to close. Blood came, and the blood was ordinary, was red, was the same blood that had been on his hands the night he came back from Duncan's chamber holding two daggers he had forgotten to leave behind, the same blood he had ordered spilled on a road outside the castle when the murderers found Banquo riding home, the same blood that had pooled in a castle in Fife around bodies too small and too bewildered to understand why the men had come. The blood was the same. It had always been the same. The distinction he had maintained — between his blood and theirs, between the blood of the charmed king and the blood of the things the charmed king killed — that distinction dissolved in the space between the cut and the pain, and the dissolution was quiet, was merely a fact, was the body's way of saying what the prophecy had already said and Macduff had already confirmed: *you are not protected. You were never protected. You are flesh, and flesh opens, and what opens bleeds, and what bleeds can die.*

He swung. The sword still knew how to swing. The muscles still fired in the old sequence — shoulder, arm, wrist, the rotation of the hip that sent the force down the blade's length and into the air where Macduff's body had been a half-second ago and was no longer, because Macduff moved the way grief moves, which is without hesitation, which is with the directness of a thing that has already lost everything it feared losing and therefore has no fear left, only the clean mechanical trajectory of intent.

The courtyard. The castle. Dunsinane. The war going on around them — Malcolm's men inside the walls now, the last of the garrison surrendering or dying or dropping their weapons and stepping back from the thing they had been defending because the thing they had been defending was not a kingdom, had never been a kingdom, had been a man's ambition given the shape of governance and the governance had been the disguise and the ambition had been the disease and the disease was here, in this courtyard, bleeding from the arm and swinging a sword that connected with nothing.

Macduff came again. The blow caught the breastplate and the breastplate held but the force behind it did not dissipate, traveled through the steel and into the ribs and the ribs received it the way a wall receives the blow that will eventually bring it down — not breaking, not yet, but remembering the blow, storing the damage, adding it to the accumulation of stored damages that the body had been cataloguing since the fight began.

He staggered. Caught himself. Swung again, and this time the sword found something — found Macduff's shield, found the edge of it, skidded along the surface and caught the rim and the impact traveled up Macduff's arm and Macduff's arm absorbed it and did not waver, did not retreat, did not acknowledge the blow as anything more than the cost of proximity to the thing he had come to kill.

Around them the castle continued its capitulation. Doors opening. Men emerging with their hands visible and empty. The banner — his banner, the banner that had replaced Duncan's banner the day the crown was placed on his head at Scone — the banner was being pulled down from the tower. He could not see it but he could feel it, could feel the symbolic architecture collapsing in parallel with the physical architecture of his body, the kingdom unmade in the same minutes the king was being unmade, the two dissolutions synchronized because they had always been the same dissolution, had always been one event distributed across two registers — the political and the personal, the crown and the man who wore it, Scotland and Macbeth.

The second wound was at the thigh. The leg buckled. Did not fail — the leg was too stubborn to fail, was the leg of a man who had marched across battlefields, who had stood for hours in armor, who had carried the weight of steel and kingship and murder and sleeplessness for years without folding — but buckled, bent at the wrong angle, turned the stance from solid to provisional, from a man fighting to a man trying to remain standing while fighting, and the trying was visible, was legible, was the thing that crossed Macduff's face not as triumph but as recognition that the end was near, that the body before him was approaching the limit of what bodies can sustain.

He thought of her. Not deliberately — the thought arrived the way the wounds arrived, through a gap in the defenses he had not known was there. He thought of her upstairs in whatever room her body lay in, the body that had held his hand the night they killed Duncan, the hands that had taken the daggers from him and carried them back to the chamber and smeared the grooms with blood, the hands that later could not stop washing themselves, that scrubbed at nothing in the dark while a doctor and a gentlewoman watched and the watching was all that could be done because the stain was not on the hands, had never been on the hands, was inside, was in the place where the ambition and the partnership and the marriage had lived before the ambition consumed the partnership and the partnership consumed the marriage and the marriage consumed her. She was upstairs. She was dead. She had arrived at the silence before him, had walked into it without armor, without a sword, without the fighting man's refusal to stop fighting, had simply — and here the thought broke apart because he did not know how she had died, did not know the specifics, knew only that she was dead and that the dead do not wait but the dead are patient and that patience was the only thing left of her.

He would be with her soon.

The thought was not comfort. It was geometry — the simple spatial recognition that the distance between the living and the dead was closing, that each wound Macduff opened was a shortening of that distance, that the interval between *here* and *where she was* was measured not in miles but in minutes, perhaps seconds, and the seconds were few.

Macduff struck again. The blow caught the sword — not Macbeth's body but Macbeth's sword — and the angle was wrong, was a leverage Macbeth's wrist could not counter because the wrist was tiring and the grip was loosening and the blood from the arm wound had reached the hand and the hand was slick and the slickness was the body's betrayal, the body's final commentary on the enterprise the body had been recruited into the night it carried a dagger up the stairs toward Duncan's chamber — *this is what the blood does, this is where the blood ends up, on your hands, always on your hands, and the hands cannot hold what the hands have done* — and the sword twisted, not free of his grip but wrong in it, turned the wrong way, opened the guard the way a door opens when the bolt fails, and through the opened guard Macduff's sword came.

The third wound was the wound that mattered.

It entered below the breastplate, in the gap between the steel and the belt, the gap that every suit of armor carries because armor is designed by men and men cannot close every opening, cannot protect every surface, cannot anticipate the exact angle at which the blade will arrive — and the blade arrived at the angle the gap permitted, which was the angle the gap had always permitted, which was the angle that had been waiting inside the armor's design since the armor was forged, the flaw that was not a flaw but a fact, the fact that no protection is total, that every wall has a gap, that every prophecy has an exception, that every charm has a term, and the term was now, and the now was the blade, and the blade was inside him.

He felt it. Not as pain — pain would come, or would not come, it no longer mattered which — but as arrival. The arrival at the place the corridor had been leading to since the heath. Since the three figures in the fog. Since *All hail, Macbeth, that shalt be king hereafter.* Since the letter to his wife. Since her voice in the torchlit chamber — *unsex me here* — and the voice had been answered, and the unsexing had been performed, not on her but on both of them, on the marriage, on the partnership, the ambition unsexing them of everything that had been human in the bond until what remained was the mechanism, and the mechanism had run, had operated, had produced its outputs — a dead king, a crown, a sequence of murders, a reign, a tyranny, a madwoman washing her hands in the dark, a man in a courtyard with a sword in his body — and now the mechanism was stopping, was winding down, was arriving at the silence it had been generating since the beginning.

His knees found the ground. The ground was stone. The stone was cold. The coldness was the first purely physical sensation he had experienced in — how long? Since before the heath, perhaps. Since before the witches. Since the time when the body's sensations were the body's own, unmediated by prophecy, uncolored by ambition, uncontaminated by the knowledge that every sensation was occurring inside a trap. The cold stone under his knees was just cold stone. It meant nothing. It signified nothing. It was the ground, and he was on it, and the being-on-it was the body's last statement about the nature of the body, which was that the body is heavy and the body falls and the falling is not metaphor but physics, not poetry but gravity.

Macduff withdrew the blade. The withdrawal was precise, professional, the withdrawal of a man who had fought in battles and knew how swords behaved inside bodies and knew that the withdrawal was not cruelty but completion, was the final step in the sequence that began when Ross brought the news across the border and Macduff fell to his knees in Malcolm's court and Malcolm said *dispute it like a man* and Macduff said *I shall do so, but I must also feel it as a man* — and the feeling had been felt, and the feeling had been converted to purpose, and the purpose had been carried north across the border and into the castle and through the corridors and into this courtyard and into this body, and the purpose was now complete.

Macbeth looked up. The sky above Dunsinane was gray, was the same gray it had been the morning Duncan arrived at Inverness, the same gray it had been the morning they found Duncan's body, the same gray it had been every morning of the reign because Scotland's sky is gray and the grayness is not symbolic, is not the pathetic fallacy, is simply the weather, and the weather does not care who is king and who is dying and who is already dead.

He did not speak. The time for speaking was finished. The soliloquies were done — the dagger speech, the *tomorrow* speech, all the language he had produced to mediate between himself and the thing he had become, all the eloquence that had made the becoming bearable, that had turned murder into poetry and tyranny into philosophy and despair into a five-stress line about sound and fury — all of it was finished, and what remained was the silence, and the silence was not a tale told by an idiot, was not sound and fury signifying nothing, was simply the absence of speech, which is what death is, which is where speech goes when the speaker stops.

Macduff struck the final blow.

The body that had defended Scotland fell. The body that had murdered Duncan fell. The body that had ordered Banquo killed on the road and the Macduff family killed in their castle fell. The body that had held Lady Macbeth's hand and received Lady Macbeth's courage and carried Lady Macbeth's ambition until the carrying broke them both — that body fell, and the falling was not dramatic, was not slow, was not cinematic, was the ordinary mechanical event of a body that has been deprived of the mechanisms that keep bodies upright, and the deprivation was total, and the body met the stone, and the meeting was final.

Macbeth was dead.

The courtyard received the fact the way courtyards receive all facts: in silence, in stone, in the indifferent architecture of a place that had existed before him and would exist after him and would carry no memory of what had happened on its surface because stone does not remember and memory is the province of the living and the living were elsewhere — were inside the castle, surrendering; were outside the castle, in Malcolm's army, sheathing their swords; were upstairs, in a chamber where a woman's body lay on a bed and the body was cold and the coldness was the same coldness as the stone and the sameness was not meaningful, was not a connection, was simply the temperature of things that have stopped being alive.

Macduff stood over the body. His breath came in the heavy rhythm of a man who has exerted himself past the point of ordinary exertion and is now standing in the aftermath, in the space between the action and its meaning. He looked down at the thing on the ground — the armor, the blood, the face that had been a thane's face and then a king's face and then a tyrant's face and was now simply a dead man's face, which is no face at all, which is the face's renunciation of the project of being a face — and what moved across Macduff's features was not satisfaction. Not relief. Something closer to completion. The completion of a task that had cost everything it could cost and had returned only itself, only the fact of its own completion, which was not enough and would never be enough because the wife was still dead and the boy was still dead and the household was still dead and no amount of killing the man who killed them would unkill them, would return them from the place where they had gone, the place where Macbeth had now followed them, the place where Lady Macbeth had preceded them all.

The sword was heavy in Macduff's hand. The sword had done what the sword was brought to do. The war was over. The tyrant was dead. Scotland was liberated, or would be, in the minutes and hours that followed — the formal surrender, the securing of the castle, the finding of Malcolm, the presentation of proof, the rituals by which a kingdom acknowledges that one chapter has ended and another is beginning.

Macduff bent down. His hands, steady now, did what remained to be done — the grim practical work that would serve as evidence, as proof, as the thing he would carry to Malcolm to show that the work was finished. The head. The crown's last vessel, severed from the body that had carried it to the throne and carried it into battle and carried it into this courtyard where the carrying ended.

He lifted it, and turned from the courtyard, and walked toward the sound of Malcolm's army assembling, and behind him the body lay on the stone in the gray light, and the gray light did not change, and the stone did not change, and the silence that had come to the courtyard remained, and the remaining was the thing that silence does, which is to fill the space where sound was, where speech was, where the voice of a man who had been a hero and a husband and a murderer and a king had been, and the filling was complete, and the completeness was the end.

The sky above Scone was clear.

Not the gray that had hung over Dunsinane, not the fog that had covered the heath the day three figures appeared and disappeared and appeared again in the space between the thunder and the silence after the thunder. The sky above Scone was clear in the way that skies are clear after storms — not the absence of weather but the aftermath of it, the atmosphere having exhausted its capacity for disorder and arrived, through exhaustion, at stillness. The clouds had broken. The light that came through was thin, was winter light, was the pale exacting light of a Scottish morning that illuminates without warming, that shows everything and forgives nothing, that falls on stone and skin and steel with the same disinterested precision.

The lords gathered.

They came from the corridors and the courtyards, from the fields where the army had camped, from the roads that led north and south and east and west across a kingdom that had spent years contracting under the weight of a reign that was not a reign but an occupation, a single man's ambition given the apparatus of governance and the apparatus turned against the governed. They came in armor and in court dress, in the stained clothing of men who had marched and fought and in the cleaned clothing of men who had waited and calculated and chosen the right moment to declare what they had privately known for months or years — that the king was not a king, that the crown was not a crown, that the thing on the throne was the thing the throne existed to prevent. They came, and the coming was itself the restoration, the simple physical fact of men arriving at the place where kings are made, arriving voluntarily, arriving because the arriving was the oldest form of consent, the body's presence standing for the body politic's assent, and the assent was not coerced, was not purchased, was not extracted through the intermediary of murderers dispatched to roads and castles — the assent was given, freely, in the open air, under a sky that had no fog in it.

Macduff had brought the head. It had been displayed — the grim certificate, the proof that the mechanism had stopped, that the body which had carried the usurpation was no longer a body but an object, no longer a king but evidence that a king had been and was no longer. The display was brief. The lords looked and the looking was enough. The thing was removed. What replaced it was the ceremony, which is to say: the words, spoken in the proper order by the proper voices in the proper place, because ceremony is the technology by which a kingdom tells itself what it is, and what Scotland was telling itself now was that it remembered — remembered the order that had existed before the disorder, remembered the succession that had been interrupted, remembered Duncan and the line of Duncan and the son of Duncan who had fled south the night his father's blood was found on the grooms' hands and the grooms' daggers and the grooms' faces and everywhere except where it actually belonged, which was on the hands of the man and woman sleeping — or not sleeping, never again sleeping — in the chamber down the hall.

Malcolm stood where his father's thanes could see him.

He was young. The youth was visible not as weakness but as fact — the fact that time had passed, that the boy who fled to England was now a man who had returned with an army, that the flight which had looked like cowardice was revealed now as the thing it had always been, which was survival, which was the necessary precondition for return, which was the patience that tyranny cannot outlast because tyranny is urgent and patience is not. He stood in the place where Macbeth had stood the day Macbeth was crowned, and the standing-in-the-same-place was not irony but correction — the same stone, the same hall, the same ceremony, but the man was different, and the difference was the point, was the entire point, was the thing the war had been fought to establish: that it matters who stands here, that the person matters, that the crown is not a hat.

Ross was there. The surviving thanes were there. The men who had served Macbeth and then stopped serving Macbeth and then served Malcolm were there, and their presence was not hypocrisy but the ordinary pragmatism of men who live inside political structures and must navigate the transitions between one structure and the next, and the navigation was itself a skill, was the skill that keeps kingdoms functioning across the deaths of kings, and the skill was in operation now, quietly, in the way men positioned themselves in the hall, in the way they arranged their faces, in the way they spoke to one another in the low careful voices of men who have survived a catastrophe and are now engaged in the work of establishing that the catastrophe is over.

The crown was placed.

Not the same crown — or perhaps the same crown, it did not matter, crowns are instruments, are the visible registration of an invisible agreement, and the agreement was what mattered, and the agreement was: this man, this son, this heir, this Malcolm, is king of Scotland, and the being-king is not seized but conferred, is not taken but given, is the gift of the governed to the governor, the oldest transaction in the political grammar of the kingdom, and the transaction was completed here, now, in this hall, under this sky, in this light.

Malcolm looked out at the assembled faces. He knew what they knew: that the ground they stood on was soaked in the consequences of the last time a king was made at Scone. He knew that the women were dead — the wife who had washed her hands in the dark, the wife and son who had been slaughtered in a castle in Fife. He knew that Banquo was dead on a road somewhere, and that Fleance was alive somewhere else, and that the prophecy — if prophecy was the word for what the three figures on the heath had spoken — the prophecy had said things about Banquo's line that had not yet resolved and might never resolve or might resolve in ways that no one present could foresee. He knew these things. He carried these things. He would govern with these things inside the act of governing, the way all kings govern with the knowledge of what governance has cost and will cost and cannot stop costing.

He spoke. The words were the words of a new king — the formal distribution of gratitude, the naming of thanes as earls, the summoning home of the exiled, the acknowledgment of debts that would be paid in the currency of titles and lands and the restored trust of a kingdom that had learned what the absence of trust looked like. The words were adequate. They were not poetry. They were not the five-stress lines of a man whose eloquence had been the most beautiful thing about his damnation. They were the plain, practical, necessary words of administration — the language of a king who understood that the first task of kingship is not to be interesting but to be legitimate, not to be eloquent but to be present, not to fill the air with sound and fury but to stand in the hall and be seen standing and let the standing speak.

The lords answered. The answer was the oldest answer: the voiced assent, the collective acknowledgment, the hall's agreement that the man before them was the man who should be before them. The answer traveled through the stone and into the air and the air carried it outward, past the walls, past the fields, into the country that would receive it the way countries receive the news of new kings — with relief or suspicion or indifference or hope, depending on where you stood and what the old king had done to you and whether the new king's words would become the new king's actions or would remain, as words so often remain, merely words.

But here, in this hall, the words were enough. Here, the ceremony completed its work. Here, the crown sat on the head it was meant to sit on, and the head bore it, and the bearing was the beginning.

Outside, the sky held. No thunder. No fog. No figures appearing and disappearing at the edge of vision, chanting in trochees about the interchangeability of fair and foul. The heath was somewhere to the north, and the heath was empty, and the emptiness was not ominous but was simply the condition of a place where nothing was happening, where the supernatural had withdrawn or had never been or had finished its business and moved on to other corridors, other thanes, other ambitions waiting to be named and given a path. The heath was empty and Scone was full, and the fullness was order, and the order was fragile — order is always fragile, is always the temporary and provisional arrangement of forces that could at any moment rearrange themselves into chaos — but the fragility was not a flaw. The fragility was the point. The fragility was what made the ceremony necessary, what made the gathered lords necessary, what made the crown necessary: because order must be chosen, must be enacted, must be performed again and again in halls like this one by men like this one, and the performance is not pretense but practice, is the practice by which a kingdom remains a kingdom instead of becoming the thing it becomes when a man with a dagger walks up the stairs toward a sleeping king and the king's blood unmakes the world and the world must be remade.

The world had been remade.

Malcolm was king of Scotland. The lords stood in their places. The light came through the windows and fell on the stone floor and the stone floor held it the way stone holds everything — without comment, without memory, without preference for the feet that crossed it now over the feet that had crossed it before. The hall was full of the living. The dead were elsewhere — were in the ground, were in the past, were in the silence that the living had paused to acknowledge and were now, with the ordinary cruelty of continuation, leaving behind.

The ceremony ended. The kingdom began.

## Save-the-Cat beat sheet (the 15-beat structure this renders)
Macbeth, a victorious Scottish thane, is told by three witches that he will be king. Spurred by the prophecy and by his wife, he murders the good king Duncan in his sleep and takes the crown — and then must kill and kill again to hold a throne the same prophecy said would pass to another man's line. His wife unravels into madness and death; he hardens into a tyrant with nothing left to feel; and the prophecy's promised protections turn out to be the trap that destroys him.

The fifteen beats in order (every scene below is mapped to its beat (AUTHORED)):
- [1] Opening Image: the heath; thunder; three witches appear and disperse before any human has entered the play. The world's first register is supernatural disorder — weather, omens, ambiguity — setting the tone for everything the play's human action will be judged against
- [2] Theme Stated: the Witches chant 'Fair is foul, and foul is fair' — the play's thematic claim stated in its opening minutes by non-protagonist voices. Everything that follows will enact this inversion: the loyal warrior becomes the tyrant, the gentle queen becomes the murderer's accomplice, the prophecy's protection becomes the trap
- [3] Set-Up: Macbeth is introduced as Scotland's hero — the thane who has just won Duncan's war for him. Duncan is the good king, generous to a fault. The marriage is established: Macbeth writes home immediately upon hearing the prophecy. The status quo is a capable warrior loyal to a kind king, married to an equal partner. Everything the play will break is introduced as intact
- [4] Catalyst: the Witches deliver the first prophecy: thane of Glamis, thane of Cawdor, king hereafter. Within minutes, the Cawdor half is confirmed by royal messengers — the prophecy has already partly come true. The status quo is disrupted not by an external event (a knock at the door) but by an internal event (ambition named and given a path)
- [5] Debate: Macbeth's hesitation meets Lady Macbeth's pressure. 'If it were done when 'tis done, then 'twere well it were done quickly' — the debate rendered as concrete dramatic question (should I kill the king under my own roof?), answered through his wife's iron counter-arguments. The debate is both spouses' first joint project
- [6] Break Into Two: Macbeth commits: 'I am settled, and bend up each corporal agent to this terrible feat.' The choice is crossed. Everything from here is the new world — the world in which this thane is going to murder his king
- [7] B Story: the marriage surfaces as the play's moral engine just after the threshold. Lady Macbeth handles logistics; Macbeth's resolve wobbles; her 'unsex me here' earlier in the act is cashed here as practical command. The B story is the marriage's full conspiratorial unity — the moment the partnership is most effectively one
- [8] Fun and Games: Duncan is murdered in his sleep; Lady Macbeth smears the grooms with blood; the sons flee; Macbeth is crowned. This is the 'promise of the premise' sequence — the prophecy's first half has literalized. Macbeth navigates the new world (kingship) without yet fully bearing its costs. But the dagger speech and 'Macbeth shall sleep no more' hint the costs are already being accrued
- [9] Midpoint: Macbeth crowned; the new court established; false victory. The stakes raise and the A and B stories collide: the prophecy also named Banquo's line as future kings, which means the prize Macbeth has seized is temporary. From here, every action will be to protect a throne whose loss is already foretold
- [10] Bad Guys Close In: Banquo murdered (Fleance escapes); the banquet ghost breaks Macbeth's public composure; he returns to the Witches for the second prophecy. Pressure mounts — from the supernatural (the ghost, the new prophecies), from the political (Macduff's absence becomes suspicion), from the internal (Macbeth's sleep gone, his wife's influence waning). The Macduff family is slaughtered offstage: the moral nadir
- [11] All Is Lost: Lady Macbeth dies offstage — her sleepwalking was the last sign of the self that ambition had unmade; her death (cause unspecified, suicide implied) is the rock bottom. The B story's protagonist is gone. Macbeth is alone with what he has become
- [12] Dark Night of the Soul: 'Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow' — Macbeth's response to news of his wife's death. Not grief, exhaustion; not despair, deflation. Life is a tale told by an idiot, full of sound and fury, signifying nothing. The despair before the insight — except the insight that arrives is not redemption but recognition of meaninglessness
- [13] Break Into Three: news arrives that Birnam Wood is moving toward Dunsinane — the second prophecy's first 'impossible' protection has literalized against Macbeth. He arms himself anyway. The answer is found: the prophecies were traps, not shields. He commits to the final approach — not with the lesson the story has taught, but in defiance of it ('at least we'll die with harness on our back')
- [14] Finale: Macbeth faces Macduff in single combat. He boasts of the prophecy's last protection ('none of woman born shall harm Macbeth'); Macduff reveals the Caesarean birth ('from his mother's womb untimely ripped'); the last protection collapses. Macbeth fights anyway and is killed. The A and B stories resolve together — the political plot by the tyrant's death; the marriage plot by the husband joining the wife he lost
- [15] Final Image: Malcolm crowned at Scone. The lords gather; the rightful succession is restored; the kingdom names its new king. Mirrors and inverts the Opening Image — where Act 1 opened on supernatural disorder and an absent king, the play closes on political order and a present, acknowledged king. The world has changed by being restored

## Strands (the A and B stories — weave both)
- a-story: the political arc: loyal thane receives prophecy, murders his king, seizes the throne, rules as tyrant, is overthrown by the rightful heir. Scotland's natural succession is broken and then repaired. The external stakes are the kingdom's moral order and its subjects' safety under a usurper
- b-story: the marriage arc: a partnership that begins in shared ambition, unifies around the conspiracy to kill Duncan, collaborates through its aftermath, then isolates as Macbeth acts without his wife, then ends in her death and his 'tomorrow' soliloquy. The marriage carries the theme: the more ambition succeeds, the less of the marriage remains
