The heat of the Amalfi coast struck him before the harbour smell did — that particular union of salt, pitch, and rotting oranges that no French port could counterfeit. Antonio Bologna stood at the rail as the galley warped alongside, watching the white facades climb the cliff-face like a disease of marble, and felt the old pull of the place settle into his bones. A year in France. The French king's horsemanship, the French king's tilting-yard, the orderly administration of a court where accounts were kept in something better than hope and fabrication — all this had refined him, he supposed, or at least sharpened a dissatisfaction he had carried blunt before departure.

Delio was waiting on the quay. That was like Delio: to know the ship's arrival before the harbour-master, to stand in precisely the spot where the gangplank would drop, wearing the expression of a man who has been there only a moment though he has been there an hour.

"Bologna." The embrace was brief and genuine. "You look French."

"I look sunburnt. The crossing was filthy." Antonio shifted the strap of his travelling case. "Is the court at Amalfi still, or has it migrated?"

"Still. Very much still." Delio fell into step beside him as they climbed the first of the narrow streets, the cobbles slick with fish-water and the condensation of heat. "The Duchess keeps her household here. She has not removed since the Duke her husband died."

Antonio said nothing to this. He had heard of the Duke of Amalfi's death in France — a wasting sickness, the rumour ran, that had eaten the man by degrees until there was nothing left to inter but a title and a widow.

"She governs well," Delio continued, in the tone of a man measuring his words against the walls. "Better than the old Duke, if one is honest about it — which one is not, publicly, because she has brothers."

"I remember the brothers."

"Then you remember enough. Ferdinand, Duke of Calabria, is her twin — mark that, her *twin* — and loves her as twins love, which is to say excessively and with an eye to possession. The Cardinal is the elder, and a churchman of the kind that makes the word *churchman* taste of iron filings. Between them they bracket her like bookends on a shelf, and the volume they hold upright is her widowhood. They would have her remain so."

"That is her concern, not theirs."

Delio glanced sideways. "You have been in France."

They passed through the lower gate and into the courtyard of the Duchess's palace, where the fountain played its usual comedy of abundance in a country of thirst. Servants moved in the arcades. Antonio recognised some of them — the steward's office had been his before his departure, and a steward learns faces the way a usurer learns debts, by the weight of what is owed behind each one.

"She has kept the post open for you," Delio said, as if reading the thought. "Your books are where you left them. Your ink, I suspect, has dried."

"The post of steward?"

"The post. The accounts. The management of her revenues, which are considerable and which, in your absence, have been managed by a committee of men who cannot add. She asked after you twice in the autumn. I would say she values your service."

Antonio stopped at the edge of the courtyard, where the shadow of the arcade met the hammer of the sun, and looked up at the gallery windows. Somewhere behind that façade a young widow governed a duchy under the surveillance of a duke and a cardinal, both of whom called her sister, both of whom — if Delio's tone was any index — regarded her freedom as a disorder to be cured.

He had served her before. He would serve her again. The books and the revenues and the careful architecture of a great house's accounts — these were things he understood, things that answered to diligence. Whatever else stirred in the palace of Amalfi, above and beyond the management of its household, was not his province.

"Come," he said to Delio. "Let me present myself before my ink dries further."

They crossed the courtyard together, into the shadow of the arcade, and the gate behind them admitted the noise of the harbour and then closed it out.

Ferdinand watched his sister before she knew he was watching. That was the privilege of arriving unannounced — you caught the world in its natural posture, before it arranged itself for your inspection.

She was in the long gallery, standing at a table spread with estate papers, speaking to one of her officers about a drainage ditch or a boundary wall or some other piece of provincial business that would have bored him in a sentence but which she attended to with the quality of attention he associated with falconers and poisoners — patient, specific, lethal in its focus. The afternoon light from the gallery windows caught the side of her face, and Ferdinand thought, as he often thought: we shared a womb. The same face, nearly. The same jaw, the same colouring, the same quick darkness in the eyes. What was handsome in him was beautiful in her, and the injustice of this had never entirely stopped rankling.

The Cardinal was already seated in the gallery's far alcove, having arrived an hour before Ferdinand by the expedient of travelling faster and caring less about the dust on his vestments. He sat with one leg crossed over the other, turning a ring on his finger, watching the Duchess with the expression of a man calculating a sum he already knows the answer to.

Ferdinand stepped into the gallery. The officer saw him first and went pale — men tended to — and the Duchess turned.

"Brother." No surprise in her voice. She had trained surprise out of herself early, or perhaps it had never been native to her. She came forward and offered her hands, and he took them, and held them, and looked at her face the way one looks at a property whose title one holds but whose tenancy one cannot quite control.

"Sister. You look well. Widowhood agrees with you."

"Grief is a good complexion," she said, withdrawing her hands with the precise minimum of delay that courtesy permitted. She had seen the Cardinal already; she turned to acknowledge him with a nod that carried the warmth appropriate to a brother who was also a prelate — which was no warmth at all, merely the correct angle of the head.

"We are all here, then," the Cardinal said from his alcove, not rising. "A family reunion. How wholesome."

Ferdinand moved through the gallery with the restlessness that was his particular affliction — he could not hold still in a room, could not occupy one position when three were available. He touched the edge of the table where the estate papers lay, glanced at a drainage map without seeing it, turned back to the Duchess.

"We will not stay long. We came to see how you keep yourself."

"I keep myself as I have kept myself these two years. Governing."

"Governing," the Cardinal repeated, as though tasting the word for rot. "Yes. We hear good reports of your government. Your revenues are well managed, your tenants content, your household ordered. All very admirable. We did not come to speak of government."

The Duchess said nothing. She stood very still in the centre of the gallery, and Ferdinand felt the particular frustration she always provoked in him — that stillness, that quality of waiting without yielding, as though she were a wall against which one could throw anything and find it merely absorbed.

"You are young," Ferdinand said. He had not meant to begin there, but the words came out with the directness that was either his virtue or his disease, depending on who received them. "You are young, and you are a widow, and you are — " He stopped. The word *beautiful* had risen in his throat and he swallowed it back, not because it was untrue but because it was not the sort of truth a brother should be heard to utter. "You are not without attractions that will draw suitors."

"I have had no suitors."

"You will. That is the point." He moved closer. "A lusty widow, sister — the word is not mine, it is the world's — a lusty widow is a thing that draws men as a candle draws flies. And the flies that come are not the sort one wants in one's house."

The Cardinal unfolded himself from the alcove and stood. When he stood, the room rearranged itself around him; it was a quality of his office or his person, Ferdinand had never determined which. "What my brother means," the Cardinal said, with the precision of a man who has spent decades translating passion into policy, "is that we would counsel you against remarriage."

"Counsel," the Duchess said.

"We would *tell* you not to remarry," Ferdinand corrected. He did not like the Cardinal's diplomatic diction; it gave her room to manoeuvre. "We would instruct you. We would *forbid* you, if the word did not sound too much like a father and too little like a brother — but the authority is the same, sister, whatever name you give it."

She looked at him, and Ferdinand saw in her eyes the thing he could never govern — a quality of private judgement, as though behind that gaze there existed a court of appeal to which his arguments would never be admitted.

"I will not hear this twice," she said quietly.

"Then hear it once and remember it." Ferdinand took her hand again — seized it, this time, rather than received it — and held it between both of his. Her fingers were warm. "If you remarry — if you take any man to your bed — you disgrace our blood. The Aragon blood. You mingle what should remain pure with whatever common stuff presents itself, and the stain of it will never wash. Do you understand me? I am speaking of blood."

"You are speaking of something," she said, and there was a note in her voice — not defiance, exactly, but a refusal to be frightened that functioned as something worse than defiance. It functioned as indifference.

The Cardinal stepped closer. "Sister. We do not speak from malice. We speak from interest — yours as well as ours. The reputation of a widow who remarries is a glass that, once cracked, shows every face that looks into it as deformed. We would preserve you from deformity."

Ferdinand released her hand. He could feel the heat in his own face, the familiar rising pressure that Delio, had Delio been present, might have called fury and that Ferdinand called love. "If I could find a way to keep you as you are," he said, "I would build a wall around this palace and let no man pass through the gate. But I cannot wall you in, and so I must trust your honour — and your obedience."

"You may trust what you like," the Duchess said. "I have told you I will not hear this twice."

She left the gallery. The door closed behind her with no more force than was necessary to close a door, and Ferdinand stood looking at it, and the Cardinal stood looking at Ferdinand, and neither spoke for a time.

"She will do as she pleases," the Cardinal said at last.

"Then she will find out what it pleases me to do in return," Ferdinand said. His voice was very quiet, quieter than it had been when he was speaking to her, and the quietness was the part that should have frightened anyone listening. "I will not lose her to some — " He stopped again. The sentence had too many possible endings, and none of them were the sort of thing one said aloud, even to a brother who was a cardinal and therefore professionally acquainted with the unsayable.

The Cardinal picked up his gloves from the alcove and drew them on, finger by finger. "We have delivered our warning. What she does with it is her affair — until it becomes ours."

Ferdinand did not answer. He was still looking at the closed door, at the space where his sister had been, and he was thinking — with the particular intensity of a twin whose other half has just walked away from him — that the face behind that door was his own face, rearranged, and that whatever she did with her body she did, in some sense he could feel but not articulate, with his.

The courtyard was empty at that hour, which was the hour Ferdinand had specified, which told Bosola everything he needed to know about the nature of the conversation before a word of it was spoken. Honest business kept regular hours. The other kind kept this kind — the grey interval between vespers and supper when the household was occupied with its stomach and the corridors belonged to rats and men who resembled them.

Bosola had been waiting for eleven minutes. He had counted. Counting was a habit of the galleys — you counted strokes, you counted days, you counted the lesions on your hands until the lesions became too numerous and too ordinary to merit arithmetic. He had served the Cardinal's purposes and received the Cardinal's silence in return, and the silence had lasted seven years, which was the length of a galley sentence and also, he had been told, the interval required for every cell in a man's body to replace itself. He was, by that accounting, an entirely new man composed of entirely old grievances.

Ferdinand came through the archway with no attendant, which was the second thing that told Bosola what was coming. Princes travelled with entourages the way saints travelled with haloes — the absence of one suggested either extraordinary humility or extraordinary sin, and Ferdinand did not look humble.

"Bosola."

"Your Grace."

Ferdinand stopped at a distance that was not conversational. It was the distance at which one inspected a horse or a weapon — close enough to assess, far enough to avoid being bitten. His eyes moved over Bosola with the quick, acquisitive attention of a man who has already decided to purchase but wants to confirm the merchandise is not visibly damaged.

"You served my brother," Ferdinand said. "The Cardinal. You did — what was the word he used? — a *piece of service* for him, some years ago."

"I did." Bosola felt the old anger stir in his throat, familiar as bile. "And was rewarded with a suit of chains and a place at the oar. Your brother's gratitude has a particular texture, my lord. It chafes."

"You are bitter."

"I am accurate. Bitterness is accuracy that has not been paid for."

Ferdinand smiled. It was not a pleasant expression — it was the smile of a man who has found exactly the tool he was looking for, rusted and ill-used and therefore cheap. "I have a place for you," he said. "The provisorship of the horse, here at my sister's court. It is a good office. It carries income, title, the authority to move freely through her household. You would be — established."

Bosola said nothing. He understood the architecture of the offer: the provisorship was the visible structure, load-bearing and respectable, but there was a second structure beneath it, and it was the second structure that Ferdinand was selling.

"In return," Ferdinand continued, and now his voice dropped to the register Bosola associated with confessionals and contracts — the register in which men said what they actually meant, "you would watch. You would observe my sister's household, her movements, her — associations. You would report to me what you observe. Privately. Regularly. With the fidelity of a man who understands that his comfort depends upon his employer's satisfaction."

There it was. The word had not been spoken — *intelligencer*, *spy*, the cruder terms that cruder men might use — but the shape of the thing was plain enough, and Bosola looked at it the way one looks at a toad on a dinner plate: with recognition, with distaste, and with the certain knowledge that one is going to eat it anyway, because the alternative is to go hungry.

"You want me to spy on the Duchess."

"I want you to *attend* to the Duchess. The word you use is your own affair."

"The word I use is the honest one, my lord. The work is dishonest enough without dressing it in better clothes." Bosola heard himself and marvelled, as he often marvelled, at his own capacity for moral commentary in the act of moral surrender. He was the devil's preacher — he could identify sin with theological precision and then commit it with professional competence. It was a gift. It was a disease. The distinction between the two had ceased to interest him somewhere around the fourth year of his galley sentence.

Ferdinand reached into his doublet and produced a purse. He did not offer it — he held it at his side, visible but unconceded, the way one holds a treat before a dog whose obedience one is not yet certain of.

"The provisorship pays well," Ferdinand said. "My private gratitude pays better. And my gratitude, Bosola, unlike my brother's, does not express itself in chains."

Bosola looked at the purse. He looked at Ferdinand's face, which was the Duchess's face rearranged into something harder and more angular and infinitely more dangerous. He thought of the galleys. He thought of the seven years of new cells that had replaced his old ones and left him, against all biological promise, exactly the same man — cynical, capable, hungry, and entirely willing to do what he despised for the compensation of being allowed to despise it aloud.

"I am your creature," Bosola said. The word tasted the way he expected it to taste — like iron, like oar-wood, like the particular flavour of a man swallowing his own contempt and calling it employment.

Ferdinand handed him the purse. Their fingers touched in the exchange, and Bosola noted that the Duke's hand was hot — feverishly hot, as though something inside the man were burning at a rate that could not be sustained indefinitely.

"Go to Amalfi," Ferdinand said. "Take up the provisorship. Be visible. Be trusted. And write to me."

He turned and left through the archway, and Bosola stood alone in the courtyard with the weight of the purse in his hand and the weight of the commission in his gut, and he thought: I know what this is. I have always known what this is. A man who has served one brother's purposes and been broken for it now accepts the other brother's purposes with his eyes wide open and his objections fully articulated and his will to refuse entirely, perfectly, catastrophically absent.

He pocketed the purse. The coins shifted against each other with the sound of small bones settling.

She had dismissed the household by degrees, the way one empties a room of candles — extinguishing each presence singly so that the growing dark might seem a natural thing, an accident of evening, rather than what it was: an architecture of privacy constructed with the precision of a woman who understood that what she was about to do required no audience and exactly one witness.

Cariola she had positioned behind the arras. Not hidden — *placed*, the way a notary is placed at the signing of a contract, visible to the law if not to the other party. Cariola had looked at her with an expression the Duchess recognized as the particular face her waiting-woman wore when loyalty and judgement were pulling in opposite directions, and the Duchess had said only, "Stay. Listen. Whatever you hear, remember it," and Cariola had gone behind the heavy cloth with the obedience of a woman who has served long enough to know when questions will not be answered because the answers have not yet been made.

The Duchess stood alone in her chamber. Her brothers' warning sat in her memory like a stone in a shoe — present, irritating, impossible to ignore, impossible to determine whether one walked differently because of the stone or because one had always intended to walk this way and the stone merely made the walking conscious. Ferdinand's face when he had spoken: *They are most luxurious will wed twice*. The Cardinal's cooler iteration of the same prohibition, theological where Ferdinand's had been visceral, both arriving at the identical conclusion — that her body, having been married once and widowed, was now their property to dispose of or to leave fallow, as they preferred. She had smiled at them. She had nodded. She had felt, beneath the smile, something she could not name with certainty — something that was either love or defiance or the calm recognition that a contract in a chamber, per verba de praesenti, was absolute marriage by every authority she acknowledged, and that the authorities she acknowledged did not include her brothers.

She could not have said which of these it was. She suspected it was all of them at once, braided together so tightly that the separation of one strand from another would destroy the rope entire. She did not attempt the separation. She had a use for the rope.

Antonio entered when summoned. He came with the steward's posture she had catalogued over months — the slight forward inclination of a man whose profession was attendance, whose body had learned to angle itself toward the needs of others the way a compass needle angles toward north. He carried a ledger, because she had mentioned accounts, because the pretext required a pretext, and she noted the way his hands held the book: carefully, with the grip of a man who understood that the objects entrusted to him were not his own.

"Set the ledger down," she said.

He set it down. His face held the neutral attentiveness of the good servant, and she thought: I am about to dismantle that. I am about to take the careful architecture of his deference and pull it apart board by board, and I do not know whether what I will find underneath is what I need to find.

"Antonio. If I were to speak to you — not as your duchess, not as your employer, but as —" She paused. The sentence had several possible endings and she was aware of each of them, fanned out before her like cards, and she did not know which one she would select until she selected it. "As a woman with a proposal."

His face changed. It was a small change — the careful neutrality did not break so much as *shift*, the way a wall shifts in an earthquake, maintaining its shape but revealing, in the new alignment of its stones, that the foundation beneath it has moved.

"Your Grace —"

"Do not *Your Grace* me. Not now. I am removing that, for the duration of this conversation. I am removing rank the way one removes a glove — you will see the hand beneath it, and the hand is the thing that matters."

She watched him absorb this. She watched the steward's calculus operating behind his eyes — the rapid assessment of risk, of propriety, of the distance between what was being said and what was being meant. He was, she knew, an intelligent man. She had chosen him for his intelligence, among other qualities, and among the other qualities was something she had observed in the way he moved through her household: a kind of fundamental straightness, not rigidity but *rectitude*, the quality of a line drawn without a ruler that nevertheless arrives where it intended.

"I have heard lawyers say," she said, and her voice was steady because she had decided it would be steady, "that a contract in a chamber, per verba de praesenti, is absolute marriage." She let the sentence sit between them. It sat the way a key sits on a table — small, metallic, unambiguous in its function. "Do you understand what I am saying to you?"

"I understand what the words mean," Antonio said. His voice had lost the steward's careful modulation; it was his own voice now, stripped of professional inflection, and it was — she noted this with the attention she gave to all things that mattered — slightly rough, as though something in his throat had caught. "I do not understand why you are saying them to me."

"Because I am saying them to you. Because you are the man I am saying them to. That is the whole of the reason, and it is either sufficient or it is not, and I would like you to tell me which."

The room was very quiet. Behind the arras, Cariola was a held breath, a presence the Duchess felt the way one feels a draught from an unseen window — something there, something listening, something that would serve, when the time came, as proof that what was spoken in this room was spoken and was meant.

Antonio looked at her. She could see him measuring the distance between them — not the physical distance, which was the width of a small table and a ledger, but the other distance, the one her brothers would have called insuperable, the distance between a duchess and a steward, between blood and service, between what the world permitted and what she was proposing. She could see him measuring it and she could see, in the movement of something behind his eyes, the moment he decided the measurement did not matter, or mattered less than something else.

"It is sufficient," he said.

She took his hand. His fingers were warm and dry and she noted this the way she had noted everything about him — with a precision that was either love or strategy or the simple exercise of a woman's sovereign will over her own future, and she did not distinguish between these because the distinction, if it existed, was not hers to make. The lawyers said the contract was absolute. The contract required words, and a witness, and the mutual consent of two people who understood what they were doing.

She understood what she was doing. She understood it the way one understands a step taken off a ledge — completely, irrevocably, with the full knowledge that the air beneath will either hold or it will not, and that the decision to step was made before the stepping and cannot be retrieved after it.

"Then I take you," she said, "for my husband."

"And I you," he said, "for my wife."

The words were plain. They were as plain as words could be — unornamented, unwitnessed except by the woman behind the arras who would remember them, legally binding by every canon the Duchess acknowledged, and treasonous by every canon her brothers would apply. She held Antonio's hand and felt the contract settle over them like a garment — close, warm, binding, invisible to anyone who was not wearing it.

Behind the arras, Cariola exhaled. The sound was very small. It was the sound of a woman who had just become the sole witness to an act that was either a marriage or a catastrophe or both simultaneously, and who understood, with the clarity of the serving class, that the difference between the two would be determined not by the act itself but by whether the Duchess's brothers ever learned of it.

The Duchess did not look toward the arras. She looked at Antonio, whose face held an expression she had not seen before — something open and undefended and entirely, terrifyingly his own — and she thought: *This is mine. I have made this. Whatever comes of it, I have made it, and it is valid, and my brothers' faces when they warned me are the faces of men who believed their authority extended to places it does not reach.*

Whether this thought was love, or defiance, or the calm assertion of canonical principle, she did not examine. The ring she placed on his finger was her own — removed and given, the way one transfers a title, the way one signs a deed. The metal was still warm from her hand when it settled onto his.

The room held them in its silence the way a church holds the echo after the last word of a sacrament — not empty but *full*, the quiet a substance rather than an absence, pressing against the walls, the arras, the closed door, the two bodies standing with their hands still joined across the width of a small table and a ledger no one would open tonight.

The Duchess felt the marriage the way she imagined one felt a wound before the pain arrived — as a fact of the body, established and irreversible, not yet translated into sensation. She was married. She had been married before, to the old Duke, and she remembered that ceremony as a public architecture of brocade and incense and witnessed signatures, an event that had happened to her in the way weather happens to a landscape — implacably, from without. This was not that. This had happened from within. She had built it with her own mouth, her own hand, her own ring now circling a finger that was not hers, and the difference between the two marriages was the difference between a room one is led into and a room one constructs around oneself while standing in it.

Antonio had not released her hand. She did not think this was because he had forgotten he was holding it; she thought it was because he understood, as she did, that the contact was itself a kind of evidence — that their joined hands were the outward sign of an inward contract, and that releasing them would not undo what had been done but would begin the long work of concealing it, and neither of them was ready yet to begin that work.

"We are married," she said. She said it not because he did not know — his face told her he knew, the open undefended expression still there, still entirely and terrifyingly his own — but because the words needed to be spoken a second time, declaratively, in the indicative mood rather than the performative. The first speaking had *made* the marriage. This speaking *named* it, and the naming was for all three of them: for herself, for Antonio, for the woman behind the arras whose breathing the Duchess could hear now, shallow and deliberate, the breathing of someone trying to take up as little space as possible in a room where something enormous had just occurred.

"Cariola," the Duchess said.

The arras moved. Cariola emerged with the expression the Duchess had anticipated — not shock, precisely, but the careful blankness of a woman who has witnessed an act she cannot undo and is already calculating the weight of it, already sorting the knowledge into the two categories that would govern her life from this moment forward: what she knew, and what she must appear not to know.

"You heard," the Duchess said.

"I heard, Your Grace."

"Then you are my witness. You are the witness to a marriage contracted per verba de praesenti between two consenting parties, valid by the law of the Church and binding before God." She heard her own voice and noted its quality — clear, precise, juridical, the voice of a woman who was also a ruler and who understood that even acts of love required documentation if they were to survive the scrutiny of men who wished them annulled. "You will remember what was said. You will remember the words and the order in which they were spoken and the fact that both parties spoke freely and without coercion. Do you understand what I am asking you to carry?"

Cariola looked at her. Then at Antonio, whose hand the Duchess still held, whose ring — *her* ring — caught the candlelight with the dull warmth of gold that has been worn long and is already familiar with skin. Then back to the Duchess.

"I understand, Your Grace." A pause, brief, weighted with everything the woman would not say. "I will carry it."

The Duchess released Antonio's hand. The separation was deliberate — not reluctant but *chosen*, the way one sets down a document after signing it, not because one wishes to be parted from it but because the signing is complete and the document must now be placed somewhere safe, somewhere it cannot be tampered with. Antonio's hand remained where she had left it for a moment, suspended in the air between them, before it returned to his side with the careful control of a man who was learning, in this first minute of his marriage, that every gesture he made would henceforth carry a double meaning: the meaning it bore in the world that knew, and the meaning it bore in the world that did not.

Three people. The number sat in the Duchess's mind with the clarity of an equation. Three people knew. Her brothers did not know. Bosola — the new provision-master her brother had installed with the solicitousness of a man hanging a lantern in someone else's window — did not know. The household did not know. The courts of Calabria and Rome did not know. The whole machinery of Italian political alliance, which treated a widow-duchess's body as a counter to be placed on the board by the hands of her male relations, did not know. Three people, in a room, in the dark, with a contract that was valid by every authority she recognized and void by every authority that had the power to destroy her.

She looked at Antonio. His face had begun its reconstruction — the steward's composure reassembling itself over the exposed thing beneath, stone by careful stone, and she understood that this was not dishonesty but survival, the necessary work of a man who would walk out of this room and resume his attendance in her household as though nothing had changed, because everything had changed and therefore nothing could appear to have changed, and the distance between these two facts was the space in which their marriage would have to live.

"Go," she said to him. Her voice was gentle and absolutely steady. "Go to your chambers. Tomorrow you are my steward. Tonight —" She stopped. She did not finish the sentence. The unfinished sentence hung between them like the ring: small, warm, wholly understood.

He bowed. The bow was the steward's bow, the familiar inclination she had catalogued over months, but it carried now a second thing inside it, a private thing, the way a letter carries its meaning inside the formality of its folding. He left. His footsteps receded down the corridor with the measured pace of a man who was not hurrying because hurrying would be noticed, and what could not be noticed could not be known, and what could not be known could not be punished.

The Duchess stood in her chamber with Cariola. The candles burned. The ledger sat on the table, unopened, its columns of accounts as irrelevant now as the terms of a lease on a building that has been rebuilt from the foundation up, every wall in a new place, every room serving a different purpose, the exterior unchanged.

"Your Grace," Cariola said. Her voice was the voice of a woman standing at the edge of something she could see but could not measure. "Your brothers —"

"My brothers," the Duchess said, "are my brothers. They are not my confessors, nor my sovereigns, nor the arbiters of canon law. What was done tonight was done lawfully, and what was done lawfully need not be confessed to men who would make it unlawful by the sole authority of their displeasure."

Cariola said nothing. Her silence had the particular quality of a silence that contains an argument too dangerous to speak — the argument that the Duchess's brothers' displeasure was not an abstraction but a force, a thing with agents and instruments and a reach that extended well beyond the walls of this room and the ears of its three witnesses.

The Duchess heard the unspoken argument. She heard it clearly, as one hears a bell whose sound one has been expecting. She chose not to answer it. She chose, instead, to undress for bed, to take down her hair, to perform the ordinary actions of the evening as though the evening were ordinary, as though the ring on her steward's hand were not also a key, as though the locked thing it opened were not also, by every calculation her brothers would eventually make, a tomb.

She did not think this. She thought: *I am married. I have done what I intended. The law supports me and God witnesses me and three people in this room know what is true.* 

Whether three was enough — whether three could hold against the weight of what did not know — was a question she set aside the way one sets aside a letter one is not yet ready to read. The letter would keep. The marriage was made. The concealment had already begun.

She knew before she was certain. The body speaks first in a language older than confirmation — a heaviness low in the abdomen that was not illness, a sensitivity in the breasts that was not soreness, a faint metallic taste at the back of the tongue in the mornings that she remembered from the first time, years ago, with the old Duke, when her body had done the same thing and she had not understood what it was doing until the physicians named it for her. She did not need physicians now. She had been this once before, and the body does not forget its own grammar.

She stood at the window of her private chamber and counted weeks. The counting was mechanical, precise, the arithmetic of a woman who understood that her body was now a calendar as well as a kingdom, that the two durations — the private reckoning and the public observation — would diverge with increasing obviousness as the months advanced, and that the distance between them was the same distance in which her marriage lived: the space between what was true and what could be seen.

Three months. Perhaps a little more. She had attributed the first missed course to the strain of concealment itself — the daily work of being a wife in a room and a widow in a court, of touching Antonio's hand over a ledger and releasing it before the gesture became legible, of sleeping beside him in the hours when the household slept and waking before the household woke, of carrying the marriage inside her body the way one carries a swallowed stone, invisible and constant and pressing against everything. She had thought the strain might account for it. It did not account for it. Nothing accounted for it except the thing itself, which was growing inside her with the mute insistence of all biological fact, indifferent to her calculations, indifferent to her brothers, indifferent to every structure of concealment she had built around her marriage — because the marriage could be hidden but the child could not, the child was the marriage made flesh, and flesh takes up space, and space can be observed, and what can be observed can be reported, and what can be reported can reach Calabria, can reach Rome, can reach the ears of men who had forbidden this very eventuality with the specificity of men who understood that a widow's pregnancy was not a rumor but a proof.

She pressed her hand flat against her stomach. Nothing yet. The body was still her own in its outward line — the gowns still fell as they had fallen, the waist still held, the silhouette that moved through the receiving rooms and the chapel and the public corridors of Amalfi was still the silhouette of a duchess-widow whose grief was decorous and whose conduct was irreproachable. But she felt the change beneath the surface the way one feels the first fracture in a wall that still stands — a hairline thing, invisible to everyone who is not pressing their hand against it, but real, structural, the beginning of a rearrangement that could not be reversed.

She told Antonio in the evening, in the chamber, with the door locked and the arras pulled and the candles burning at the low, close height they used for private hours. She told him with the same clarity she had used when she proposed the marriage — declarative, factual, the syntax of a woman who had learned that the surest way to speak dangerous truths was to speak them plainly, without the embroidery of apology or the padding of circumlocution, because embroidery and padding were forms of doubt, and she could not afford doubt, not now, not with the fact of the child already establishing itself inside her with an authority that outstripped her own.

"I am with child," she said.

Antonio's face did what she had learned it did when it received news it had both hoped for and feared — it opened and closed in the same instant, the joy and the terror arriving simultaneously, neither one masking the other, the two expressions coexisting on his features the way two notes coexist in a dissonance: each fully itself, the combination something neither could produce alone. He reached for her hand. She gave it.

"How long?" he asked.

"Three months. Perhaps more."

He was quiet. She watched him perform the same calculation she had already performed — the arithmetic of months, the geometry of a body that would expand beyond the capacity of any gown to disguise, the calendar of a pregnancy set against the calendar of a court and a household and a provision-master who had been installed in that household for the express purpose of observing precisely this kind of alteration. She saw him arrive at the answer she had already arrived at, and she saw that the answer frightened him not because he lacked courage but because he understood, as she did, that courage was not the relevant faculty. The relevant faculty was architecture — the ability to construct, around a fact that grew larger every day, a concealment that grew larger with it, without the concealment itself becoming visible. It was a problem of engineering, and neither of them was an engineer.

"Cariola will need to know," the Duchess said.

"Cariola already knows," Antonio said. His voice had the quality of a man stating something he had observed rather than something he had been told, and the Duchess understood that he was right — that Cariola, who dressed her, who attended her in the mornings when the metallic taste sat on her tongue and the heaviness pressed low against her spine, who had been a woman long enough to read the semaphore of another woman's body, had already performed her own reckoning and arrived at her own conclusion and was waiting, with the particular patience of a waiting-woman who has been made a witness and understands that witnessing is a form of bondage, for the Duchess to confirm what Cariola's eyes had already confirmed.

She confirmed it that night. Cariola received the news with the expression the Duchess had anticipated — not surprise, not fear, but the careful, compressed look of a woman adding a new weight to a burden she was already carrying, a woman recalculating the load and finding that it was heavier than she had prepared for but not heavier than she could bear, not yet, not if the structure held, not if the three of them — three, still only three — maintained the architecture they had built around the marriage and extended it, now, around the child.

"Your Grace will need different lacing," Cariola said. It was a practical statement, the kind of statement a woman makes when the practical is the only register in which she can speak without trembling. The Duchess understood this. She understood that Cariola's competence was itself a form of loyalty — that the woman was offering the only thing she had to offer, which was skill, which was attention, which was the willingness to dress a secret in the clothing of the ordinary and send it out into the world looking unremarkable.

"Yes," the Duchess said. "Different lacing."

Three people knew. The number had not changed. But the thing they knew had changed — had doubled, redoubled, taken on mass and duration and a terminus that could be calculated to the week. The marriage had been a fact that could live indefinitely in concealment, weightless, invisible, a thing of words and a ring and the memory of three witnesses. The child was not that. The child was a fact with a body, and the body would arrive, and when it arrived it would need to be hidden or explained, and explanation was the end of concealment, and concealment was the only wall between her marriage and her brothers' comprehension of it.

She lay in the dark beside Antonio and felt the child — not movement, not yet, but *presence*, the mute thereness of a life that did not know it was secret, did not know it was dangerous, did not know that the room it was growing in was also, by every calculation the Duchess could not stop herself from making, a room whose walls were watched.

She set the calculation aside. She had set it aside before. She was becoming skilled at setting it aside — the letter she was not yet ready to read, the question of whether three could hold, whether the architecture could extend, whether the concealment could outpace the thing it concealed. The letter would keep. The child would keep. The body would do what the body did, and she would dress it in the morning and carry it through the public rooms and answer for it to no one, because she was the Duchess of Amalfi still, and the Duchess of Amalfi answered to no one, and the distance between that principle and the truth of her situation was a distance she could not yet afford to measure.

He had been watching for weeks before he understood what he was watching for.

The commission from Ferdinand had been precise in its object — observe the Duchess, report what you observe — and imprecise in its expectation, which was the way of princes who suspected everything and specified nothing, who hired eyes and then complained about what the eyes had seen. Bosola had taken the post of provision-master because the post was offered and because a man who has rowed galleys does not refuse an office that comes with a roof and a title, however provisional the title and however damp the roof. He had taken it knowing what it was. A provision-master provisions. An intelligencer watches. He was both, and the doubleness sat in him like a meal he could not digest — present always, neither nourishing nor poisoning, simply *there*, a heaviness in the gut that attended every interaction with the household, every inventory of flour and linen, every morning when he walked the corridors of Amalfi and understood that the corridors were not corridors but sightlines, and the sightlines were not his own but Ferdinand's, extended across the distance between Calabria and Amalfi through the borrowed apparatus of Bosola's attention.

He had watched. He had seen nothing. The Duchess moved through her court with the composure of a woman who had nothing to conceal, which was either the truth or its most accomplished counterfeit, and Bosola, who had spent years in the company of men who lied for their survival, could not determine which. She received petitions. She heard suits. She walked in the gallery with Cariola at her side and spoke in the low, unhurried cadence of a woman whose days were orderly and whose nights were her own. Antonio Bologna kept the household accounts with the scrupulous neutrality of a steward who understood that his position depended on the appearance of competence and nothing else. The two of them occupied the same rooms and did not look at each other with any frequency or duration that exceeded the ordinary commerce of a duchess and her officer.

And yet.

There was something. Not a thing he could name — not a glance intercepted, not a word overheard, not the kind of evidence that could be folded into a letter and dispatched to Calabria with the confidence of a man reporting fact. It was subtler than that. It was architectural. The household had shifted its geometry in a way that Bosola could feel but not prove — a slight redistribution of proximity, a recalibration of who stood where and when, the kind of adjustment that a building makes when a new weight has been introduced to its structure and the beams have quietly accommodated themselves to the load. Cariola attended the Duchess more closely than before. Antonio's presence in the private wing had acquired a regularity that was not quite explicable by the accounts. The Duchess herself had altered — not in manner, not in speech, but in the way she carried herself through space, a fractional change in posture that Bosola noticed because noticing was his profession and because the body, unlike the tongue, cannot be taught to lie with perfect consistency.

The apricots arrived from Naples in early June, a crate of them, still green at the shoulder and flushing gold along the cheek, packed in straw that smelled of the orchard and the road. Bosola had not ordered them. He had arranged for them. The distinction mattered: an order was a record, a line in the household ledger that Antonio would read and note; an arrangement was a conversation with a particular merchant, a particular sum paid from Bosola's own purse — Ferdinand's purse, in truth, the money indistinguishable — and a particular instruction regarding delivery. The apricots were to arrive as a gift. Provenance vague. The kind of unsolicited offering that a provisioner might present to his mistress as evidence of his attentiveness to her comfort, the way a dog brings a bird to the door and lays it down and waits to see if the offering is received.

He knew what apricots did. Every midwife in Naples knew what apricots did. The fruit was notorious among women who were with child — it provoked the stomach, quickened the blood, and in a woman whose body was already engaged in the labour of gestation, it could produce an effect so immediate and so unmistakable that no composure, however practised, could entirely suppress it. The knowledge was common. The application of the knowledge was Bosola's.

He brought them to her in the gallery, mid-morning, when the light came in from the south windows and laid itself across the floor in long rectangles and the household traffic was at its most ordinary. He brought them on a dish. He brought them with the manner of a provision-master performing his office — solicitous, slightly obsequious, the posture of a man whose ambitions were contained within the boundaries of fruit and flour and the daily management of a noble household. He had cultivated the manner carefully. It was not false. It was *partial*, which was worse than false, because a false manner could be detected and a partial manner could not — it was true in every particular and dishonest only in what it omitted, which was everything.

"Your Grace," he said. "The first apricots of the season. I thought they might please."

She looked at the dish. She looked at the apricots. Something moved across her face — not alarm, not recognition, but a flicker of appetite so involuntary and so immediately suppressed that Bosola would have missed it if he had not been watching for precisely that flicker, the way a hunter watches for the tremor in the grass that is not wind.

"You are attentive, Bosola," she said. Her voice was even, pleasant, the voice of a duchess accepting a courtesy from a servant.

"It is my office to be attentive, Your Grace."

She took one. He watched her take it. He watched her hand close around the fruit with a directness that was itself information — not the delicate selection of a woman choosing among pleasures but the purposeful grip of a woman whose body had made a demand and whose hand had obeyed before her judgement could intervene. She bit into it. The juice ran. She ate it with a concentration that she seemed unaware of, her attention folding inward toward the act of eating in a way that excluded the gallery and the light and Bosola and everything that was not the fruit and the body consuming it.

She ate a second. Then a third. On the third she paused — mid-bite, the flesh of the apricot still between her teeth — and something changed in her colour. It was not dramatic. It was the kind of change that registers only to an observer who has stationed himself at the correct distance and angle, who has arranged the encounter for this purpose, who is reading the surface of a woman's face the way a physician reads the surface of urine in a glass — for hue, for shift, for the small betrayals of the body's interior condition. She went pale. Not white — pale, the blood withdrawing from the surface in the particular pattern of a stomach turning, of a nausea arriving not from illness but from the exquisite sensitivity of a body that was already occupied, already working, already devoting its resources to a project that did not welcome interruption.

She set the apricot down.

"I am — a little warm," she said. Her hand went to her throat. "The gallery is close today."

"Shall I open a window, Your Grace?"

"No. I will retire for a moment. Cariola."

Cariola was there — had been there, Bosola noted, closer than a waiting-woman customarily stood, positioned not beside the Duchess but slightly behind and to the left, the placement of a woman ready to intercept rather than attend, the geometry of someone who expected the need to intervene. Cariola took the Duchess's arm with a swiftness that was itself an argument. The two of them moved toward the private apartments with a pace that was not hurried and not leisurely but precisely calibrated to convey the impression of ordinary withdrawal while covering the distance as quickly as dignity permitted.

Bosola stood in the gallery with the dish of apricots and watched them go.

He did not smile. Smiling was an indulgence, and indulgence was a form of exposure, and a man who had survived the galleys understood that exposure was death in all its forms — physical, political, professional. He did not smile. But he felt the information settle into place inside him with the satisfaction of a mason setting the final stone in an arch — the satisfaction not of completion but of structural confirmation, the knowledge that the shape he had suspected was in fact the shape that was there, that the weight the household had been quietly accommodating was the weight he had hypothesized, that the Duchess of Amalfi, widow, whose brothers had extracted from her a promise of perpetual mourning with the specificity of men who understood what a widow's body could do if left ungoverned, was with child.

He did not know by whom. The *by whom* was a separate architecture, a separate investigation, and Bosola was disciplined enough to keep the questions distinct. The pregnancy was one fact. The father was another. The two facts were related but not identical, and the letter he would compose to Ferdinand need not conflate them. Report what you observe. He had observed.

He carried the dish back to the kitchen. He scraped the half-eaten apricot into the waste. He washed the dish himself, because a dish washed by the provision-master was a dish that passed through no other hands, and hands were witnesses, and witnesses were leaks, and leaks were the failure of the only craft Bosola possessed, which was the craft of watching without being seen to watch, of knowing without being known to know, of standing in a gallery with a dish of summer fruit and reading in the colour of a woman's face the thing her brothers had hired him to find.

The letter would go tonight. The road to Calabria was three days. Ferdinand would know within the week.

Bosola returned to his accounts. The provision-master provisions. The intelligencer watches. The apricots were noted in the ledger as a gift to the household, unremarkable, the kind of entry that Antonio Bologna would read and pass over without suspicion, because Antonio Bologna, whatever else he was — and Bosola was beginning to develop hypotheses about what else he was — did not yet understand that the household's provisions and the household's surveillance were the same activity, conducted by the same hand, funded by the same purse, and aimed at the same woman, who was now in her private chamber, attended by Cariola, suffering the predictable consequences of early-season apricots on a body that was doing exactly what Bosola had suspected it was doing.

He dipped his pen. He began the letter. *My Lord—*

The ink was black. The news was worse. He wrote it anyway, because that was what he had agreed to do, and a man who has agreed to do a thing and does it is at least consistent, which is the nearest thing to virtue that a man who has rowed galleys and eaten the bread of princes can reasonably expect of himself, and which is, in any case, more than Ferdinand deserved, and more than the Duchess would forgive, and more than Bosola — alone now, the gallery empty, the dish clean, the nausea confirmed, the letter forming itself in the plain, unsentimental hand of a man reporting another person's secret to the person who had paid to know it — could entirely bear.

The child came in the small hours, when the palace was at its most still and the stillness was itself a kind of accomplice.

Antonio had been standing in the antechamber for what felt like the duration of a season — standing, because sitting implied a composure he did not possess, and pacing implied an agitation he could not afford to display, and so he stood, occupying the narrow ground between those two impossibilities, his hand on the back of a chair he did not use, listening to the sounds that came through the door that separated him from the room where his wife was doing the thing that wives did and that he could not help with and that terrified him in a manner so complete it had ceased to feel like terror and had become instead a kind of stupefied attentiveness, every nerve drawn taut and pointed toward the door like a compass needle toward north.

He was not supposed to be here. A steward had no business in the private apartments at this hour. The household understood Antonio Bologna as a man of regular habits — early to his ledgers, punctual at meals, visible in the public rooms during the hours when visibility was expected, and absent thereafter to whatever modest quarters a steward occupied, which the household imagined were modest because the household had not been told otherwise and because Antonio had been meticulous, scrupulous, relentless in maintaining the fiction that his quarters were his quarters and his life was his life and neither had anything to do with the rooms behind the painted door at the end of the corridor where the Duchess of Amalfi slept. He had come tonight by the back passage, the one that ran behind the tapestries in the gallery and opened into the private wing through a door disguised as panelling — the door she had shown him on the night of their marriage, laughing at its ingenuity, her hand on the hidden latch, her face bright with the pleasure of a woman revealing a secret to the only person entitled to share it. He had used it so many times now that his hand found the latch without thought, the way a hand finds a familiar banister in the dark, and the passage had become so routine that he sometimes forgot it was a deception and imagined it was simply the way a man came home.

Tonight the passage had not felt routine. Tonight he had moved through it with the awareness that on the other side of the panelling there was a woman in labour and the woman was his wife and the labour was his child and the entire event was occurring in silence — deliberate, enforced, necessary silence — because the child whose arrival Antonio was awaiting could not be heard to arrive, not by the household, not by the guards, not by the provision-master whose office was two floors below and whose attention, Antonio had begun to suspect, was not entirely contained by the boundaries of flour and linen.

Cariola came through the door. Her sleeves were pushed above her elbows and there was a darkness on her forearms that Antonio understood was blood and chose not to examine further. Her face was drawn but not alarmed — the face of a woman performing hard work competently, which was Cariola's face in all circumstances, the unshakeable pragmatism of a servant whose loyalty had been tested so thoroughly and so continuously over the past months that it had ceased to be loyalty and had become something closer to architecture, a structural element of the household without which the entire edifice would have collapsed months ago.

"A boy," she said.

The word entered Antonio like a change in pressure — not a sound but a force, a redistribution of everything inside him, as though his organs had been rearranged by a hand he could not see and set down again in a new configuration that was the same as the old but meant something entirely different. A boy. He had a son. The steward of Amalfi had a son, and the son was also the son of the Duchess of Amalfi, and the two facts existed simultaneously in the same child, in the same room, in the same silence that Antonio was now required to maintain because the joy that was mounting in his chest like a wave — joy so physical it pressed against his ribs and shortened his breath — could not be expressed, could not be spoken, could not be made audible in any form, because audibility was exposure and exposure was ruin and ruin was not his alone to risk.

"She is well?" His voice came out barely formed, a whisper shaped like a question.

"She is well. She is tired. She is asking for you."

He went in.

The room smelled of iron and tallow and the close, animal warmth of a body that had just completed an enormous labour. The candles had been kept to two — economy of light as a form of secrecy, because fewer candles meant fewer shadows on the windows that faced the courtyard, and shadows on windows were information, and information was the currency in which their ruin would be transacted. In the dim glow he saw her on the bed, propped against bolsters, her hair undone and dark with sweat against the linen, and in the crook of her arm a shape so small it seemed impossible that it could be the thing it was — a new human creature, entire and separate, arrived into the world through the specific, irrevocable passage that made Antonio a father and the Duchess a mother and the household a place where a secret had just doubled its weight.

She looked up at him. Her face was exhausted and luminous and entirely herself — the face she wore only when they were alone, the face that was not the Duchess's public composure and not a private dissolution of that composure but something other, something that existed only in the space their marriage had made, the space behind the panelled door, the space that was real and lawful and invisible.

"Come see," she said.

He crossed the room. He looked down at the child — *his* child, his son, the fact still so new it would not settle into sense — and saw a face crumpled and darkened with the effort of arrival, eyes shut, fists clenched, the whole miniature body curled inward as though still shaped by the space it had just departed. The boy was breathing. The breath was shallow and quick and the most astonishing sound Antonio had ever heard, because it was the sound of a life that had not existed an hour ago and now existed irreversibly, a life that would require protection of a kind Antonio had never before been required to provide and was not certain he knew how to provide, because the protection this child needed was not the ordinary protection of a father — food, shelter, the slow education of the world — but the specific, ruthless, unceasing protection of concealment, the same concealment that had governed the marriage and the pregnancy and this birth, which had been conducted in two candles' worth of light with the silence of a conspiracy because that is what it was, however much Antonio wished to call it something else.

He reached down. She guided his hand. He touched the boy's face — the cheek, impossibly soft, warm with the heat of new blood — and felt something close inside him, some door he had not known was open, a finality that was not an ending but a commitment so absolute it left no room for the calculations that had preceded it, the weighing of risk against desire, the nightly arithmetic of what they stood to lose. The arithmetic was finished. The sum was here, breathing, in his wife's arms, in a room lit by two candles, in a palace that did not know what it harboured.

"He has your mouth," she said. She was smiling — not the Duchess's smile, the measured diplomatic instrument she deployed in the public rooms, but the other one, the unguarded one, weary and radiant and entirely for him. "Poor child."

"He has your stubbornness," Antonio said. "He would not come quietly."

"Nothing worth having comes quietly."

He sat on the edge of the bed. Cariola moved behind them, tidying with the efficient discretion of a woman who understood that her presence was both necessary and intrusive, who performed the necessary parts and rendered the intrusive parts invisible, folding linens, carrying basins, maintaining the room as a functional space while its occupants occupied it as something else — a marriage bed, a family, a country of three citizens whose sovereignty was recognized by no one outside its borders.

The boy made a sound — not a cry, or not yet a cry, but the preliminary motion toward a cry, the intake of breath that precedes the noise, and Antonio watched his wife shift the child against her breast with a competence that astonished him, the way she simply *knew*, the body's knowledge preceding the mind's, and the sound subsided into the small wet rhythm of feeding, and the room was quiet again, and the quiet was not the enforced tactical silence of an hour ago but something softer, something that resembled peace, though Antonio knew it was not peace, not truly, because peace implied safety and safety implied the absence of threat and the threat had not been absent for a single moment since the night she had placed the ring on his finger and spoken the words that had made him her husband in the eyes of God and in the eyes of no one else, which was the precise topology of their situation — visible from heaven, invisible from earth, and the distance between the two was the distance in which they lived and in which this boy would live, and the distance was not safe, had never been safe, and would not become safe merely because Antonio wished it so, however fervently, however desperately, however completely he wished it in this moment, sitting on the edge of his wife's bed in a palace that believed he slept elsewhere, watching his son take his first meal in a world that did not yet know he existed.

Dawn was two hours away. Before dawn he would have to leave — back through the passage, back behind the tapestries, back to the steward's quarters where the steward's bed would be cold and undisturbed and would testify, if anyone cared to examine it, that Antonio Bologna had slept alone, as Antonio Bologna always slept, as Antonio Bologna would continue to appear to sleep for as long as the appearance could be sustained, which was a duration Antonio could not calculate and did not wish to calculate, because the calculation would return an answer, and the answer would be finite, and he did not want finitude tonight. Tonight he wanted only this: the two candles, the feeding child, his wife's hand resting on his where it lay on the coverlet, the sound of breathing — hers, slower now, steadying toward sleep; the boy's, quick and shallow and new — and the knowledge, inexpressible and perfect and dangerous, that he was a father, that the woman beside him was the mother of his son, that the son was here, alive, in Amalfi, a fact accomplished and irreversible, as permanent as the walls around them and as fragile as the silence that kept those walls from knowing what they held.

The courtyard was Bosola's preferred theatre of observation — not because it offered the best vantage, though it did, the colonnaded walks providing the kind of structured shade in which a man could stand without appearing to loiter, but because it was the place where the household performed its daily business of being a household, the traffic of servants and provisions and minor officials moving through the open space with the unselfconscious regularity of blood through veins, and Bosola had learned, in the galleries and the galleys both, that the truth of any organism was best read in its circulation, in what moved and when and with what urgency, and urgency was what he was reading now, had been reading for weeks, the way a physician reads a pulse — not for the single beat but for the pattern, the acceleration, the irregularity that betrays the hidden labour of the heart.

Something had changed in the palace. He could not yet name it, but his body knew it the way a dog knows weather, some shift in atmospheric pressure that preceded the visible event. The household moved differently. Cariola, the waiting-woman whose composure was ordinarily so complete it bordered on the geological, had been seen at unusual hours in corridors where her duties did not require her, carrying linens that did not correspond to any schedule of laundering Bosola had been able to determine, and Bosola had been determining, because determining was his office now, the office Ferdinand had purchased for him at the price of the provisorship of the horse — a title that put grain and leather under his authority while requiring him to attend to matters considerably more intimate than feed stores. The old woman who served as midwife to the town had been observed entering by the kitchen gate three weeks prior. A physician had attended the Duchess twice in a single week and had been paid, Bosola had noted, not through the household accounts but privately, by Cariola, in coin.

He had reported what he suspected. Ferdinand's letters in return had been brief and febrile — the handwriting of a man gripping the pen as though it were a throat. *Continue. Confirm. Name the father.* Bosola had continued. He had not yet confirmed, not with the certainty Ferdinand required, the kind of certainty that could bear the weight of whatever Ferdinand intended to build upon it. The apricot business had been suggestive — the Duchess's sudden appetite, the vomiting that followed — but suggestion was not proof, and Bosola, who had served time in the galleys for a murder committed on the Cardinal's behalf and had received in recompense nothing but the Cardinal's silence and seven years of rowing, understood the difference between suggestion and proof with the precision of a man who had been convicted on the latter.

It was early morning. The light was thin and provisional, the sun not yet above the eastern wall, and the courtyard was occupied by the particular emptiness that precedes the full activity of day — a servant crossing with a water jug, a groom leading a horse toward the farrier's, the small pre-dawn movements that constituted the palace's first consciousness. Bosola stood in the colonnade near the entrance to the private wing, not because he expected to see anything — expectation was a poor instrument; availability was better — but because he had made it his practice to be in this place at this hour, the hour when the private wing disgorged whatever the night had consumed, and because practice, unlike expectation, required no justification and attracted no attention, and attention was the one commodity Bosola could not afford to attract, his entire utility depending on the household's continued belief that the provisioner of the horse was merely the provisioner of the horse and not the anatomist Ferdinand had installed to open the Duchess's life and read its organs.

Antonio Bologna came through the door.

Bosola did not move. Movement was information, and information flowed in both directions; a man who shifted at another man's appearance confessed that the appearance mattered, and Bosola could not afford that confession. He remained where he was, in the shadow of the column, and watched.

Antonio moved quickly — not running, which would have been conspicuous, but walking with the controlled haste of a man who has stayed too long somewhere he should not have been and is attempting to compensate for the excess by the efficiency of his departure. His doublet was fastened incorrectly, the points misaligned by one eyelet, the kind of error a meticulous man commits only when dressing in darkness or in haste or both. His hair was disordered. His face carried the particular exhaustion of a night spent without sleep, but underneath the exhaustion there was something else, something Bosola could not immediately categorize — a brightness, a stunned elation imperfectly suppressed, as though Antonio were carrying inside himself a fire he was trying to conceal by walking normally while his entire body wished to do something other than walk.

He came down the steps into the courtyard. He did not see Bosola. This was itself information — Antonio Bologna was a careful man, a man whose years of service in the French court had taught him the perpetual lateral awareness of the professional subordinate, the habit of knowing who was in a room before entering it, and his failure to notice Bosola in the colonnade meant that his attention was elsewhere, occupied by something so consuming it had displaced the habits of a lifetime.

At the bottom of the steps, Antonio paused. He reached into his doublet — the gesture quick, furtive, the hand darting in and withdrawing as though checking that something was still there — and in the withdrawal his fingers caught on something, a paper, and the paper came free of the doublet and fell.

It fell the way papers fall — slowly, turning, catching the thin light as it descended — and Antonio did not notice. He was already moving again, crossing the courtyard toward the far colonnade and the passage that led to the steward's offices, his stride lengthening now as though distance from the private wing were itself a form of safety, and the paper lay on the flagstones behind him, white against grey, conspicuous as a dropped glove, as a confession.

Bosola waited. He counted Antonio's steps — ten, fifteen, twenty — until the steward passed under the far archway and was gone. Then he moved.

The paper was folded once. It was of good quality — not the coarse stuff of household accounts but the finer grade kept in the Duchess's private study, which Bosola knew because he had made it his business to know, to inventory every category of object that moved through the palace so that anomalies could be detected against the established pattern, and this paper was an anomaly, its quality inconsistent with a steward's correspondence, its presence on the flagstones inconsistent with the habits of a man who managed the household accounts with a precision that bordered on the devotional.

He unfolded it.

The hand was Antonio's. Bosola had seen it a hundred times on ledgers, requisitions, receipts — the neat, slightly compressed script of a man trained in chancery work, every letter formed with the economy of someone who wrote more than he spoke. But the content was not a ledger entry and not a requisition and not a receipt.

It was a horoscope.

Bosola read it standing in the colonnade with the dawn light strengthening around him, and as he read he felt the thing he had been circling for months resolve itself with the mechanical precision of a lock accepting its proper key. The horoscope was cast for a nativity — a birth — dated this night, this very night, the planetary positions calculated with the careful competence of a man who had studied such things in the French court where such studies were fashionable, and the calculations indicated a male child, born in the small hours, and the notations in the margins — Antonio's margins, Antonio's hand, Antonio's obsessive precision applied to the single most dangerous document a man in his position could produce — specified the hour and the house and the ascendant and all the apparatus of judicial astrology that a father might deploy in the effort to read his son's future, because that is what fathers did, they looked forward, they tried to see what was coming, and Antonio had been looking forward, had cast the chart, had committed to paper the astronomical coordinates of an event that could not be permitted to have occurred.

The Duchess had not merely been pregnant. The Duchess had delivered. A boy. This night.

Bosola folded the paper again along its original crease. He held it in his hand — this small, annihilating document, this square of good paper on which a careful man had written his own destruction in the language of the stars — and he stood in the colonnade and thought.

So. The suspicion was confirmed and the confirmation exceeded the suspicion. Not merely a pregnancy, which could have been explained — widows sometimes erred, sometimes were imposed upon, sometimes yielded to the persistent mathematics of loneliness — but a nativity horoscope in the steward's hand, which meant the steward was not merely the steward, which meant the steward had been in the private wing tonight not on business of the household but on business considerably more intimate, which meant the access Antonio Bologna enjoyed to the Duchess's private apartments was not the access of a servant to his employer but the access of — 

Bosola stopped the thought. He did not need to complete it. The horoscope completed it. The misbuttoned doublet completed it. The brightness in Antonio's exhausted face completed it — the unmistakable, idiotic radiance of a man who has just become a father, visible even through the fear, perhaps especially through the fear, because joy of that magnitude could not be fully suppressed by any act of will, and Antonio's will, however disciplined, was not equal to the force of what had happened to him tonight.

The Duchess of Amalfi had a child. The child had a father. The father was her steward. The steward had dropped the proof on the flagstones of the courtyard in the grey light of a morning that had not yet fully committed to being morning, and the proof was now in Bosola's hand, and Bosola's hand was the instrument of Ferdinand's will, and Ferdinand's will concerning his sister's sexual conduct had been made explicit in terms that left no room for interpretation and very little room for mercy.

Bosola put the horoscope inside his own doublet. He felt its edges against his chest — stiff, precise, incriminating — and he thought of Ferdinand's letters, the compressed handwriting, the single repeated instruction: *Confirm. Name the father.* He could confirm now. He could not yet name — the horoscope did not bear the Duchess's hand, only Antonio's, and a horoscope cast for a birth was evidence of a birth but not, strictly, evidence of paternity — but the confirmation was sufficient, more than sufficient, it was overwhelming, it was the kind of evidence that made further investigation not merely unnecessary but dangerous, because further investigation implied further proximity to the Duchess's private life, and further proximity to the Duchess's private life meant further proximity to whatever Ferdinand would do when he received the confirmation, and Bosola, who had rowed for seven years in consequence of his last proximity to the Aragonese family's requirements, understood with the clarity of a man who has studied the subject at length and at cost that proximity to the Aragonese family's actions was not a position from which one emerged unaltered.

He would write tonight. The letter would travel by the courier Ferdinand had designated — the man with the unremarkable face and the excellent horse who appeared in Amalfi at irregular intervals under the pretence of delivering cloth samples and departed with documents sewn into the lining of his saddlebag. The letter would contain the confirmation. The letter would describe the apricots, the midnight physician, the midwife at the kitchen gate, and now this — the steward's horoscope, the steward's disordered doublet, the steward's emergence from the private wing at an hour when the private wing should have held no one but the Duchess and her women. The letter would note that the horoscope specified a male child. The letter would not speculate on Ferdinand's likely response, because speculation was not Bosola's office, and because Bosola did not wish to speculate, and because the speculation, if conducted honestly, would return conclusions that Bosola preferred not to hold in his mind any longer than was strictly necessary — conclusions about what a man like Ferdinand would do with information like this, conclusions about what the information would cost the woman who had generated it, conclusions about what it meant to be the mechanism by which that cost was transmitted from the man who imposed it to the woman who would pay it.

The sun was above the wall now. The courtyard was filling with the ordinary commerce of morning — servants, tradesmen, the farrier's hammer ringing from the stables, the sounds of a household that did not know what it harboured and would not learn it from Bosola, because Bosola's report went not to the household but over it, above it, around it, directly to the man in Calabria whose interest in his sister's conduct could not be explained by politics alone and whose response to the confirmation Bosola was about to provide could not be predicted by any calculus Bosola possessed, which meant that Bosola was doing what he had always done, what the galleys had taught him and what the Cardinal's silence had confirmed — serving a purpose he could see for masters whose purposes he could not, converting his intelligence into their action, his knowledge into their power, his proximity into their reach, and accepting in exchange the provisorship of the horse and the understanding that the exchange was not equal and never would be and that the deficit was the price of the office and the office was the price of having survived the galleys and survival itself was the original debt, the debt that preceded all others, the debt that could not be discharged because it had been incurred not by choice but by the mere fact of continuing to exist in a world that had made it clear, repeatedly and at length, that his existence was not required but merely, for the moment, useful.

He touched the paper through his doublet. It crinkled faintly under his fingers — the sound of a birth, the sound of a secret, the sound of a family's destruction folded once along its original crease and carried against the chest of a man who had not made the family and would not destroy it and was merely, as he had always been, the passage through which destruction travelled from those who willed it to those who would receive it.

He turned and walked toward his quarters to compose the letter.

The letter arrived with the cloth merchant — the man Ferdinand had selected for his unremarkable face and his willingness to carry documents without reading them, two qualities that coincided less often than one might suppose. Ferdinand received him in the small cabinet adjoining his bedchamber at Calabria, the room he used for correspondence he did not wish his secretary to handle, a room with one door and no window large enough to admit a man and a lock he kept on his own person, because Ferdinand understood, as all the Aragonese understood, that privacy was not the absence of observation but its active, perpetual defeat, and the defeat required architecture.

The merchant stood while Ferdinand cut the stitching from the saddlebag lining. The man's hands were steady, his expression the professionally vacant arrangement of a face that had learned not to register what passed through it, and Ferdinand did not dismiss him immediately because dismissal implied urgency and urgency implied that the contents mattered, and Ferdinand did not wish any organism in his employ to understand how much the contents mattered, how completely the contents of this particular saddlebag lining had colonised his attention for months, had occupied the forward portion of his mind the way a siege occupies a city — not by destroying the ordinary functions but by subordinating them, so that every act of governance, every audience granted, every letter dictated to his secretary on matters of Calabrian administration, had been performed in front of the siege, before it, with the constant awareness that behind the ordinary business of the duchy there was this other business, this darker and more essential business, the business of his sister's body and what it contained and who had put it there.

He paid the merchant. The man left. Ferdinand locked the door.

The letter was in Bosola's hand — that cramped, efficient script Ferdinand had come to recognise the way a physician recognises a symptom, the letters pressed hard into the paper as though Bosola wrote with more force than the task required, the force of a man who had learned penmanship in circumstances where pen and ink were not readily available and who therefore used them, when available, with a kind of compensatory violence. Ferdinand did not sit. He read standing, near the single window, holding the paper at an angle to catch the light, and as he read he felt something happen in his chest that was not pain and not pleasure and not surprise but some compound of all three that had no name in any language he possessed, a chemical reaction for which no nomenclature existed because the reagents — fraternal love, fraternal authority, something else, something he would not name and had never named and whose namelessness was itself a form of naming, an outline visible precisely because the interior was left blank — the reagents were themselves unnamed, and unnamed things, when combined, produced unnamed results.

Bosola confirmed. The apricots — Ferdinand read the passage twice, the description of the Duchess's appetite and its consequence, the sudden sickness that was not sickness but its opposite, the body performing its ancient and irrefutable disclosure — the midnight visits of the physician, the midwife entering by the kitchen gate as though the kitchen gate were somehow less visible than the front, as though entrances and exits were not precisely the features of a building that a competent intelligencer observed first and most thoroughly, and Bosola was competent, Ferdinand had paid for competence and had received it, and the competence had produced this letter, these facts, this confirmation.

A male child. Born in the small hours. A horoscope cast for the nativity — cast by whom? Bosola's letter specified: the steward's hand. Antonio Bologna. The horoscope was in the steward's hand, and the steward had emerged from the private wing at dawn with his doublet misbuttoned, and Bosola noted these facts with the clinical detachment of a man presenting evidence rather than conclusions, but the evidence arranged itself into conclusions without requiring any assistance from Bosola or from Ferdinand or from anyone — the evidence was self-arranging, self-concluding, the way gravity is self-concluding, the stone does not require permission to fall.

She had a child.

Ferdinand set the letter on the table. He placed both hands flat on the wood, on either side of the paper, and stood like that for what might have been a minute or might have been longer — time had become unreliable, the room's single window admitting a light that seemed to thicken as he stood, the afternoon sun acquiring the viscosity of something poured — and he looked at the letter between his hands and he thought:

She has done this thing. She has done this thing that I told her she must not do. She stood before me in her widow's weeds and heard me say it — heard the Cardinal say it in his churchman's register and heard me say it in mine, which was not a churchman's register, which was the register of a brother who holds his sister's honour as he holds his own, as though they were a single garment worn by two bodies, a single skin stretched across the distance between Calabria and Amalfi, so that what touched her touched him, what entered her —

He lifted his hands from the table. The gesture was sudden, as though the wood had become hot, and he turned from the letter and walked the length of the small room — four paces, no more, the cabinet was built for privacy not for movement — and turned and walked back, and the letter was still there, Bosola's compressed handwriting still legible in the afternoon light, still confirming what it had confirmed a moment ago and would go on confirming regardless of Ferdinand's movement through the room, because facts, unlike brothers, did not pace.

A male child. A son. She had produced an heir outside the sanctioned channels of Aragonese marriage, outside the political calculus that governed such productions, outside the system of alliances and treaties and territorial consolidations that made a duchess's womb a diplomatic instrument rather than a private organ, and she had done this not in ignorance — she was not ignorant, she had never been ignorant, the Aragonese did not produce ignorance, they produced intelligence and ambition and the capacity to deploy both with a precision that other families admired and feared — she had done this in full knowledge of his prohibition, which meant she had weighed his prohibition against whatever had driven her to this act and had found his prohibition lighter, insufficient, unequal to the counterweight, and that insufficiency was — 

Ferdinand stopped. He was at the window. Through it he could see the courtyard of his own palace, the orderly Calabrian afternoon proceeding without reference to what he held in his mind, grooms and servants and the mundane machinery of a ducal household operating under the assumption that the Duke's attention was available for the Duke's business, an assumption that had been false for months and was now catastrophically false, because the Duke's attention was in Amalfi, had always been in Amalfi, had never left Amalfi, had remained there when his body departed after that last conversation in which he had given her the poniard and told her — what had he told her? He had told her that her reputation was her second life, that the public gaze upon a young widow was sharp and unforgiving, that she must close her doors against the world, and she had stood there, his twin, his mirror, the other half of whatever divided thing they were, and she had listened with that expression he could not read — an expression that might have been obedience or might have been its perfect imitation, and the difference between obedience and its imitation was the difference between a sister and a stranger, and he did not know which she was, had perhaps never known, and the not-knowing was —

He returned to the table. He picked up the letter and read it again. The facts had not changed. They would not change. A child existed that had not existed before, a child that was half Aragonese and half — what? Bosola could not yet name the father. The horoscope was in the steward's hand, but a steward might cast a horoscope at his employer's instruction. Bosola had noted the doublet, the disordered hair, the emergence from the private wing — circumstantial, suggestive, the architecture of an accusation without its keystone — but Ferdinand did not need Bosola to name the father, not yet, not for this purpose, because for this purpose the father was irrelevant, a variable that did not alter the equation, because the equation was: she had been told not to, and she had.

He folded the letter along its original creases. He folded it again, and again, reducing it to a tight square that fit inside his closed fist, and he held it there, his hand white at the knuckles, the paper compressed to the density of a small stone or a tooth or one of those objects the body produces and expels, and he thought of her face — not as it was now, however it was now, the face of a woman who had recently given birth, whatever changes that event worked upon a face, but as it had been, as it was in his memory, which was the only place he possessed it and therefore the place where his possession was most complete and most absolute and most unbearable, because memory did not admit revision, memory preserved the original, and the original was the face of his twin, his counterpart, the woman who shared his birth and his blood and his features — their mother had said they were difficult to tell apart as children, had dressed them differently so that the household could distinguish, and Ferdinand remembered this, remembered the sameness, the mirror-quality, the way her face was his face feminised or his face was hers made masculine, either formulation producing the same vertigo, the same sense that the boundary between self and other had been drawn in pencil rather than ink and could be, had been, was being erased by forces he could not govern because they preceded governance, preceded language, preceded the very categories of brother and sister by which he attempted to organise what he felt into something he could bear to feel.

She had a child.

He put the folded letter in the locked drawer of the cabinet, the drawer whose key he kept on the same chain as the key to the door, the chain he wore against his skin, under his shirt, so that the keys were always warm, always body-temperature, always in contact with him the way secrets were always in contact with the people who kept them — not adjacent to the body but part of it, woven into the skin's routine of warmth and sweat and the small perpetual labour of being alive.

He would not write to Bosola tonight. He would write tomorrow, or the day after, when the thing in his chest had resolved into something that could be conveyed in the compressed handwriting of a composed man giving composed instructions, because Ferdinand understood — had always understood, the understanding was constitutional, bred into the Aragonese line like the jaw and the dark eyes — that the distance between impulse and action was the space in which power resided, and a man who acted on impulse surrendered that space, collapsed it, and a collapsed space was a space without power, which was a space Ferdinand could not occupy, not as Duke, not as brother, not as whatever else he was in relation to his sister, the thing without a name that lived in the blank centre of the outline he would not draw.

Tomorrow. He would write tomorrow.

He unlocked the door and opened it and stepped into the corridor where the afternoon was still proceeding as though nothing had changed, as though the world were the same world it had been an hour ago, and Ferdinand moved through it with the precise, controlled gait of a man walking on a surface he no longer entirely trusted, each step placed with the deliberation of someone who suspects the floor may at any moment give way beneath him but is determined that no observer shall see the suspicion, no servant shall read in his stride the thing that Bosola's letter had planted in his chest, the small bright detonation still expanding, still displacing the air in his lungs, still burning.

He had entered by the private stair — the one the household did not use, the one whose existence he knew because he had grown up in this palace before it became hers, before their father's death and the subsequent redistributions of Aragonese property had assigned her Amalfi and him Calabria, splitting the family's holdings along lines that were geographical but also, Ferdinand understood now, temperamental, as though the division of territories had been a division of natures, Calabria's dry hillsides and fortified keeps corresponding to something in him while Amalfi's softer country, its coastal air and orange groves and the particular quality of light that entered these chambers in the evening, corresponded to something in her, something yielding, something that admitted entry.

The private stair was narrow and unlit. He climbed it with one hand against the wall, feeling the stone under his palm, stone he had touched as a child, stone that retained no memory of the touching but that he remembered with the fidelity of a body recalling its first geographies, and when he reached the door at the top he stood for a moment in the dark, listening, because a man who has traveled from Calabria unannounced, who has entered his sister's palace without presenting himself to her household, who has climbed a stair he was not expected to climb in order to arrive at a door he was not expected to open, such a man pauses before the final act, not from hesitation but from the need to collect himself into the shape the act requires — the shape of a brother arriving in judgment, the shape of a Duke whose authority extends beyond his own duchy into the conduct of his blood.

Through the door he could hear her voice. She was speaking to someone — her woman, Cariola, from the timbre of the response — speaking in the register she used for private hours, the register Ferdinand had known before any other voice, the register of his twin in her unguarded state, and the sound of it produced in him the reaction the letter had produced, the compound without a name, the reagents combining in his chest with a heat that was not anger and not grief and not the other thing, the thing he would not name.

He opened the door.

The bedchamber was lit by candles — too many candles for the hour, as though she had been reading or writing or performing some task that required more light than the evening provided, and in that excess of light she turned toward the sound of the door and her face performed the sequence he had anticipated during every mile of the journey from Calabria: recognition, then surprise, then the rapid calculation that followed surprise in any Aragonese face, the computation of what the unexpected presence meant and what response it demanded, and Ferdinand watched this sequence with the attention of a man watching a document being written, reading each expression as it formed, and what he read was fear — not the large theatrical fear of a woman confronted by a threat, but the small, precise fear of a woman confronted by a knowledge she had hoped to keep at a distance, the fear of someone whose concealment has been narrowed, if not yet breached.

"Brother."

The word hung in the candlelit air between them. Cariola had risen from her seat near the window, her hands arrested in the gesture of folding linen, and Ferdinand looked at the waiting woman long enough to convey that her presence was both noted and unwelcome, and Cariola looked to the Duchess, and the Duchess made some small motion — a tilt of the head, an adjustment of the eyes — and Cariola withdrew, leaving the linen half-folded on the chair, leaving the room through the door Ferdinand had not used, the ordinary door, the door that belonged to the household's daily traffic, and the sound of it closing was the sound of two people being left alone with whatever was between them, which was everything.

Ferdinand did not sit. The room offered several possibilities — a chair near the writing table, a cushioned bench beneath the window — but Ferdinand remained standing because standing preserved the geometry he required, the vertical authority of a man who has arrived to deliver something rather than to receive it, and also because sitting would have brought him closer to the surfaces of her life, the table where she wrote letters he had not read, the bed where she slept and where — where whatever had happened had happened, the bed being the site of the act whose consequence Bosola had reported, and Ferdinand could not sit near that bed, could not arrange himself in proximity to it with any composure, because the bed was not furniture but evidence, not an object but an accusation, and accusations were things one stood before.

She was watching him. The fear had settled into something more controlled — the Aragonese composure reasserting itself over the initial breach, the face rearranging into the expression he could not read, the expression that might have been obedience or its imitation — and Ferdinand felt the distance between them as a physical substance, the seven or eight feet of candlelit air that separated his body from hers, air that was warm and faintly scented with whatever she used, some preparation of flowers or oil that he recognised from childhood and that struck him now with the force of something recovered from a depth he had not known he possessed, a scent that was not a scent but a coordinate, a fixed point in the architecture of his memory of her, and the memory was —

"You have grown fair," he said. The words emerged before the sentence he had prepared, the sentence he had composed during the journey, the measured Calabrian prosecution he had rehearsed through miles of increasingly soft country as Amalfi approached — that sentence remained unspoken, displaced by this observation, which was not the observation of a brother arriving in judgment but of something else, something that watched her face in the candlelight and noted the changes the intervening months had worked upon it, changes that were not deterioration but a kind of ripening, the face fuller than he remembered, the body —

He stopped the thought. He stopped it the way a man stops a horse at a precipice, with the whole force of the reins and the whole weight of the body thrown backward, and the thought stopped, but the momentum of it continued past the stopping point, the way a horse's head continues forward even as the body halts, and what continued forward was the awareness that her body had changed, that the body he was looking at was not the body he had last seen, that between his last visit and this visit her body had performed the act that Bosola's letter described, had swelled and contracted, had opened and produced, and the evidence of that performance was visible in the way the candlelight fell across surfaces that were altered, and Ferdinand was looking at those surfaces with an attention that exceeded the attention of a brother, that exceeded the attention of any relation he could name, and the excess was the thing without a name, and the thing without a name was in the room with them now, standing in the seven feet of scented air, breathing.

"I did not expect you," she said.

"No." He heard his own voice as though it belonged to someone else — someone calmer, someone whose chest did not contain the expanding detonation, someone who could conduct this interview in the register of political authority rather than the register of — whatever this was. "You did not expect me. That was the point of the visit. To arrive at a moment when I was not expected. To see what the unguarded household revealed."

Her expression shifted. The composure held, but beneath it he could see the fear reasserting itself, and the fear confirmed what the letter had confirmed, because an innocent woman does not fear her brother's unannounced arrival, an innocent woman is surprised and then pleased, and the sequence he had watched on her face had not included pleasure, had not progressed beyond the calculation, and the absence of pleasure was itself an admission, a confession made by omission, the way silence can confess what speech withholds.

"And what does it reveal?" she asked, and her voice was steady, and the steadiness enraged him because it was the steadiness of someone managing a situation rather than experiencing it, the composure of a diplomat rather than a sister, and he wanted — what did he want? He wanted the composure to break. He wanted to see beneath it to the face he remembered, the unguarded twin, the other half, the mirror that showed him his own reflection without the intervening apparatus of political self-presentation, and the desire to break through to that face was so precise and so violent that he felt it as a physical sensation, a contraction of the muscles in his hands, his fingers curling toward his palms as though preparing to seize something or someone.

"It reveals," he said, and paused, and the pause was not rhetorical but necessary, a moment in which he selected the next words from the several available options with the care of a man handling blades, "that my sister's household has grown. That the Duchess of Amalfi has been — productive."

She did not flinch. He had expected her to flinch, had prepared for the flinch, had composed a response to the flinch — and its absence disoriented him, the way the absence of an expected step disorients a man descending a staircase in the dark, the foot finding air where it anticipated stone, and the disorientation produced a stumble in his composure that he corrected immediately but that she saw, he was certain she saw it, because she was his twin and twins read each other's faces the way scholars read texts, with an attention to the minor variations that carries meaning — the misplaced comma, the overlong pause, the hand curling toward the palm.

"I have administered the duchy," she said. "There are revenues to report, if you wish to discuss administration."

Administration. She was offering him administration. She was redirecting the conversation toward the surface business of the duchy the way a magician redirects attention from the mechanism to the spectacle, and Ferdinand felt the rage consolidate in his chest, the expanding detonation contracting now into something denser and more directional, a point of heat that aimed itself at the seven feet of air between them, at the composure she maintained like a wall, at the particular cruelty of her refusal to acknowledge what they both knew she had done.

He took a step toward her. One step. The distance became six feet, then — he stopped. He stopped because the closer he came the more visible the changes were, the body's testimony more legible at six feet than at seven, and at this distance the scent reached him fully, the floral preparation mixed with something else, something underneath, her skin's own contribution, and the two scents combined in his perception with the force of a key turning in a lock, opening a room he had kept sealed, and in that room was the knowledge he had carried from Calabria, the knowledge Bosola's letter had installed in him, and the knowledge was this: someone had been this close to her, closer than this, close enough to smell what he was smelling, close enough to see what he was seeing, and that someone had —

"I gave you a poniard," he said. His voice had dropped. The political register was gone. What remained was lower, more intimate, the voice of the private stair, the voice of the narrow unlit passage that led to her bedchamber, a voice meant for the distance between six feet and none. "I told you what it was for. I told you your reputation was a thing that, once damaged, would not mend. I told you the world watched young widows the way it watches — " He could not finish the simile. The simile required him to say what the world watched for, and what the world watched for was what had happened, and what had happened was in the room with them like a third person, occupying the space Cariola had vacated, sitting in the chair with the half-folded linen, breathing the candlelit air.

She held his gaze. The steadiness was still there — that intolerable steadiness, that composure that functioned as a fortification, walls he could not scale from this side of the seven feet, six feet, whatever the distance was now, it seemed to fluctuate, to contract and expand with his breathing, as though the air between them were tidal, governed by a force he did not control.

"I have done nothing," she said, "that a widow of rank and conscience might not do."

The sentence detonated in him. *Might not do.* The conditional tense — the lawyer's tense, the tense of hypothetical permission, the tense that admitted the act while disputing the prohibition — she was speaking the language of the courts rather than the language of the family, she was constructing a defence where no defence had been requested, because Ferdinand had not accused, had not named the act, had circled it the way one circles a wound one cannot bring oneself to touch, and her defence of the unnamed act was itself a confirmation, a second confession layered over the first, because you do not defend what you have not done, you defend what you have done and what you know to be indefensible, and the defence was the admission, and the admission was in her voice, in the steadiness of her voice, in the particular calm of a woman who has rehearsed this moment in her mind and arrives at it prepared, fortified, composed.

The steward. It was the steward. Ferdinand knew it with the certainty of a man who has assembled evidence in the sealed room of his mind over months of silence, Bosola's letter providing the final element, the horoscope in Antonio Bologna's hand, the misbuttoned doublet, the emergence from the private wing — and now, standing in her bedchamber, seeing the changes the months had worked on her body, smelling the scent that someone else had smelled at closer range, Ferdinand felt the knowledge complete itself, not as deduction but as recognition, the way one recognises one's own face in an unexpected mirror — the steward, the household officer, the man who managed her revenues and her correspondence and, Ferdinand now understood, her body, the man who had been admitted to this room, to this bed, to the proximity Ferdinand occupied now, the six feet or five feet or whatever the diminishing distance was, and who had closed that distance entirely, had arrived where Ferdinand stood and continued past it, past the point where Ferdinand had stopped, into the territory Ferdinand could not enter, the territory whose border was the thing without a name.

"Do not think," Ferdinand said, and the words were quiet now, so quiet that the candle flames seemed louder, the small hiss of wax and wick competing with his voice, "that I will not discover what you have done. That the walls of this palace do not report to me. That the loyalty of your household is to you rather than to your blood."

She did not answer. The silence was its own architecture — a structure she erected between his words and whatever response she withheld — and in that silence Ferdinand heard what she did not say, heard the name she did not speak, the confession she did not make, and the hearing was more terrible than any speech because it left the name in the place where Ferdinand had put it, in his own mind, unconfirmed by her voice, so that the knowledge remained his private possession, unshared, unreleased, trapped inside his chest with the unnamed thing, the two of them — the knowledge and the unnamed thing — cohabiting in the sealed space behind his ribs the way a prisoner and a jailer cohabit in a cell, each unable to leave without the other.

He wanted her to say it. He wanted her to say the name. He wanted her to open her composure and let him see the act she had committed, not because the seeing would relieve him but because the not-seeing was worse, the not-seeing required him to supply the images himself, and the images he supplied were — they were —

He turned. He turned away from her because the alternative to turning was to close the remaining distance, and he did not know what he would do at the end of that distance, did not trust the shape his hands would take when they arrived at her body, the body that had been touched, the body that had admitted, the body that was his body's mirror, his twin, the other version of the thing he was, and the hands that wanted to reach her were the hands that had given her the poniard, the hands that had told her the blade was for her own use and had meant — what had he meant? He no longer knew what he had meant. The meaning had become contaminated by the months of waiting and the letter and the private stair and the scent and the six feet and the knowledge and the thing without a name, and the contamination was total, irreversible, the way a drop of blood in water is irreversible, dispersing until the entire vessel is coloured and no clear water remains.

"I will leave tonight," he said, facing the door, the door through which he had entered, the private stair, the narrow dark passage back to the version of himself that could function as a Duke, the version that governed Calabria and conducted diplomacy and wrote letters in a composed hand. "I will leave, and you will consider what I have said, and you will know that I am watching you through instruments you cannot see, through eyes that do not belong to faces you recognise, and the watching will not stop."

He heard her breathe. One breath, drawn in through what might have been a constricted throat, and the sound of it — that small involuntary sound, the first crack in the fortification, the first admission that his presence had reached her, had penetrated the composure, had arrived at the person beneath the Duchess — the sound of it entered him with a precision he would remember for the rest of his life, which would not be long, though he did not know

The breath he had heard — that single drawn breath, the crack in the fortification — had not closed. It had widened in the hours after Ferdinand's departure, widened through the night while she sat in the chair near the writing table with the candles burning down to their sockets, widened through the grey arrival of morning when Cariola returned and found her sitting where she had been left, the linen still half-folded on the opposite chair, and the widening was not collapse but aperture, an opening through which she could see, for the first time with full clarity, the architecture of her situation — its walls, its passages, its points of egress, and the fact that the points of egress were fewer than she had permitted herself to believe.

He was watching her through instruments she could not see. He had said this, and the saying of it had been redundant, because she had known — had she not always known? — that the household contained eyes that were not her own, that the palace she administered was also a palace that administered her, reporting her movements through channels she could not map to destinations she could not control. Bosola. She thought the name without speaking it, the way one thinks the name of an illness one suspects but has not yet confirmed, and the thinking of the name produced a sensation she recognised as the tactical equivalent of nausea — the body's intelligence arriving before the mind's, the visceral registration of a threat the intellect was still composing into sentences.

She had married Antonio Bologna in this room. Per verba de praesenti, the words spoken into the air of this chamber with Cariola as witness, the ring placed on his hand, the contract sealed by the oldest authority available to a woman of her rank and conscience — the authority of her own voice, her own consent, her own body offered freely to the man she had chosen freely, and the freedom of that choice had been, for the years of its concealment, a private country she inhabited, a territory more truly hers than Amalfi, because Amalfi had been assigned to her by the dead while the marriage had been chosen by the living, by the version of herself that was not the Duchess and not the widow and not the sister but the woman, the irreducible thing beneath the titles, and that woman had chosen well, had chosen a man whose qualities she had observed with the patience of someone reading a long text carefully, noting the grammar of his conduct, the syntax of his judgments, the particular eloquence of his competence in matters both administrative and — other. The marriage was right. She held this certainty the way one holds a blade by its handle, the cutting edge directed outward, toward the world that would dispute it.

But the concealment. The concealment was the thing that could not hold.

Three children now. The eldest — the son whose horoscope Antonio had cast in those reckless first hours, the horoscope that had fallen from his pocket like a confession dropping from a sleeve — the eldest was old enough to be seen, old enough to carry in his face the evidence of his parentage, and though the household had accepted the fiction she had constructed (a fiction of charitable adoption, of noble orphanage, of the Duchess's well-known generosity toward foundlings), the fiction required the household's willingness to believe, and Ferdinand's visit had altered the economy of belief within these walls, had introduced a new currency — fear — that would inflate until the old currency of loyalty was worthless, and when that happened, when the household's fear of Calabria exceeded its loyalty to Amalfi, the fiction would collapse, and the collapse would expose not only the marriage but the children, and the children were —

She could not think about the children. She could not bring the children into the calculus of threat because the calculus of threat was rational and the children were not rational, the children were the bodies she had produced, the bodies that had emerged from her body into the candlelit air of this chamber and had drawn their first breaths in the same room where she had drawn her constricted breath as Ferdinand stood with his back to her, facing the door, and the two breaths — the children's first and her own recent one — existed in the same acoustic space, the same chamber, and the chamber was no longer safe.

Antonio came to her at midday, entering through the ordinary door, the door that belonged to the household's traffic, and she watched him cross the room the way she always watched him cross rooms — with the attention of a woman cataloguing what she loved, storing it against some future in which the catalogue might be all that remained — and today the cataloguing carried an urgency it had not carried before, because the future she had stored against was no longer hypothetical but approaching, visible on the horizon the way weather is visible, a darkening at the edge of the sky that had not been there yesterday.

He knew, of course. He had known since the night of Ferdinand's visit, had known from the sound of her voice when she summoned him before dawn, from the particular quality of her composure which was not composure at all but its residue, the structure left standing after the substance had been evacuated, like a wall remaining after a fire has consumed the house it bounded. He knew, and the knowing had settled in his face as a stillness she recognised — the stillness of a man performing the arithmetic of survival, counting the available resources against the magnitude of the threat and arriving at a figure that did not balance.

"We cannot stay," she said.

The words had been forming since Ferdinand's departure, assembling themselves in the sealed room of her mind the way words assemble in a throat before speech — the muscles gathering, the breath collecting, the tongue positioning itself against the palate — and now they emerged into the air between them with a finality that surprised her, because until she spoke them they had been revocable, and now they were not.

Antonio did not answer immediately. He stood near the writing table where he had paused in his crossing, one hand resting on the wood surface, and the hand on the wood was steady, and the steadiness of it was the quality she had first noted in him, years ago, before the marriage, before the children, before the concealment — the steadiness of a man whose centre of gravity was lower than other men's, more firmly planted, less susceptible to the tilts and shifts that toppled less anchored persons, and she loved the hand on the table with a specificity that was almost painful, loved the particular way the fingers rested, the breadth of the palm, the wrist emerging from the sleeve, because the hand was the emblem of the man, and the man was the choice she had made, and the choice was right even if the concealment was failing, and the distinction between the choice and the concealment was the distinction she lived inside, the narrow habitable space between what was right and what was falling.

"Loretto," she said. "A pilgrimage. The Duchess of Amalfi, moved by devotion, undertaking a journey to the shrine of Our Lady — the household will not question it, the court will not question it, the motive is legible in the vocabulary of widowhood, a woman of rank and conscience seeking the intercession of —" She stopped. The strategy was assembling itself as she spoke, the pieces clicking into place with the mechanical precision of a lock's tumblers, and she could hear in her own voice the register Ferdinand had accused her of — the register of the diplomat, the manager of situations — and she did not care, because the situation required management, because management was what stood between her children and whatever Ferdinand's instruments would deliver if she remained.

"You will go ahead," she said, and now she was looking at Antonio directly, and the looking was both the wife's gaze and the Duchess's command, the two authorities occupying her face simultaneously. "You and the eldest boy. To Ancona. You will go before us, separately, so that the departure does not present itself as a departure but as the ordinary dispersal of a household in motion, servants sent ahead to prepare the route, the steward traveling on the Duchess's business —" She could hear the architecture of it, the plan constructing itself in real time, each element bearing the weight of the element above it, and she could also hear what the architecture concealed, which was flight, plain flight, the oldest human motion, the body removing itself from the site of danger, and the concealment of the flight within the pilgrimage was another concealment layered over the original concealment, a second fiction erected inside the first, and she knew — in the part of her mind that performed the calculations she did not share with Antonio, the calculations that concerned the probability of success rather than its desirability — she knew that fictions nested inside fictions were unstable structures, that each additional layer of concealment increased the surface area available to discovery, the way each additional room added to a house increases the number of walls that might be breached.

But the alternative was to remain. And to remain was to wait for Ferdinand's instruments to complete their work, to sit in this chamber while the walls reported and the eyes watched and the loyalty of the household converted, person by person, from her currency to his, until the morning arrived when she woke to find herself not the Duchess of Amalfi but the prisoner of Calabria, confined by the brother whose hands had curled toward his palms as he stood six feet from her body.

Cariola was in the room. She had been in the room throughout — standing near the window with the quality of stillness particular to servants who have learned to make themselves architecturally invisible, part of the wall, part of the furnishing — and the Duchess turned to her now because Cariola was the third element, the witness, the woman who had stood behind the arras during the marriage and who had carried the knowledge of the marriage through every subsequent day like a stone sewn into the lining of her dress, heavy but hidden, altering her posture without being visible, and Cariola's face as she received the Duchess's gaze showed what the Duchess expected it to show: understanding, and beneath the understanding, fear, and beneath the fear, the loyalty that had not yet converted to Ferdinand's currency and that might — the Duchess calculated, and the calculation was the kind of calculation one makes about the materials of one's own survival, the tensile strength of rope, the depth of water — that might hold long enough.

"You will come with me," the Duchess said to Cariola. "The two younger children travel with us. The pilgrimage covers the movement. At Loretto we turn south to Ancona; Antonio and the boy are already there. From Ancona —"

She did not finish the sentence. From Ancona was the territory of pure hypothesis, the country beyond the plan's last reliable coordinate, and she could not describe it because she could not see it, because the future beyond Ancona depended on variables she did not control — the Cardinal's reach, Ferdinand's patience, Bosola's diligence, the willingness of Ancona's authorities to shelter a Duchess who had defied her family's interdiction — and the dependence on uncontrolled variables was the condition she had inhabited since the night of the marriage, the night she had spoken the words per verba de praesenti into the candlelit air and sealed a contract that was legally absolute and practically precarious, a marriage that existed fully in the chamber of law and barely at all in the chamber of the world.

Antonio's hand was still on the table. She looked at the hand and thought: I chose this. I chose this man and this marriage and these children, and the choosing was right, and the concealment was the error, and the pilgrimage is another concealment, and the error is compounding, and I know it is compounding, and I will compound it anyway because the alternative is surrender, and surrender is a word that does not exist in the vocabulary I have chosen, the vocabulary of a widow of rank and conscience who has done nothing she might not do.

"Loretto," Antonio said, and the word in his voice carried a different weight than it had carried in hers — in hers it had been strategy, but in his it was geography, the actual distance between Amalfi and the shrine, the roads, the inns, the checkpoints where a steward traveling with a small boy might be questioned, might be recognised, might be intercepted — and the difference in weight was the difference between their positions in this arrangement, the Duchess who commanded and the steward who executed, and the difference was also the thing that Ferdinand could not bear, the asymmetry of rank that the marriage had bridged privately but that the world, if it learned of the bridging, would treat as a collapse.

"Trust the plan," she said, and did not say: because the plan is all we have. Did not say: because the walls report and the eyes watch and the instruments are in motion. Did not say: because my brother stood in this room and smelled my skin and curled his hands and spoke in a voice that was not the voice of policy, a voice from some deeper register, some lower passage, and in that voice I heard what I have always heard from Ferdinand, the sound of a thing that has no name and no remedy and no end except the end it will eventually bring about.

She said: "Trust the plan." And Antonio nodded. And Cariola, by the window, drew a breath that was the breath of a woman receiving orders she would obey not because they were wise but because the woman who gave them was the woman she served, and service, like marriage, was a contract that held until it was broken by forces larger than the parties who had made it.

The candles from the night before had been replaced. New candles burned in the brackets, clean wax, fresh wicks, and the light they produced was indistinguishable from the light of the old ones, the same amber glow falling across the same surfaces, the same chamber, and the Duchess looked at the light and thought: we will not see this room again. The thought arrived with the clarity of prophecy, which is not foresight but pattern recognition, the mind perceiving in the present arrangement of things the shape of what the arrangement will become, and what this arrangement would become was absence — the room continuing after they had left it, the candles burning for no one, the walls reporting to Ferdinand's instruments that the Duchess was gone, that the pilgrimage had carried her beyond their jurisdiction, that the concealment had moved from a fixed position to a mobile one, and the mobility was both advantage and exposure, because a thing in motion can evade but a thing in motion can also be tracked, and Ferdinand's instruments were designed for tracking.

She began to compose the letters. The announcement of the pilgrimage, the instructions to the household, the carefully worded communication to Bosola — because Bosola would need to be told something, and the something would need to be plausible, and the plausibility of the something was the last wall between her family and Ferdinand's knowledge — and as she wrote, Antonio stood by the table and Cariola stood by the window, and the three of them occupied the room like the three points of a triangle, a geometric figure that would hold its shape only as long as all three points remained in their positions, and the Duchess understood, as she formed the letters on the page, that the triangle was about to move, to detach itself from this room and travel across the map of Italy toward a destination that was not Loretto but Ancona, not devotion but survival, not pilgrimage but flight disguised as pilgrimage disguised as the ordinary movements of a Duchess of rank and conscience, and the disguises were multiplying, and each multiplication was a thinning, and the thinning would continue until the disguise was transparent and the flight was visible and the instruments had done their work.

But that was later. Now there were letters to write.

They left Amalfi in stages, as things disassemble — not all at once but piece by piece, so that the absence accumulates before it is perceived, the way a wall loses its mortar grain by grain until the day it falls and the neighbors say they had not noticed.

Antonio went first. He rode out through the eastern gate on a Tuesday morning with the eldest boy mounted before him on a horse the household knew as the steward's bay, a serviceable animal, unremarkable, chosen precisely for its unremarkability, because the journey of a steward on his mistress's business was a sentence the gate-guards could read without effort, a sentence whose grammar required no scrutiny, and they read it and passed it and forgot it, as one forgets any sentence that says nothing unexpected. The boy was three years old, or near enough. He sat in the crook of Antonio's arm with the solemn concentration children bring to novelty, watching the road open before them as though it were a text he was learning to decipher, and the road — the road from Amalfi through the Campanian hills, northeast toward the Papal States — was a text, and what it said was flight, but the saying was in a language the gate-guards did not read, and the boy could not yet read, and only Antonio, his hand steady on the reins as it had been steady on the table, understood the grammar fully, the grammar of a man removing his son from the jurisdiction of those who would, if they knew the boy's parentage, treat the boy not as a child but as evidence.

Behind him, Amalfi closed like a mouth around the space he had vacated.

The Duchess departed six days later. The household had been informed: a pilgrimage to Our Lady of Loretto, the shrine in the Marches, an act of devotion consonant with the Duchess's known piety and consistent with the widowed state she publicly maintained — the widowed state that was, in point of legal fact, no longer her state at all, the marriage to Antonio having dissolved it per verba de praesenti in the candlelit chamber that was now behind her, growing smaller with distance, though not with memory, never with memory. She traveled with Cariola and the two younger children and a retinue sufficient to confirm the journey's announced purpose but insufficient to suggest what it concealed, and the insufficiency was calculated, as all insufficiencies in the Duchess's arrangements were calculated, the mathematics of deception requiring that the visible quantity be large enough to persuade but small enough to move.

Bosola watched the departure from the courtyard. He stood near the fountain — the fountain whose basin collected the runoff from the palace roof, water descending through channels built into the walls of a house that was itself a system of channels, information descending through its own architecture toward destinations the water did not choose — and his face, as the Duchess's carriage passed, showed nothing that the passing required it to show. He was the instrument. The instrument observed. What the instrument concluded it transmitted elsewhere, through channels of its own, and the transmission was already in motion before the carriage cleared the gate, the letter composing itself in the space behind his eyes where intelligence performed its conversions, rendering observation into report, report into consequence, consequence into the currency Ferdinand spent.

The road from Amalfi to Loretto passes through country that does not care who travels it. The hills have their own concerns — erosion, season, the slow business of geological time — and the carriages that cross them are interruptions the landscape tolerates without interest, the way a body tolerates a fever: registering the disturbance, continuing its deeper processes, indifferent to the human drama the disturbance carries. The Duchess's carriage moved through this indifference for four days. Cariola sat opposite her, the two younger children between them — warm weights, breathing cargo, the bodies the Duchess had produced and was now transporting across the map of Italy toward a destination the children could not comprehend, because the destination was not a place but a condition, the condition of being beyond Ferdinand's reach, and the children understood reach only as the arms that held them, not as the political extension of fraternal obsession across provincial borders.

At Loretto the pilgrimage performed itself. The Duchess knelt before the shrine. She made the appropriate offerings. The priests received her with the deference her rank commanded and the attentiveness her donations ensured, and the kneeling and the offering and the receiving were a theatre in which the Duchess was simultaneously the performer and the audience — performing devotion for the priests, performing pilgrimage for whatever eyes had followed her from Amalfi, and watching herself perform with the detached precision of a woman who understood that all her actions now existed in two registers, the visible and the actual, and the distance between the registers was the distance she had to cross before she could stop performing and begin, simply, to live.

She did not stop at Loretto. The turn south — toward Ancona, away from the return route to Amalfi, away from the itinerary the household expected — the turn south was the moment the pilgrimage shed its skin and the flight emerged, wet and blinking, into the open air. The reduced retinue noticed. Some of the servants who had accompanied her looked at each other with the glances of people recalculating, adjusting the story they had been told against the evidence of the road they were now traveling, and the Duchess saw the glances and understood that the fiction was thinning, that each mile south of Loretto subtracted a layer of plausibility from the pilgrimage narrative, and she could feel the subtraction the way one feels a garment wearing through — not all at once but thread by thread, the fabric growing translucent, the body beneath it becoming progressively visible to anyone who cared to look.

She did not explain. Explanation would have required a replacement fiction, and she was done with fictions, or nearly done — done enough to let the servants' glances go unanswered, to let the silence where an explanation should have been speak its own language, the language of a woman whose trajectory had departed from the announced course and who did not intend to justify the departure.

Ancona received them in the late afternoon of a day whose light was the color of straw. The city sat on its promontory above the Adriatic, white and indifferent, a trading port accustomed to arrivals that did not explain themselves, and the Duchess's carriage entered through the harbor gate and moved through streets that smelled of salt and hemp and the particular mineral odor of stone buildings warmed by a long day's sun, and the smelling of these foreign smells was the first sensation of exile, the body's registration that the air it breathed was no longer the air of home, that the lungs were performing the same function in a different country, and the difference was not chemical but political — the air of Ancona carried no obligation to Calabria, owed nothing to Ferdinand, answered to a jurisdiction her brothers did not control, and the freedom of the air was the freedom she had traveled toward, the condition she had sought.

Antonio was waiting. He had taken lodgings in a house near the harbor — modest, defensible, chosen with the steward's eye for practical sufficiency — and the eldest boy was with him, healthy, brown from the road, standing behind his father's legs with the wariness of a child in an unfamiliar place, and when the Duchess descended from the carriage the boy looked at her with the face she loved, the face that carried Antonio's brow and her own mouth and the particular arrangement of features that was neither parent's alone but the testament of their union, the evidence their bodies had produced, and the evidence was standing in a doorway in Ancona, alive, and the aliveness was the thing that mattered, the thing the flight had been for.

Cariola carried the younger children inside. The house closed around them — a different house, different walls, different channels, but a house nonetheless, a structure that would contain them while they determined what came next, and the determining of what came next was the work that began immediately, in the first hour, because Ancona was not a destination but a position, a coordinate on a map that other people were also reading, and the other people had instruments, and the instruments were in motion, and the motion did not pause because the Duchess had arrived at a harbor town and felt, for the first time in months, that the air she breathed owed nothing to her brothers.

The Duchess stood at the window of the rented house and looked out at the Adriatic, which was grey and moving and indifferent, and behind her Antonio was arranging the room with the efficiency of a man who had managed households his entire life and could manage this smaller one — this household of five, six counting Cariola, this remnant, this extracted core — and the arranging was love expressed as logistics, devotion rendered in the grammar of practical action, and she listened to him moving behind her and thought: we are here. We have left the room where we married and the palace where I governed and the city whose name I carry, and we are here, in a rented house in Ancona, and the renting is temporary because everything is temporary now, because we have exchanged the fixed position for the mobile one, and the mobile one is both freedom and exposure, and the exposure has already begun, and we cannot see it yet, but it has begun.

The sun set over the harbor. The candles were lit — different candles, different wax, different wicks, but the same amber glow, the same light falling across different surfaces, and the Duchess looked at the light and did not think of the room in Amalfi where she had composed the letters, did not think of the walls that reported, did not think of the instrument standing by the fountain watching her departure with a face that showed nothing, because the thinking of those things belonged to the position she had vacated, and she was in the new position now, the position called Ancona, and the position had its own problems, its own calculations, its own walls and channels, and the walls and channels were unfamiliar, which meant she could not yet read them, which meant she could not yet know what they reported or to whom.

The boy fell asleep in Antonio's arms. Cariola put the younger children down in the room that served as nursery. The house settled into the particular silence of a new habitation, the silence of a structure learning the sounds of the bodies it now contained, adjusting its acoustics to their breathing, their footsteps, their murmured conversations, and the silence was temporary, as the house was temporary, as Ancona was temporary, as everything was temporary now except the marriage, which was permanent, which had been permanent since the night of the words spoken per verba de praesenti, and the permanence of the marriage inside the temporariness of everything else was the structure the Duchess inhabited, the narrow space between what held and what was falling.

They were in Ancona. The flight had delivered them. What it had delivered them to, only the subsequent days would show.

At Loretto the ceremony was performed in the manner of all ceremonies: publicly, with vestments, before witnesses whose presence converted a private intention into an institutional fact.

The Cardinal had arrived the day before. He came not from Rome but from the direction of Rome, which was sufficient — the direction carried the authority the city conferred, the way a river carries the mineral content of its source long after the source is out of sight. He traveled with an escort appropriate to his rank: armed men, ecclesiastical attendants, a secretary whose function was to produce documents that bore the seal of an office the Cardinal inhabited the way a hermit crab inhabits a shell — not having grown it, not being of it, but finding it useful, finding it protective, finding it a structure from which to extend and retract as circumstance required.

He knew about the marriage. The knowing had reached him through channels the play does not specify because the channels are not the point; the point is that the knowing had arrived, fully formed, at the desk of a man whose desk was a surface on which knowledge was converted into action with the efficiency of a counting-house converting currency. The Duchess had married her steward. The Duchess had married beneath her. The Duchess had married in defiance of the explicit prohibition her brothers had delivered in the chamber at Amalfi — the prohibition she had received with the downcast compliance of a woman who was already, at the moment of receiving, composing her refusal, though neither brother had read the composition on her face because neither brother read faces; they read obedience, and obedience was what her face had shown them, and they had accepted the showing and departed, and the departure was the error, if error it was, though error implies miscalculation, and the brothers had not miscalculated so much as they had failed to calculate at all, assuming the prohibition self-enforcing, the way one assumes a wall will stand because it is a wall, forgetting that walls require mortar, and mortar requires maintenance, and maintenance requires presence, and they had not been present.

Now the Cardinal was present. He stood in the shrine at Loretto where the Duchess had knelt six days earlier and made offerings the priests had accepted with deference, and the standing was a correction, the Cardinal's body occupying the space her devotion had occupied, overwriting her piety with his authority, and the overwriting was deliberate, calculated to occur in the same location so that the location itself would carry the contradiction — here she knelt in false pilgrimage, here her brother stood in actual judgement, and the here was the same here, the same stone floor, the same incense-darkened air, the same shrine that received all comers with the indifference of a structure that had outlasted the intentions of everyone who had ever entered it and would outlast these intentions too.

He removed his cardinal's robes.

The removal was performed before the assembled witnesses — priests of the shrine, papal functionaries, officers of the local jurisdiction — and the removing was not a renunciation but a translation, the cardinal's scarlet yielding to the soldier's steel, the ecclesiastical office shedding itself so that the man beneath could act in the register the ecclesiastical office technically forbade. He put on armour. He buckled a sword. The transformation was silent and complete, performed with the practical economy of a man who had done this calculus before, the calculus of which body to present to which situation, and the situation required the armoured body, the body that could sign instruments of banishment without the theological impediment of the body that was supposed to forgive.

The priests watched. What they thought, the shrine did not record, because shrines record offerings, not opinions, and the priests' opinions — if they had opinions, if the witnessing of a prince of the Church exchanging his vestments for a breastplate at the altar of Our Lady of Loretto produced in them anything resembling judgement — their opinions were stored in the same silence that stores all opinions that cannot be safely spoken, the silence of men who understood institutional power with the intimacy of men who served it and knew, with the knowledge service provides, that the institution's moral content and the institution's operational content occupied different registers, and the register that mattered — the register that paid salaries, confirmed appointments, maintained the physical fabric of the shrine in which they stood — was the operational one.

The instrument of banishment was read aloud. The document named the Duchess of Amalfi, formerly widow, now discovered to have contracted a clandestine marriage with one Antonio Bologna, steward of her household, in violation of her obligations to her family and her station, and the naming was a stripping, each clause removing a layer of the Duchess's public identity the way the Cardinal's vestments had been removed — first the dignity of the widowed state, then the autonomy of the ducal office, then the protection of the family name, until what remained was a woman, married to a steward, banished from the territory she had governed, and the banishment was total: she could not return to Amalfi, Antonio could not return, the eldest son — the boy who had ridden through the eastern gate on a Tuesday morning, mounted before his father on the steward's bay, the boy whose parentage the gate-guards had not known to scrutinize — the boy could not return. The document made it so. The seal made it so. The Cardinal's signature, applied with the hand that had unbuckled the ecclesiastical and buckled the martial, made it so.

The news reached Ancona before the document did, because news travels faster than documents, faster than horses, faster than anything except rumour, which is instantaneous, which arrives before it departs, which was already in the harbour streets and the trading houses and the rented room near the water where the Duchess stood at the window looking out at the Adriatic, which was grey and moving and indifferent, and was still grey and moving and indifferent when Cariola brought the report, her face carrying the information before her mouth delivered it.

Banished.

The word entered the room and changed its architecture. The walls that had been temporary were now insufficient. The harbour that had been a position was now an exposure. The air that had owed nothing to Ferdinand now owed something to the Cardinal, who was not Ferdinand but who was the other brother, the brother whose interest was political where Ferdinand's was — was something else, something the play does not name because the naming would reduce it — and the political interest had produced a political instrument, the banishment, and the instrument had been applied, and the application was public, which meant the Duchess's secret marriage was no longer secret, which meant the fiction was not thinning but gone, not translucent but transparent, not a garment wearing through but a garment removed, and the body beneath — the body that had married, the body that had borne children, the body that had fled — was visible now to anyone who looked, and everyone was looking.

Antonio received the news standing. He did not sit down. The not-sitting-down was not courage, precisely, nor defiance, precisely, but the habit of a body that had managed crises at a desk and understood that the vertical position was the working position, the position from which one calculated next actions, and he was calculating, his steward's mind performing the arithmetic of diminished options — Ancona was no longer safe, or would not be safe once the banishment's implications propagated through the local jurisdiction; other cities might receive them or might not, depending on the weight they assigned to the Cardinal's displeasure weighed against whatever advantage the Duchess's presence might confer — and the arithmetic was producing small numbers, numbers that shrank with each variable he added, and the shrinking was the measure of what the banishment had cost.

The eldest son did not understand. He was three years old, or near enough, and banishment was a word that belonged to the grammar adults used, the grammar of jurisdictions and instruments and seals, and the boy's grammar was simpler — house, father, mother, food, sleep, the bay horse, the harbour smell — and in the boy's grammar nothing had changed, because the room was the same room and the parents were the same parents and the harbour was the same harbour, and the change that the word had introduced existed in a register the boy could not access, the register where a family's coordinates on the map of political possibility had just been recalculated by a man in armour who had been, an hour earlier, a man in scarlet, and would be, an hour later, a man in scarlet again, the armour shed as easily as it had been assumed, the ecclesiastical body reassembling itself over the martial one like a garment pulled back on after dirty work, the vestments settling over the breastplate's memory the way absolution settles over sin — not erasing it, not denying it, but covering it, making it invisible, making it someone else's problem, and the someone else was the Duchess, and the problem was total, and the covering was complete.

At Loretto the shrine resumed its silence. The priests swept the floor. The candles continued to burn. The offerings the Duchess had made six days earlier remained in the coffers where the priests had placed them, her money indistinguishable from any other supplicant's money, because money does not carry the identity of its giver once given, and the shrine was richer by the amount of her devotion, and the devotion had purchased nothing — not protection, not sanctuary, not the intercession of the Virgin whose image presided over the nave with the painted serenity of a figure who had outlasted every petition ever brought before her and would outlast this one too, this petition that had not been spoken aloud because the Duchess had known, even as she knelt, that the kneeling was performance, and the shrine had known too, because shrines always know, and knowing, do nothing, because doing is not the function of shrines; the function of shrines is receiving, and the shrine had received the Duchess and the Cardinal equally, with equal indifference, taking the Duchess's money and the Cardinal's authority and storing both in the same institutional silence, the silence of a structure that served whoever entered it and judged no one and protected no one and would stand, white and indifferent on its hillside, long after the Duchess and the Cardinal and Antonio and the boy and the banishment and the marriage and the flight and the pursuit were finished, were artifacts, were text in a language the shrine did not read because the shrine read only offerings, and the offerings were already counted, and the counting was done.

She told him to go, and the telling was the hardest arithmetic she had ever performed — harder than the counting of weeks after the first missed course, harder than the calculation of which lie to offer which courtier on which occasion, harder than the long division of her life into the public portion and the private portion, the duchess-portion and the wife-portion, the two sums that had never reconciled and now, with the banishment's ink still wet in whatever registry the Cardinal maintained, would never need to reconcile again, because the public portion had been subtracted entirely, leaving only the private, and the private was what she was now dividing, with her own mouth, with her own words, sending the larger part of it away.

Antonio stood at the table where maps had been spread earlier that morning, maps of roads leading north and west, roads that passed through territories governed by men who might or might not honour the Cardinal's displeasure, and the maps were still spread, and his hand was still on them, one finger resting on a road that led to Milan, and the finger did not move when she spoke, because the body processes instruction more slowly than the ear receives it, and for a moment — a moment she would hold in the space behind her ribs for whatever time remained to her, which she did not know was short, which she suspected was short, which she calculated was short with the same faculty that had once calculated household accounts and found, in the calculating, that she was better at the work than the steward she had hired to do it, the steward she had then married, the steward whose finger was on the road to Milan and whose face was performing the particular operation of a man who understands what he is being told and has not yet decided whether to resist the understanding — for that moment, the room held all of them in a single frame: herself, Antonio, the boy playing near the window with a bit of cord he had found on the harbour stairs, the cord looped and relooped into shapes the boy called animals but which resembled nothing except the cord itself, rearranged, the way the family was about to be rearranged, the constituent parts remaining the same — mother, father, child — but the arrangement changing, the proximity dissolving, the cord that held them in the shape of a family about to be pulled from both ends until the shape was lost.

Take the boy, she said.

She said it simply because there was no complex way to say it that would make it easier and no simple way that would make it harder, and the simplicity was its own kind of mercy, the way a clean cut is a mercy compared to a ragged one, though both cuts bleed, and this one would bleed, she knew, for a long time, perhaps for whatever time she had, which she did not know, which she suspected.

Antonio's finger left the map. He looked at her. The looking was the thing she had married — not the competence, not the horsemanship Delio had praised, not the mind that kept accounts with the precision of a man who understood that numbers were the language in which truth could not be evaded, though it could be delayed, and he had delayed certain truths, certain numbers, and she had helped him delay them, and the delay was ending now, here, in this room that smelled of harbour salt and other people's cooking and the particular staleness of temporary habitation, the smell of a place that knows it is being used and not loved. The looking was what she had married, and the looking was what she was sending away.

He began to speak. She let him begin and then she continued over his beginning, not because what he would say was wrong but because what he would say was an argument, and the argument would be that they should remain together, and the argument would be correct, morally correct, matrimonially correct, correct by every measure except the measure that mattered, which was survival — not hers; she had stopped calculating her own survival with the precision the calculation required, had begun rounding down, estimating, accepting approximations that a careful accountant would reject — but his, and the boy's, the boy at the window who had found in a piece of harbour cord the raw material for a world, a boy who was three years old and heir to a duchy he had been banished from and son to a steward who had been publicly named in the instrument of banishment and was therefore marked, visible, findable, and the findability was what she was trying to subtract from the situation by sending him north, sending him away, sending him to Milan where friends might shelter him or might not, where the Cardinal's reach might extend or might not, where the odds — she was calculating odds now, the gambler's arithmetic, which is not the accountant's arithmetic but is adjacent to it, both disciplines requiring a tolerance for unfavourable numbers — where the odds were better than here, in Ancona, where the banishment had landed and where, she understood with the understanding of a woman who had governed a territory and knew what governing meant, where the next instrument would land too, the instrument that was not banishment but something worse, something the play's structure was already preparing though she could not see the structure, only the shadow it cast, and the shadow was long, and it pointed toward her, and it pointed toward everyone standing near her, and the best thing she could do for the people she loved was to ensure they were not standing near her when the shadow arrived.

Milan, she said. Go to Milan. Take the eldest with you.

The boy looked up from his cord. He had heard his designation — the eldest, a word he recognized not as a title but as one of the names adults used for him, the way they sometimes called him *boy* or *child* or *your son* depending on which adult was speaking and to whom, and the word had pulled his attention from the cord the way a tug on a fishing line pulls the line taut without necessarily catching anything, and his attention was taut now, directed at his mother, whose face he read with the literacy children possess and adults lose, the literacy of emotion unmediated by language, and what he read there was something he did not have a word for, something that lived in the register between the things his mother said and the things his mother's face said when she was saying them, a register he navigated by instinct, the way a small boat navigates by current, and the current was pulling in a direction he did not understand, toward a place he could feel but not name, the place where parents go when they are about to do something that will change the shape of the family, and the boy felt the shape changing but did not know the new shape, and not knowing, returned to his cord, because the cord was reliable, the cord maintained the shapes he gave it, and the shapes were animals, he insisted they were animals, and no one corrected him because no one had the time or the cruelty to correct a child whose world was about to be rearranged in ways the child's grammar could not parse.

Antonio folded the map. The folding was consent. She watched his hands — the hands she had held in the chamber at Amalfi when she had placed the ring on his finger per verba de praesenti, the hands that had trembled then and did not tremble now, because then the thing being ventured was joy, and joy makes the hands tremble, and now the thing being ventured was separation, and separation steadies the hands, gives them tasks, gives them maps to fold and bags to pack and a boy to lift onto a horse, and the tasks are what carry a person through the moment that would otherwise be untraversable, the way a bridge carries a person over water that would otherwise be impassable, and the tasks were the bridge, and Antonio was crossing it, one practical action at a time.

She told him what money she could spare. The sum was specific — she had counted it that morning, coins sorted by denomination on the bed they had shared, the coins occupying the space their bodies had occupied, and the substitution was precise and ugly, metal where flesh had been, currency where intimacy had been, and she had counted the coins twice because the first count had been interrupted by the knowledge of what the counting was for, and the interruption had introduced an error she could not afford, not at this margin, not with numbers this small.

He would travel light. He would travel as a man of no particular importance, a steward displaced, a functionary between posts, and the travelling-as would be its own disguise, the way her pilgrimage to Loretto had been a disguise, though her disguise had failed, had been transparent to the brother who read not faces but obedience and found, in the pilgrimage's excessive piety, the very disobedience the piety was meant to conceal. Antonio's disguise might hold. Might. She was trafficking in *might* now, the conditional tense, the mood of uncertainty, which was the only mood available to a woman whose indicative — *I am Duchess of Amalfi* — had been revoked by a document she had not been permitted to contest and would not have contested if permitted, because contesting required a jurisdiction, and the jurisdiction was her brothers', and her brothers' jurisdiction was the thing she had been fleeing since the morning she had placed the ring on Antonio's finger and understood, in the placing, that the ring was also a key, and the key opened a door, and the door led to a corridor, and the corridor led eventually here, to this room, to this parting, to this arithmetic of coins on a bedspread and a map folded into the pocket of a man who was about to walk out of the room and take her son with him.

She knelt to the boy. The kneeling brought her face level with his face, and the levelling was necessary because what she was about to do required equality, required that she meet him at his height, in his grammar, where the words were simple and the cord was an animal and the parting was a trip, a journey, an adventure with Father, and the adventure was the word she used, the word she chose from the limited vocabulary of deceptions available to a mother sending her child away — *adventure* — and the boy received the word the way he received all words from his mother, with the trust that was not yet conditional, not yet earned or tested but simply given, the way sunlight is given, without negotiation, without contract, and the trust was what made the deception possible and what made the deception monstrous and what made the deception necessary, all three at once, and she held all three in her body as she kissed his forehead and stood.

Antonio was at the door. He had his hand on the frame. The hand on the frame was the last of a sequence of gestures she was committing to the storage her body maintained for things the mind could not safely hold — the hand on the map, the hand folding the map, the hand accepting the coins, the hand on the frame — and the sequence was a sentence in the language of departure, each gesture a word, and the sentence was almost complete, and when it was complete he would be gone, and the room would contain only her, and Cariola, and the two younger children sleeping in the next room, and the harbour sounds, and the salt air, and the absence, and the absence would be the largest thing in the room, larger than the furniture, larger than the walls, larger than the view of the Adriatic which was grey and moving and indifferent, which had been grey and moving and indifferent when she arrived and would be grey and moving and indifferent when she was taken, because the sea does not adjust itself to the affairs of people who stand at its edge and make decisions that will determine whether they live or die; the sea continues; the sea is what continues.

Go, she said.

He went. The boy went with him, the cord still in his hand, the cord's animal shapes bobbing as the boy walked, his small stride matching his father's longer stride in the ratio of three to one, three small steps for each of Antonio's, and the ratio diminished as they moved down the corridor, the sounds of their walking thinning the way sound thins over water, and then the corridor turned, and the turn took them, and they were gone.

She stood at the window. Ancona was below her, and below Ancona the harbour, and below the harbour the sea, and the sea was a surface on which, if she watched long enough, she might eventually see a sail carrying the two of them north, though she knew she would not see it because they would travel by road, not by water, and because the watching was not practical but devotional, the last rite of a woman whose devotions had purchased nothing at Loretto and would purchase nothing here but who performed them anyway, because the performing was what she had, and what she had was what she would use, and she would use it until it was taken from her, which she knew — which she calculated, rounding down — would be soon.

She was still at the window when the light changed. The light changed because time had passed, and time passes whether observed or not, but she had observed it, had watched it move across the harbour stones the way she had watched everything that afternoon — with the attention of a woman who suspects she is memorising, who suspects that the present tense is converting itself into the past tense even as she inhabits it, and the conversion is irreversible, and the harbour and the light and the salt air and the absence are already artefacts, already the material of a memory she will carry into whatever room she is taken to next, and the memory will be this: the window, the light, the sea, the sound of three small steps for every one large step, diminishing down a corridor, turning, gone.

He found them on the road south of Ancona where the road stopped being a road and became a suggestion — a track worn by goatherds and by people who did not wish to be found, which was itself a kind of announcement, because the people who take the goatherd's track are the people who believe they are being followed, and the belief is correct more often than not, and in this case the belief was correct, and Bosola was the proof of its correctness, walking the same track with six armed men behind him and the commission folded in his doublet where it pressed against his ribs like a second heartbeat, which it was, in a sense, because the commission was what animated him, what moved his legs along this track in the late-afternoon light that fell across the Marche countryside in the particular amber that makes even ugly country beautiful and beautiful country unbearable, and this country was beautiful, hills descending toward the coast in long green folds like cloth being gathered by an unseen hand, and the beauty was unbearable, and Bosola bore it because bearing unbearable things was the skill his years in the galleys had taught him, the single competence he had carried out of that service and into this one, this service that was not different from the galleys in any respect that mattered, the oar having been replaced by the errand and the chain by the commission and the overseer by Ferdinand, who was not here but whose presence was here, folded into the paper against Bosola's ribs, Ferdinand's hand in Ferdinand's ink making Ferdinand's demand, which was Bosola's action, the two connected by the mechanism the age provided for converting a duke's desire into a steward's motion, the mechanism called employment, called service, called — if one were honest, and Bosola was honest the way a man with a wound is honest about the wound, not because honesty is a virtue but because the wound does not permit the alternative — called corruption.

He saw them before they saw him. The Duchess was walking. This was the first thing he registered and the thing that stayed: she was walking, not riding, because there was no horse, or the horse had been left behind, or the horse had been sold for the coins a horse brings when sold in haste by a person whose haste is legible and whose legibility reduces the price, and the absence of the horse was the detail that told him everything the commission had not told him, which was that the situation had already collapsed past the point where his intervention was structurally necessary, that he was arriving at a ruin and claiming it for the man who had caused it, the way a bailiff arrives at a foreclosed property and affixes a notice to a door that is already hanging from one hinge.

She was walking, and beside her walked Cariola, and between them and slightly behind, the way moons orbit a planet that is itself falling, moved two small children whose ages Bosola calculated — because calculating was the habit, the professional habit, the habit of the intelligencer who converts observation into report and report into payment and payment into the continued fact of his own existence, which was the chain, which was the mechanism, which was the thing he had agreed to when he accepted Ferdinand's offer in the gallery at Amalfi where the light had been different from this light, interior rather than exterior, candle rather than amber, and the difference in light did not change the nature of the agreement, only the quality of the shadow it cast — whose ages he calculated at two years and perhaps one year, the younger carried intermittently by Cariola and set down when Cariola's arms required relief, and the setting-down and the picking-up had the rhythm of a practice long established, the rhythm of women who have been travelling with children for longer than the children can sustain.

The Duchess's dress was not a duchess's dress. It was the dress of a woman who had dressed for the purpose of not being recognized as herself, and the not-being-recognized had failed in the way that such failures always fail, which is to say it had succeeded with everyone except the one person from whom success was required, and Bosola was that person, and the failure was therefore total. He knew her walk. He had studied it across months at Amalfi, the particular carriage she maintained that was not pride and not performance but something closer to a structural fact, the way a pillar bears weight not by deciding to bear it but by being what it is, and she was what she was even in the wrong dress on the wrong road with no horse and two children and Cariola's arms failing and the amber light turning everything beautiful and unbearable, and she was bearing it, the way she bore everything, which was with the posture of a woman who had been Duchess of Amalfi before the banishment and would be Duchess of Amalfi after it, because the title was not a thing that had been given to her and could therefore be taken from her but a thing that she was, and the being preceded the title and would survive it, and Bosola saw this, and the seeing was the thing that made what he was about to do monstrous and what made him do it anyway, because the commission was against his ribs and the commission did not accommodate what he saw, the commission accommodated only what Ferdinand required, and what Ferdinand required was the capture, and the capture was what Bosola was here to perform.

He stepped onto the road. The stepping was the act. Everything before the stepping had been surveillance, which is a form of contemplation, which is a form of delay, and the delay had been the last margin of space between the Bosola who watched and the Bosola who acted, and the stepping eliminated the margin, and the elimination was permanent, and he knew it was permanent in the way a man knows a cut is permanent — not because the wound will not heal but because the body that heals will be a different body from the body that was uncut, scarred where it was smooth, and the scar is what the body carries forward as the record of what was done to it, or in this case, what it did.

She saw him. The seeing was brief — a flicker of her gaze registering his shape on the path ahead, the shape resolving from stranger to known, from known to named, from named to understood, and the understanding arriving not gradually but all at once, the way water does not gradually become ice but crosses a threshold and is ice, and she crossed the threshold and understood, and the understanding changed her posture not at all, which was the thing that made her what she was and what made what he was doing to her what it was.

Bosola, she said.

The name in her mouth had the weight of a verdict. Not his verdict on her — hers on him. She had spoken his name and in the speaking had measured him and found the measurement complete, the full dimension of what he was and what he had been doing at Amalfi and what the apricots had been and what the kindnesses had been and what the provisorship of the horse had been, all of it now legible in the single fact of his presence on this road with armed men behind him, and the legibility was total, and she had read it in the time it took to say his name, two syllables, the first stressed, the second falling, the stress and the fall a perfect miniature of the action itself — the stress of discovery, the fall of capture.

He did not bow. The not-bowing was deliberate. A bow would have been an acknowledgment of her rank, and the acknowledgment of her rank would have made the capture a different kind of act — an act performed against someone whose rank the performer recognized, which would have made the act a violation, an offense against degree, and Bosola could not afford the act to be a violation because if it was a violation then he was the violator and the violator was the agent and the agent was the one who chose, and he had not chosen, he had been commissioned, and the commission was the thing that stood between him and the full weight of what he was doing, the commission was the membrane, thin as paper, thin as the paper folded against his ribs, that separated the act from the man performing it, and the membrane was all he had, and he would not puncture it with a bow.

Madam, he said, I am come to escort you.

The word *escort* performed the work of concealment the way her pilgrim's dress had performed it — badly, transparently, with the failure visible to everyone present including the person performing the concealment — and she received the word the way she received everything, which was with the clarity of a woman who had governed a territory and understood that language is the first instrument of governance and the first instrument of subjection and that the difference between the two uses is the difference between who holds the instrument and who is held by it, and the instrument had changed hands, and she was held.

Cariola moved. The movement was the movement of a woman who believes that physical intervention is possible, that a body placed between a threat and its object can alter the trajectory of the threat, and the belief was wrong but the movement was brave, and the bravery was irrelevant, and the irrelevance was what made the scene what it was — a scene in which bravery had no purchase, in which the virtues available to the participants had been rendered inoperative by the arithmetic of six armed men and one commission and one duke's desire, the arithmetic that Bosola had performed before stepping onto the road, the arithmetic that always yields the same answer, which is that power is a quantity and the greater quantity prevails, and the prevailing is not justice and not injustice but simply the result, the way a sum is simply the result.

The older child pressed against the Duchess's leg. The pressing was the child's calculation, performed in the child's arithmetic, which lacked numbers but possessed the single axiom that the mother's body is the safest location, and the axiom was wrong, had been wrong since Ancona, had been wrong since Loretto, had been wrong since the Cardinal read the banishment from a document whose preparation had been more careful than anything the Cardinal had prepared for God, and the wrongness of the axiom was the thing the child would learn, was learning, was in the process of learning as the armed men moved to either side of the road and the road became a thing that could not be walked in either direction, a corridor without doors, a channel whose current ran in one direction, and the direction was back, back to Amalfi, back to the brothers, back to the consequence that the Duchess had been fleeing and that had now, here, on this road that was not a road, in this light that was amber and unbearable, caught her.

She did not run. He had calculated the probability that she would run and arrived at a number so small it was effectively zero, because running was an act available to a person who believed that distance could be established between herself and the thing pursuing her, and she had ceased to believe in distance — the distance between Amalfi and Ancona had not held, the distance between Ancona and this road had not held, and no further distance would hold, and the knowledge that no distance would hold was visible in her stillness, her not-running, her standing on the track with her children and her woman and the amber light on her face and the hills descending toward a coast she would not reach.

He gave the order. The giving was quiet, a word to the nearest man, and the word was passed and the men moved and the movement was the machinery engaging, the same machinery that had engaged when he accepted the commission in the gallery, the same machinery that had engaged when he offered the apricots, the same machinery that had engaged when he composed the letters to Ferdinand, each engagement a gear turning the next gear turning the next, and the final gear was this, armed men on a goatherd's track forming an escort that was not an escort around a duchess who was still a duchess around children who did not understand and a waiting-woman whose bravery had been noted and disregarded.

Bosola walked behind them. The walking-behind was the position of the captor, the driver, the man who ensures the direction, and the direction was determined, and the determination had been made in a room he had never entered by a man whose face he could see even now, Ferdinand's face as it had been in the gallery, the face performing the twin operations of authority and need, the authority sufficient to command and the need too opaque to name, and Bosola had not named it then and would not name it now because naming it would have required him to understand what Ferdinand wanted with his sister, and understanding what Ferdinand wanted with his sister would have required Bosola to understand what he, Bosola, was an instrument of, and the understanding would have dissolved the membrane, the paper-thin commission between himself and the act, and the membrane was all he had.

The Duchess walked ahead of him. She did not look back. The not-looking-back was the last exercise of her authority — the authority to choose what she would see and what she would not, the authority of the eye, which is the last authority to be revoked because even a prisoner chooses where to look, and she chose not to look at him, and the choosing was a governance, a small governance, the governance of a woman whose duchy had been reduced to the territory of her own gaze, and within that territory she was still sovereign, and within that sovereignty she would not grant him the acknowledgment of her attention.

He watched her walk. Her carriage had not changed. The pillar bore its weight. The amber light fell on the hills and on the road and on the small procession moving along the road, and the light did not distinguish between captor and captured, between instrument and object, between the man who had stepped onto the road and the woman who had been walking on it, the light fell equally on all of them because light is not moral, light is physical, and the physical world continued around the capture the way the sea had continued around the harbour at Ancona, indifferent, amber, unbearable.

The Duchess's concealment strategy — the secret marriage, the feigned pilgrimage, the flight, the separation of husband and eldest son, the goatherd's track instead of the road, the wrong dress, the walking instead of riding — the entire architecture of concealment that had been built over years and across territories and through the expenditure of every resource available to a woman of her intelligence and position — collapsed, here, on this track, in this light, in the time it took Bosola to step from the verge onto the path and say *Madam*, collapsed not partially but entirely, the way a building does not partially collapse but entirely collapses once the load-bearing wall is breached, and the load-bearing wall had been the distance between herself and her brothers' reach, and the distance had been breached, and the building came down, and the building was her freedom, and her freedom was rubble, and Bosola was walking on the rubble, walking behind her as she walked ahead of him toward a destination she could calculate as easily as she calculated everything — the room, the confinement, the brothers' justice, which was not justice — and the calculation was the last calculation she would perform as a free woman, and the answer, like all the answers her arithmetic had been producing since Ancona, rounded down.

The room was her own. That was the refinement — the particular cruelty that bore Ferdinand's signature as legibly as the commission had borne his hand — the room was her own bedchamber at Amalfi, the room where she had governed, the room where she had received petitions and dismissed them, the room where she had stood at the window and looked down at the courtyard where her household moved in patterns she could read the way a navigator reads currents, and the window was still there, and the courtyard was still there, and the household was presumably still there, moving in patterns that were no longer hers to read because the window had been shuttered from the outside, the shutters nailed, the nails driven by someone whose hammer-strokes she had heard from within the room as each stroke sealed the margin between the light and herself, between the world and herself, between what she had been and what she now was, which was a woman in her own bedchamber who could not leave it.

She knew the walls. She knew the dimensions by the body's knowledge, the knowledge that precedes measurement, the knowledge that says *this room is fourteen paces east to west and eleven paces north to south* without the paces being taken, because the paces had been taken a thousand times before, in other circumstances, in the circumstances of governance, the circumstances of dressing, the circumstances — and here the knowledge cut, the way knowledge cuts when it arrives at a fact the knower would prefer not to arrive at — the circumstances of the night she had placed the ring on Antonio's finger and spoken the words that married them, the words that were valid by canon law and catastrophic by every other law, the law of brothers, the law of rank, the law of what a widowed duchess may and may not do with her body and her title and her future, and the words had been spoken in this room, and the ring had been given in this room, and now the room held her the way a reliquary holds a bone — preserving the shape of something that was once alive.

Cariola sat on the floor near the door. The door was locked. The sitting-on-the-floor was not a choice but the consequence of an absence — the absence of permission to sit elsewhere, the absence of any instruction at all, the absence that is itself the first instrument of confinement, because a prisoner who has not been told what is permitted does not know what is forbidden, and the not-knowing creates a paralysis that functions as obedience, which is the point, which is always the point. Cariola sat on the floor and the Duchess stood at the shuttered window and between them the room performed its transformation from bedchamber to cell, the transformation requiring no alteration of furnishing or dimension, requiring only the nails in the shutters and the lock on the door and the armed man she had heard breathing on the other side of the door when the lock engaged, the breathing audible in the silence that followed the engagement, the silence that was not silence but the sound of a house rearranging itself around a new fact, the fact of her imprisonment.

The children were elsewhere. She did not know where. The not-knowing was the second refinement, the second cruelty, the cruelty that operated not on the body but on the organ the body could not shut — the mind, which generated possibilities the way a wound generates blood, continuously, involuntarily, each possibility worse than the last: the children in a room like this one but smaller, the children in a room like this one but darker, the children with a nurse who was not Cariola and who therefore could not be trusted, the children without a nurse at all, the children — and here the mind's production exceeded what the mind could bear, and the Duchess performed the operation she had performed throughout the journey from Ancona, the operation of refusal, the refusal to permit the unbearable thought to complete itself, the amputation of the thought at the joint where it passed from painful to annihilating, and the amputation held, and the thought was incomplete, and the incompleteness was a mercy she administered to herself because no one else would administer it.

She touched the shutter. The wood was the same wood it had been when the shutter opened, when light entered, when the courtyard was visible and the courtyard's visibility was unremarkable because the unremarkable is the condition of freedom — freedom being not the presence of something extraordinary but the absence of obstruction, the absence of nails, the absence of the particular resistance she felt now as she pressed and the shutter did not move, did not yield, held, the holding a fact as physical and as final as a wall, and her hand on the shutter was the hand of a woman confirming what she already knew, which was that the confirmation was unnecessary, which was that the pressing was not an attempt to open but an act of measurement, the measurement of her new condition, the condition being: this room, this lock, this darkness where light had been, this holding where passage had been, this confinement where governance had been.

I am Duchess of Malfi still, she said.

She did not say it to Cariola. She did not say it to the room. She said it to the fact of the imprisonment, which was a fact that required an answer, and the answer was the sentence, and the sentence was not defiance and not despair but something anterior to both — a statement of identity, the kind of statement a wall might make if a wall could speak, the wall saying *I am a wall* not because anyone has questioned whether it is a wall but because the wall's being-a-wall is the fact the wall knows, the fact that precedes all other facts, the fact that survives when all other facts have been stripped away, as they were being stripped now, as they had been stripped since Loretto, title and territory and husband and eldest son and horses and road and direction and light, stripped progressively the way bark is stripped from a tree, and what remained when the bark was gone was not nothing — what remained was the wood, the hard pale wood that is the tree's actual substance, and her actual substance was the sentence she had spoken, the five words, and the five words were sufficient, and the sufficiency was what Ferdinand would not understand when Bosola reported it, because Ferdinand understood titles as things conferred and therefore revocable, and she understood her title as a thing she was, the way the wood is the tree, and the understanding was the difference between them, the difference the twin-bond did not bridge, the difference that had produced the marriage and the concealment and the flight and the capture and now the imprisonment, this room, these shutters, this lock, this breathing man outside the door.

Cariola wept. The weeping was quiet, professional, the weeping of a waiting-woman who has served long enough to know that weeping must be quiet or it becomes a burden on the person served, and Cariola would not burden her, and the not-burdening was a form of service, and the service continued because Cariola was Cariola the way the Duchess was the Duchess, the identity persisting through the confinement the way a river persists through a narrowing of its banks, the water the same water, the motion the same motion, only the width altered, and the width had been altered, and the alteration was the imprisonment, and the imprisonment was now.

The Duchess did not weep. The not-weeping was not strength and not numbness but a third thing — a conservation, a refusal to spend what might be needed later, because she could calculate what was coming the way she had always calculated, and what was coming would require what she had, and what she had was what remained after the stripping, which was herself, which was five words, which was sufficient, which was all.

The light that entered the room entered through the cracks in the shutters — thin lines of the same amber that had fallen on the hills near Ancona, the amber reduced now to filaments, to threads, to the narrowest possible expression of the fact that outside this room the sun continued and the courtyard continued and the world continued, indifferent, amber, present in the cracks the way the future was present in the cracks, visible only as lines, legible only as the promise that something existed on the other side of the wood, something she could not reach, something she could confirm by pressing her hand to the shutter and feeling the warmth the sun transferred through the grain, the warmth the last communication between herself and the exterior, the exterior that had been her duchy and was now her brothers' judgement, and the judgement was the room, and the room was her own, and the cruelty of its being her own was the cruelty she would not grant the name of cruelty, because granting it the name would have been a concession, and she was not making concessions, and the not-making was governance, the last governance, the governance of a woman whose territory had been reduced to the dimensions of her refusal, and within those dimensions she held, and the holding was the thing, and the thing was what she was, and what she was had not changed.

He came in darkness. She knew it was Ferdinand before the door opened because the breathing on the other side of the door changed — the guard's breathing, which had been the breathing of a man standing at a post, became the breathing of a man standing at attention, and the difference between the two breathings was the difference between duty and fear, and only Ferdinand produced that particular fear, and the production of it was audible through the wood of the door, and she heard it, and she knew, and the knowing preceded the opening by several seconds, seconds in which she could have composed herself but did not, because she was already composed, because composition was now her permanent state, the state she had arrived at when the shutters were nailed and the lock engaged and the five words spoken, and the state held, and the holding was what it was.

The door opened onto darkness. She had expected — if expected was the word for the calculations a prisoner performs against possibilities she cannot govern — she had expected light, a candle, a torch, the illumination that accompanies a visit because a visitor requires sight, but Ferdinand brought no light, and the absence of light was deliberate, and the deliberateness was the third refinement, the third cruelty in the series of cruelties that were not random but architectural, each supporting the next the way stones support an arch, and the arch's purpose was legible only when complete, and it was not yet complete, and the incompleteness was itself a cruelty, the cruelty of not knowing what the structure would be when it was finished, and she did not know, and the not-knowing operated on her in the dark the way the dark operated on the room, which was: totally.

Ferdinand. She said it. Not a question. A naming. The way one names a thing that has entered a space — *storm*, *flood*, *fire* — the naming not a greeting but an identification, the identification a form of governance, the governance of saying *I know what this is* even when what this is has not yet declared itself, has not yet spoken, has only entered, a presence she could not see but could locate by the displacement of the air, the air in the room having been still and now being moved, stirred by a body she could not see but could place at approximately five paces from the door, which was approximately nine paces from where she stood at the shuttered window, and the five paces and the nine paces were the geography of the encounter, the encounter occurring in darkness, the darkness Ferdinand's instrument the way Bosola was Ferdinand's instrument, each deployed with the same precision, the same calculation, the same understanding that an instrument is most effective when the person upon whom it is deployed does not fully comprehend its function until the function has been executed.

He spoke. His voice came from where she had placed him, confirming the placement, the confirmation a minor satisfaction in a field of no satisfactions, and the voice said what the voice said, which was a thing about reconciliation, a thing about the hand of brotherhood, the vocabulary of restoration deployed in the dark where vocabulary was all, where the face that accompanied the vocabulary was not visible, where the expression that would have betrayed the vocabulary's falseness was concealed by the absence of light, and the concealment was the reason for the darkness — she understood this as she heard the words, understood it with the understanding that arrives too late to prevent the action it understands, because Ferdinand was already moving, the air already further displaced, and he was closer now, perhaps six paces, perhaps five, and the words continued, the words about peace, about kinship, about the bond between siblings that survives what should destroy it, and the words were correct — the bond did survive — but the survival was not what the words proposed, the survival was not reconciliation but its opposite, the bond surviving as the medium through which the cruelty traveled, the way a wire survives as the medium through which a current travels, the wire not destroyed by the current but defined by it, and the bond between Ferdinand and herself was defined by this, by what he was doing now, which was: extending something toward her in the dark.

Take it, he said. My hand. In token of the love I bear you.

She could not see the hand. The not-seeing was the condition of the scene, the condition Ferdinand had engineered, and in the not-seeing she reached — and the reaching was the act that would, when she understood it fully, become the act she could not undo, the act that joined her to what Ferdinand intended, because a hand extended in darkness to a woman in a dark room who has not been visited, who has been confined, who has heard nothing and known nothing and whose children are elsewhere and whose husband is elsewhere and who has been reduced to a room and a sentence and a set of thin amber filaments through shuttered cracks — a hand extended to such a woman will be taken, because the taking is involuntary, because the body's need for contact precedes the mind's capacity for suspicion, and the body reached before the mind could intervene, and her fingers closed on what was offered.

The hand was cold.

The coldness was not the coldness of a hand that has been outside in winter air, the coldness that dissipates upon contact with warmth, the coldness that is temporary and circulatory and reversible. The coldness was the other coldness — the coldness that does not dissipate, the coldness that is not temporary, the coldness that is the signature of a process that has completed itself and cannot be reversed, and she knew the signature, she had known it when she touched her first husband's hand after his death, the particular waxy absence-of-warmth that is not cold in the way ice is cold but cold in the way stone is cold, the way a thing is cold that was never warm, except that this thing had been warm once and was not warm now, and the not-warm-now was what the hand communicated through her fingers to her mind in the interval between the touching and the understanding, the interval that was perhaps one second and perhaps less than one second and that contained within it the entire distance between what she had believed — that Ferdinand had extended his own hand, his living hand, the hand of her twin — and what was actual, which was that the hand she held was not attached to a living arm, was not attached to anything, was a hand and only a hand, severed, cold, dead, and placed in her grasp by her brother who stood in the darkness and who had engineered the darkness precisely so that she would take what she would not have taken in light.

She did not drop it. The not-dropping was not courage. The not-dropping was the paralysis that occurs when the body receives information the mind cannot yet process, the information traveling from the fingers through the wrist through the arm to the center of comprehension, and the center of comprehension refusing the information, rejecting it, returning it to the fingers with the message *this cannot be what you are telling me*, and the fingers insisting, *this is what we are telling you*, and the argument between the fingers and the comprehension lasting the duration of the not-dropping, which was several seconds, seconds in which she held a dead man's hand in a dark room and her brother stood somewhere near and the guard breathed outside the door and Cariola was somewhere — where was Cariola? — and the question of Cariola's location was a question the mind generated to avoid the question it could not yet ask, which was: whose hand.

Her thumb moved. The movement was not voluntary. The movement was the body's investigation, the investigation the mind had not authorized but could not prevent, the thumb moving across the surface of the dead fingers and finding — the ring.

She knew the ring.

She knew the ring the way she knew the room, by the body's knowledge, the knowledge that precedes thought, the knowledge that is stored not in the mind but in the skin, in the particular nerves of the particular fingertips that had placed the ring on the particular finger where it now sat, the finger that was cold, the finger that was dead, and the ring was the ring she had given Antonio on the night of the marriage, in this room, in the room where she now stood holding the hand that wore it, and the logic was the logic Ferdinand intended her to execute, the logic being: this ring was on Antonio's hand; this hand is severed and dead; therefore Antonio is dead; therefore the marriage that began with the placing of the ring has ended with the holding of the hand that wears it, and the ending has occurred in the same room as the beginning, and the symmetry is Ferdinand's architecture, the arch now nearly complete, the stones supporting one another, the structure legible.

She understood. The understanding arrived not as a thought but as a physical event — a redistribution of weight within the body, the weight shifting from the upright posture of a woman standing at a window to the altered posture of a woman absorbing a blow, the blow not physical but operating on the body as if physical, the knees receiving the information the mind had received and translating it into their own language, which was the language of buckling, and she did not buckle, she held, but the holding cost her something that the five words had not cost, something from a reserve she had not known she was maintaining, the reserve that was Antonio, the reserve that was the knowledge that somewhere outside the shuttered room he existed and the existing was a fact she could hold alongside the five words, the two facts — *I am Duchess of Malfi still* and *Antonio exists* — constituting the total architecture of her endurance, and now one of the facts had been removed, removed by a dead hand in the dark, and the architecture adjusted, and the adjustment was the cost, and the cost was what Ferdinand had come to extract, and the extraction was accomplished, and the hand was still in her hand, and the ring was still on the finger, and the cold was still the cold that does not dissipate.

Ferdinand — she heard him move, the air displaced again, displaced toward the door — Ferdinand was leaving. The leaving was part of the design. The design required that she be alone with the hand, alone with the ring, alone with the knowledge the hand and the ring produced, the knowledge that Antonio was dead, that the hand she held was his, that the future she had constructed — the future in which Antonio reached Milan, reached safety, reached a place from which he would send word and the word would be a thread connecting her imprisonment to his freedom — that future was now the same temperature as the hand, which was the temperature of nothing, of cessation, of the completed process that cannot be reversed.

The door closed. The lock engaged. The breathing resumed on the other side.

She stood in the dark room holding a dead man's hand and the hand wore her ring and the ring was the seal on a contract she had entered in this room in the presence of Cariola and God and no one else, the contract valid by canon law and void now by the law that voids all contracts, the law of death, and she held the hand because she could not yet release it, because releasing it would be the acknowledgment that the hand was no longer connected to the man, and the acknowledgment was a thing she was not yet willing to make, not because she doubted the evidence — the evidence was in her fingers, incontrovertible, cold — but because the willingness to acknowledge requires a moment of transition, a moment in which the mind passes from the state of knowing to the state of having-known, and she was still in the state of knowing, the state in which the knowledge is new and therefore still capable of being experienced as an event rather than as a condition, and the event was still occurring, the event was the coldness and the ring and the dark and the absence of Ferdinand and the presence of the hand, and the event would become a condition, she knew this, the event would harden into the permanent state of having-known-that-Antonio-is-dead, but the hardening had not yet occurred, and in the interval before the hardening she held the hand, and the holding was the last act of the marriage, the last contact between her body and his, his body reduced to this, to a hand, to a cold hand in a dark room, and the reduction was Ferdinand's work, and the work was accomplished, and she held it, and the dark held her, and the room — her room, her cell, the room where she had married and now held the evidence of the marriage's end — the room continued, fourteen paces east to west, eleven paces north to south, unchanged, indifferent, the same room it had always been, the room she knew by the body's knowledge, the knowledge that now included this, the dead hand, the ring, the cold, the knowledge added to the room's dimensions like a final measurement, the measurement of what the room could contain, which was: everything Ferdinand intended, and nothing she could prevent.

She set the hand down. She set it on the table near the window — the table where she had signed documents as Duchess, the table now receiving this — and the setting-down was precise, careful, the care not tenderness and not revulsion but the care one takes with evidence, because the hand was evidence, evidence of what Ferdinand was capable of, evidence she had not previously possessed in this form, in this irrefutable, tactile, cold form, and the evidence was now on the table, and her hands were empty, and the emptiness was the new condition, the condition that had hardened while she set the hand down, and the condition was: Antonio is dead, and I am Duchess of Malfi still, and the two facts do not contradict but the second fact now carries a weight it did not carry before, the weight of being the only fact, the sole architecture, the single remaining pillar where there had been two, and the single pillar held, and the holding was the thing, and the thing was what she was, and what she was had not changed, and the not-changing was the defiance, and the defiance was quiet, and the quiet was the sound the room made around the dead hand on the table and the living woman standing at the shuttered window and the darkness that did not distinguish between them.

Light came. After the darkness — after the hand, after the night that followed the hand, the night in which she did not sleep but stood or sat or moved between standing and sitting in the dark room that contained her and the hand on the table and the ring on the hand and the knowledge the ring confirmed — after that night, light came, and the coming of light was the fourth cruelty, because the light was not restoration, the light was not mercy, the light was the next stone in the arch, the arch whose completion she still could not predict but whose method she now understood, the method being: each cruelty prepares the conditions for the next, the darkness preparing for the light the way the silence had prepared for the hand, each element meaningless alone and devastating in sequence, and the sequence was Ferdinand's, the sequence was the mind of her twin operating upon her through instruments — through Bosola, through darkness, through a dead hand, and now through light, light admitted not through the shutters, which remained nailed, but through the door, the door opened by hands she could not see because the light came from behind the door, from the corridor, and the light entering the room was the light of revelation, the light that permits sight, and the permitting of sight after the enforced darkness was itself a declaration: *now you will see*.

She did not move toward the light. The not-moving was not reluctance and not defiance but the stillness of a creature that has learned, in the interval between one cruelty and the next, that movement is collaboration, that reaching toward what is offered is the mechanism by which the offering achieves its purpose — she had reached for the hand, she had taken the hand, the taking had been the mechanism — and she would not reach again, she would not move toward what was presented, she would receive it where she stood, at the window, the shuttered window through which thin amber filaments still entered as they had always entered, and if Ferdinand required her to see, Ferdinand would bring the seeing to her.

Ferdinand did not enter. The not-entering was a modification of the previous scene's architecture — previously he had entered, had been present in the dark, his voice and his breathing and his proximity constituting the terms of the encounter — and now the modification was: absence. Ferdinand absent. The light present. The light doing what Ferdinand's presence had done, which was to create the conditions under which the next element of the design could operate, and the next element was not auditory and not tactile but visual, visual because the light permitted vision, and vision was the sense Ferdinand now required, required after having systematically denied it, the denial and the restoration forming a pattern she recognized, the pattern of interrogation, the pattern of *first we remove, then we restore, and the restoration is worse than the removal because the restoration reveals what the removal concealed*, and what had been concealed was now revealed, revealed by the light from the corridor falling across a curtain she had not previously noticed — or had she noticed it? had it always been there? the curtain hanging in the far wall, the wall opposite the window, a curtain that in the darkness had been indistinguishable from the wall, had been wall, had been simply another surface of the room's fourteen-by-eleven containment, and now was not wall but curtain, and the curtain was being drawn, drawn by a hand she could see at the curtain's edge, a hand that was not Ferdinand's, a hand that was — Bosola's, it was Bosola, she saw the edge of his sleeve, the functional fabric of his provisorship, the sleeve retreating as the curtain retreated, the curtain drawn to the side and held to the side and the space behind the curtain exposed to the light from the corridor and to her eyes which were adjusting, which had been in darkness for — how long? she did not know, the duration of the darkness was a quantity she could not calculate, the calculation requiring a reference point she did not possess, the reference point of daylight, of routine, of the ordinary passage that marks time for people who are not imprisoned in dark rooms holding dead hands — her eyes adjusting and the adjustment itself a form of pain because the adjustment was the mechanism by which she would see what Ferdinand intended her to see, and the seeing was the function, and the function was being executed, and she could not prevent it, she could not close her eyes because the body that had reached for the hand was the same body that now demanded sight, the body's need to see preceding the mind's capacity to refuse, and the eyes opened fully, and adjusted fully, and saw.

Behind the curtain was a space — a shallow alcove, a recess in the wall she had not known existed, and the not-knowing was part of the design, part of the room's secret architecture now disclosed — and in the space, arranged with the precision of a tableau, with the deliberateness of a scene composed for viewing, were figures.

Antonio.

The word arrived before the seeing was complete, arrived as the mind's identification of what the eyes were reporting, the eyes reporting a form, a human form, recumbent, arranged — and the arrangement was the arrangement of death, the arrangement one makes with a body that is no longer occupying itself, the body laid out, the limbs composed, the hands — the hands — and here the seeing collided with the previous knowing, the knowing of the severed hand, and the collision produced a derangement, a momentary fracture in the sequence of perception, because the hand on the table behind her and the hands on the figure before her could not coexist, the arithmetic could not work, a man has two hands and one was behind her on the table and two were before her on the figure, the arithmetic producing three hands, and three hands is not a quantity a man possesses, and the fracture lasted the duration of the arithmetic and resolved into a possibility she did not pursue, could not pursue, because the figure's face was Antonio's face — the jaw she had traced with her fingers in this room, the brow she had touched with her lips in this room, the specific arrangement of bone beneath skin that constituted the face she had chosen, the face she had married, and the face was still, the stillness not the stillness of sleep but the stillness of the hand, the same stillness, the same signature, the same completed process — and the seeing of the face produced in her body the same event the hand had produced, the redistribution of weight, the shift from upright to absorbing-a-blow, and again she held, and the holding cost her from the same reserve, the reserve she had thought already depleted, and it was not depleted, and the not-depleted was itself a discovery, the discovery that there is always more to take, that the reserve replenishes itself precisely so that it can be drawn down again, and the replenishment is not mercy but the condition of continued suffering, because suffering requires something to suffer with, and the body provides it, the body refusing the mind's assessment that there is nothing left.

And the children.

Two smaller figures. Arranged beside the figure that was — that she understood to be — Antonio. Two smaller forms in the attitudes of death, in the composed positions of children who have been laid out, and the laying-out performed with the same precision as the larger figure, the same attention to the arrangement of limbs, the same quality of stillness, and she saw them and the seeing was the function and the function was complete, complete in the sense that the information had traveled from the eyes to the center of comprehension and the center of comprehension had received it and the receiving was the thing Ferdinand had engineered — not the hand alone, not the hand and the ring alone, but the hand and the ring and now this, the figures, the tableau, the visual confirmation of what the hand had initiated, the full scope of the destruction now displayed behind a curtain in a room where she had married and borne children and governed a duchy and done the things a living woman does in a living room, and the room was not living now, the room was a display case, a cabinet of calculated horrors, and the figures in the cabinet were the final exhibit, and the exhibit was: your family, dead, arranged for your viewing, presented in light after darkness so that the seeing is sharper, so that the eyes which have been denied sight now receive sight as a wound, and the wound is the purpose, and the purpose is accomplished.

She looked. The looking was sustained. The looking was not the glance-and-turn-away that self-preservation demands, the glance that takes enough information to confirm the worst and then retreats to the safety of not-looking, the safety of the wall, the shuttered window, the amber filaments — she did not retreat. She looked at the figures with the attention she had given to documents of state, with the scrutiny she had applied to contracts and treaties and the instruments of governance, because these figures were also instruments, and the instruments required reading, and the reading was: the face of Antonio as she had known it, now absent of the thing that made it Antonio's face rather than the face of any man, the particular animation, the particular readiness of expression that distinguished the living face from the — from this. And the children, the two younger children, the children who had been with her and Cariola when Bosola intercepted them in the countryside, the children she had not been able to protect, the children whose absence from the room she had registered as a separate imprisonment, their imprisonment elsewhere, and now the elsewhere was here, the elsewhere was behind a curtain, the elsewhere was death, and the death was displayed, and the display was the stone that completed the arch.

She understood the arch now. The arch was: first, confinement; second, darkness; third, the hand; fourth, the light; fifth, this. The progression from isolation to sensory deprivation to tactile horror to visual horror, each step building on the previous, each requiring the previous for its full effect, and the full effect was not madness — Ferdinand did not want madness, or perhaps Ferdinand wanted madness, she could not determine Ferdinand's want, she could only determine the effect, and the effect was not madness but something else, something she did not have a name for, something that was located between grief and understanding, between the experience of loss and the comprehension of the mechanism that produced the loss, the comprehension that her twin brother had constructed this sequence with the patience and precision of an architect, that the construction was his art, that the art was practiced upon her, and that the practice was — what? What was the purpose of art? The purpose of art was — she did not know the purpose of Ferdinand's art. She knew only that she was its material, its subject, its medium, and its audience, and that the fourfold occupation of these roles was the condition of her imprisonment, the condition more thorough than the locked door, more complete than the nailed shutters, more absolute than the four walls.

Bosola stood at the edge of the curtain, holding it open. She could see him now, partially, the light from the corridor catching one side of his face, and his face was the face of a man performing a function, the function being: hold the curtain, display the exhibit, serve the design. And the face was not expressionless — the face contained something, some quality she could not identify from this distance, some registration of what he was doing and what he was displaying and what she was seeing — but the something was not relevant, the something did not alter the function, and the function continued, and the curtain remained open, and the figures remained visible, and she remained looking, and the looking continued because the looking was all she had, the looking was the exercise of the only faculty Ferdinand had restored to her, the faculty of sight, and the exercise of the faculty was simultaneously its punishment, and the punishment was: see this, see what has been done, see the dead face of the man you married and the dead faces of the children you bore, see them arranged behind a curtain in the room where you married him, see the symmetry, understand the architecture, comprehend the arch.

I am Duchess of Malfi still.

The five words held. The five words did not waver and did not crack and did not require amendment. The five words were what they had been — the total architecture of her endurance, now the sole architecture, the single pillar, the pillar that had carried weight before and now carried more weight, the additional weight of the figures behind the curtain, the weight of the wax-pale faces she could not look away from and did not look away from, and the pillar held, and the holding was the defiance, and the defiance was not spoken aloud, not this time, the defiance held silently the way the hand had been held in the darkness, held because the body could not yet release it, because releasing it would be the acknowledgment that there was nothing left to hold, and there was something left to hold — there was this, the five words, the identity they named, the fact of her, the continuing, unalterable, irreducible fact of her — and the fact held, and she stood at the shuttered window and looked at the figures and the figures did not move and the light from the corridor illuminated them with the indifference of light, which falls on the living and the dead without distinction, without preference, without mercy, and the light was Ferdinand's instrument the way the darkness had been Ferdinand's instrument, each deployed in turn, each accomplishing what the other could not, and the accomplishment was complete, and the curtain remained open, and she remained, and the five words remained, and the room — the room continued, containing all of it, the dead hand on the table and the dead figures behind the curtain and the living woman at the window, containing without comment, without judgment, without the capacity for either, the room merely the space in which these things occurred, the space Ferdinand had converted from a court of governance to a theater of atrocity, the conversion complete, the theater open, the audience of one watching from the shuttered window, watching because watching was all that was left, and the watching was endurance, and the endurance was the thing, and the thing was her, and she held.

They came as sound before they came as bodies.

The sound entered the room the way the light had entered — through the door, from the corridor, preceding its source — but the sound was not the clean, interpretable signal the light had been. The light had communicated: *now you will see*. The sound communicated nothing she could parse. The sound was — voices, but not voices in the sense she understood voices, not voices organized around speech, around the conveyance of meaning from one mind to another. These were voices organized around some other principle, some principle she could not identify, and the not-identifying was the first register of what was coming, because she had always been able to identify, she had governed a duchy by identification, by the parsing of signals into sense, the conversion of sound and document and human behavior into legible patterns, and now the sounds from the corridor resisted conversion, resisted parsing, and the resistance was not accidental.

Cariola moved. Cariola had been in the corner near the table — the table from which the hand had been removed at some point she could not specify, removed the way props are cleared between scenes, the clearing performed by unseen hands in the intervals when the room was dark and the door was closed and the production was between its acts — Cariola had been in the corner and now Cariola moved toward her, toward the window, the movement not deliberate but instinctive, the movement of a body seeking proximity to the only other body in the room that was alive and familiar, and the seeking was itself information, because Cariola's body knew something about the sounds that Cariola's mind had not yet articulated, Cariola's body was responding before Cariola could speak, and the response was: *closer to you, closer to the center, away from the door*.

The sounds organized. Not into sense — into pattern. A rhythm emerged, or had been there from the beginning and was now discernible, a rhythm that was not music and not speech but something between, something that occupied the space between music and speech the way the room occupied the space between a chamber and a theater, and the rhythm accelerated, and the acceleration was an approach, the sounds growing louder not because they were increasing in intensity but because they were traveling, they were coming closer, they were in the corridor, they were moving toward the door that had admitted light and now would admit — what? What was the next element? After the hand, after the figures, what was the next stone in the arch?

The door opened fully and they entered.

Eight. She counted them because counting was the last instrument of governance she possessed, the last application of the administrative faculty that had organized the duchy's revenues and treaties and appointments, and the faculty applied itself to what entered the room: eight figures, eight bodies, eight masked faces, the masks not the carnival masks of court entertainment, not the gilt and feathered constructions she had seen at revels in Amalfi and in her brothers' courts, but masks of a different order, masks that were — wrong. The masks were wrong the way the sounds were wrong, organized around a principle she could not identify, and the principle was: these are not performers wearing the faces of characters, these are madmen wearing the faces of their conditions, and the conditions were painted on the masks or carved into the masks or simply present in the masks the way a wound is present in flesh, not applied but intrinsic, and the eight masked faces entered the room and the room, which had been a chamber and then a theater of atrocity, became something else, became a space she had no name for, a space in which the rules of containment no longer applied in the way she had understood them to apply.

They moved. The movement was choreographed — she could see the choreography, could see the structure beneath the apparent chaos the way she had always been able to see structure, the structure of a duchy's governance, the structure of a marriage concealed within a household, the structure of a flight devised as a pilgrimage — and the choreography was: each of the eight moved according to a private logic visible only to that body, and the eight private logics operated simultaneously in the fourteen-by-eleven space, and the simultaneity produced what appeared to be disorder but was not disorder, was the superimposition of eight separate orders, each coherent alone and incoherent together, and the incoherence was the instrument, the incoherence was what Ferdinand had commissioned, and the commission was: *bring eight separate insanities into her room and let them perform their separateness in her presence, and the performance will be the next stone*.

One of them spoke. The speaking was directed at her or at the room or at the condition the speaker inhabited, the condition painted on the mask, and the speech was — she heard it, she received the words as discrete units of language, each word intelligible in isolation: *liver*, *glass-house*, *soap-boiler*, *anatomy* — words she knew, words that belonged to the shared vocabulary of the living, but the assembly of the words was governed by the private logic, and the private logic was not shared, and the not-sharing was the point, the point being: *you are in a room with eight minds that do not share your logic, and your logic — the logic of governance, of concealment, of marriage, of motherhood, of endurance — your logic is one logic among nine, and the other eight are as certain of their logics as you are of yours, and the certainty is indistinguishable, and the indistinguishability is the torture*.

Another one sang. The singing was a song she almost recognized, a song that had been a song before being processed through the singer's condition, the condition altering the melody the way Ferdinand's design altered the room, each element still present — notes, intervals, the ghost of a lyric — but reorganized, reorganized so that the recognition was possible but the completion of the recognition was not, the mind reaching for the familiar and finding the familiar deformed, not destroyed but deformed, and the deformation was worse than destruction because destruction is final and deformation is ongoing, deformation is the familiar continuing in its altered state, and the song continued, and the deformation continued.

Cariola pressed against her arm. Cariola's body was trembling, and the trembling was the response her own body had not produced, the response her body had learned not to produce in the interval between the hand and the figures and now the figures and this, the interval in which the body had been re-educated, taught that trembling is expenditure and expenditure is depletion and depletion is what Ferdinand requires, Ferdinand requires the depletion of the reserve, and she would not deplete, and her body complied with the refusal, her body standing at the shuttered window without trembling while eight bodies circled and lurched and postured and sang and spoke in the space between her and the curtain that still hung where it had hung over the alcove where the wax figures had been displayed, and the eight bodies moved through the space where the light had fallen on the figures' wax-pale faces, and the moving through that space was itself a kind of desecration, the dancing over the site of the display, and the desecration was part of the design, every element part of the design.

One of them approached her. The approach was not threatening in the way a weapon is threatening, not the approach of a body intending harm to another body, but the approach of a body governed by its private logic, the logic directing it toward her the way the logic directed the others toward the walls or the corners or the empty air they addressed with equal conviction, and the approaching figure stood before her and the mask was close enough to see in detail, the detail being: a face of absolute conviction, a face that knew what it knew and did not doubt, and the not-doubting was painted into the mask's expression or was the mask's expression, the expression of a mind that had arrived at certainty by passing through doubt and out the other side, and the other side of doubt was not knowledge but this, this fixed and unshakeable occupation of a position no other mind could reach or verify or share, and the figure spoke to her, spoke directly, and the words were: *I am come to make thy tomb*.

She received the words. She did not respond. The not-responding was not silence — silence is the absence of speech in a context that expects speech, and this context did not expect speech, this context was not a conversation, this context was a performance, and she was the audience, the audience of one, the audience Ferdinand had constructed for this particular production, and the audience's function was to receive, and she received, and the receiving was the endurance, and the endurance held.

They danced. She could see now that the movement was dance — not the courtly measure, not the galliard or the pavan or any of the forms she had known in the time before this room, but a dance nonetheless, a dance in the sense that the bodies moved in relation to a rhythm they collectively maintained even as they individually violated it, the rhythm present as the thing being deviated from, the way her logic was present as the thing being surrounded by the eight other logics, and the dance was the masque, and the masque was the instrument, and the instrument operated upon her by surrounding her reason with its parodies, by placing her sanity inside a frame of insanities and allowing the frame to press inward, to suggest by proximity and by persistence and by the sheer committed energy of eight bodies moving according to eight irreconcilable imperatives that her sanity was not the ground state, that her sanity was merely one option among several, that the five words — the five words that held, that continued to hold — were not the foundation she had taken them to be but simply another private certainty, another mask's fixed expression, indistinguishable from the outside from the certainties the eight brought with them into her room.

This was the design. This was what Ferdinand had commissioned. Not the hand, which was horror. Not the figures, which were grief. This — this was the argument. The argument being: *your insistence on what you are — Duchess, wife, mother, the woman who chose and stands by the choice — your insistence is a form of the same fixity these eight demonstrate, and the fixity is madness, and the madness is yours as much as theirs, and the masque is the demonstration, and the demonstration is the stone, and the stone completes the arch, and the arch is me, your brother, your twin, and the arch holds everything, and under the arch you are contained*.

She stood at the window and the eight danced and Cariola trembled against her arm and the sound filled the room — the singing, the speaking, the rhythm of bare feet or shod feet on the stone floor, the percussion of eight bodies occupying a space meant for solitary confinement, the space now overfull, the overfullness itself a kind of violence, the violence of surfeit after the violence of deprivation, Ferdinand calibrating the sequence: first too little, then too much, first darkness, then the terrible light, first silence, then this, this cacophony of private meanings, and the calibration was precise and the precision was the art and the art was practiced upon her and she received it.

And the five words held.

Not because the five words were invulnerable. Not because the five words were beyond the reach of the eight dancers and their eight logics and the argument the masque advanced. The five words held because holding was what they did, holding was their function, and their function continued to operate in the room Ferdinand had made for them, the room that was testing the function to destruction, testing it with the hand and the figures and now with the eight, and the function had not yet reached destruction, and the not-yet was not triumph and not defiance but merely duration, merely the continuation of a process that had not yet concluded, the process of being Duchess of Malfi, of being the woman who chose and was imprisoned for the choice and was tortured for the choice and would, she understood now — the understanding arriving with the masque's argument, arriving as part of the argument's effect, the effect being not madness but clarity, the final clarity — would be killed for the choice, killed because the sequence required completion and the completion was death and the death would be the last stone, and under the arch of the completed sequence she would be contained, finally, completely, the containment Ferdinand had sought from the beginning, from the dark room where he had placed the poniard on her pillow and said *die*, from before that, from the warning in the presence gallery where he and the Cardinal had told her not to marry, from before that, from the womb they had shared, the original containment, the first room.

The eight danced. Cariola wept. The Duchess held. The room continued. The masque played to its conclusion, the conclusion being not an ending but a cessation, the eight withdrawing through the door the way they had entered, sound before bodies, the sound persisting after the bodies had gone, the sound reverberating in the stone walls the way the image of the wax figures still occupied the alcove behind the curtain, the image persisting after the figures had been — she assumed — removed, the persistence of sounds and images being the design's final mechanism: the torture does not stop when the instruments are withdrawn, the torture continues in the spaces the instruments have opened, the spaces now permanently open, and the room after the masque was not the room before the masque, and the Duchess in the room after the masque was not the Duchess before, was the same Duchess — *still* — but carrying additional knowledge, the knowledge being: the arch is nearly complete, and the completion is coming, and the completion will be Ferdinand's, and the five words will be hers, and between the completion and the five words there is the space of whatever remains, and whatever remains is narrowing, and the narrowing is the approach of the last stone, and the last stone will fall, and the arch will hold, and under the arch she will be contained, and the containment will be death, and the death will be — hers. Hers entirely. The one thing Ferdinand cannot commission. The one thing Bosola cannot perform. The dying that belongs to the one who dies, the final privacy, the final room, and in that room she will be Duchess of Malfi still, and the still will mean what it has always meant, which is: *continuing*, and the continuing will mean what it cannot mean, which is: *beyond this*, and the beyond will be the thing Ferdinand cannot reach, the thing the eight could not argue away, the thing the arch cannot contain because it is not under the arch but is the ground the arch stands on, and the ground holds.

The room after the masque was the room before the killing. She knew this because the room told her — not in the language of rooms, not in the creak of timber or the exhalation of stone cooling in the evening, but in the quality of the silence that followed the eight bodies' departure, the silence that was not the absence of sound but the presence of completion, the silence of a space that has performed its penultimate function and is composing itself for its last.

Bosola entered. He entered alone. He entered without the lantern he had carried in previous visits, without the ceremony of light-then-darkness that had structured the hand and the figures and the masque, and the absence of ceremony was itself ceremony, the final ceremony, the ceremony of sufficient light — the room's own grey ambient sufficiency, the late-afternoon light that pressed through the shutters' seams — being enough for what was to be done, because what was to be done did not require illumination, did not require the theatrical calibration of what is seen and when, because what was to be done was not a display. The displays were finished. What was to be done was an action.

He carried a cord.

She saw the cord the way she had seen the horoscope on the floor of her chamber in Amalfi years ago, the way she had seen the apricots Bosola had brought her, the way she had seen her own hand extended toward Antonio in the gallery where she had proposed marriage — she saw it as an object whose meaning preceded its appearance, an object that was already what it was before it arrived in her field of vision, the cord already a cord-for-strangling before Bosola's hand made it visible, the way the marriage had already been a marriage-that-would-end-here before the words *per verba de praesenti* had been spoken in the firelit chamber with Cariola behind the arras.

Bosola spoke. His voice was the voice she had come to know across the interval of imprisonment — the voice of the instrument, the voice that belonged to the man who had been commissioned to perform what Ferdinand had designed but who was not Ferdinand, who was the intermediary between the design and its execution, and the intermediary's voice carried the weight of intermediation, the weight of being the hands that do what another mind conceives, and the weight was audible, had always been audible, she had heard it from the beginning, from the apricots forward, the sound of a man performing a function he had accepted and could not now refuse and did not wholly wish to refuse and did not wholly wish to perform, and the not-wholly was the only human thing left in the mechanism Ferdinand had built around her.

"I am come to make your tomb," he said, or words to that effect — words she had heard already, heard from the masked figure who had approached her during the masque, the repetition confirming what she already knew: the masque had been the rehearsal and this was the performance, the masque had spoken the line in the subjunctive and now Bosola spoke it in the indicative, and the shift from subjunctive to indicative was the shift from *will be* to *is*, and the *is* was now.

She looked at Bosola and Bosola looked at her, and in the looking there was a transaction she could not have named but could identify — the transaction of the executioner and the executed, the transaction in which the one who will die and the one who will kill occupy for a moment the same knowledge, the knowledge being: *this is the last thing*, and the knowledge being shared, and the sharing being the most intimate act available in the room, more intimate than anything Ferdinand had designed, because Ferdinand had designed the tortures from outside the room and Bosola was inside the room and she was inside the room and the cord was inside the room and the three of them — woman, man, instrument — occupied the same air, and the air was finite, and the finitude was the intimacy.

"I am Duchess of Malfi still."

She said it. She said it not to Bosola and not to Ferdinand — Ferdinand was not in the room, Ferdinand had ordered the killing from whatever distance he maintained, the distance he had always maintained, the distance of the designer from the design's execution — and not to Cariola, who was behind her, Cariola whose breathing she could hear, Cariola whose body she could feel in the room's air the way she had felt it during the masque, a secondary presence, a witness, the last witness, the woman who had stood behind the arras when the marriage was made and who stood now behind the Duchess when the marriage's consequence arrived. She said it not to any of them and to all of them and to the room and to the air and to whatever persisted beyond the room and the air, and the saying was not defiance — defiance requires an opponent present to be defied, and Ferdinand was not present, and Bosola was not an opponent but an instrument, and instruments are not defied, instruments are endured — the saying was declaration, the declaration of a fact that had been true since the marriage and would continue to be true through the strangling and beyond the strangling into whatever came after, the fact being: she was. She was Duchess of Malfi. She was still. And the *still* carried both its meanings simultaneously, carried *continuing* and carried *motionless*, carried *persistent* and carried *quiet*, and the two meanings were not contradictory but complementary, the persistence expressed through the quietness, the continuing expressed through the not-moving, the not-moving being the posture she had arrived at through the hand and the figures and the masque, the posture of a woman who has been tested to the boundary of what testing can accomplish and who remains, at the boundary, what she was before the testing began.

The cord.

Bosola moved toward her and she did not move away. The not-moving was the *still* made physical, the *still* enacted in the body, the body that had carried children and carried a duchy and carried a secret marriage across years of concealment and carried the flight to Ancona and carried the capture and carried the imprisonment and now carried, as its final weight, the knowledge that Antonio was dead — the wax figures had told her this, the figures Ferdinand had displayed, Antonio's face rendered in wax with the artisan's fidelity, the wax-pale stillness of a face that had been warm and was now cold, and she had received the display as fact because what reason had she to doubt, what evidence could she marshal against the evidence of the figures, and the receiving of the fact was the last weight the body had taken on, the weight of believing that the man she had married was already where she was going, already in the place beyond the room, and the believing made the going easier, not easy but easier, the way a destination is easier to approach when someone you love is already there.

She knelt. The kneeling was her own. Bosola had not asked her to kneel and Ferdinand had not designed the kneeling — the kneeling was hers, the one gesture in the sequence that belonged entirely to her, and she performed it with the precision she had brought to every act of governance, every signed document, every diplomatic reception, every moment of the duchy's administration, the precision of a woman who understood that form matters, that the manner of a thing is part of the thing, and the manner of her death would be — this. Kneeling. Still. Duchess.

The cord settled against her throat. The cord was — she felt it as a physical fact, the last physical fact, the fibers of the cord against the skin of the throat, and the contact was ordinary, was merely the contact of one material surface against another, rope against skin, the ordinariness of it the most extraordinary thing, the most terrible thing, that after everything — the hand, the figures, the masque, the argument Ferdinand had built stone by stone — after everything, the killing itself was ordinary, was merely a cord and a throat and the pressure that would follow, and the pressure began, and she was still Duchess of Malfi, and the pressure increased, and she was still, and the still narrowed, and the narrowing was the cord, and the cord was the last room, and in the last room she was —

Still.

The room continued after she stopped. This is what rooms do. The room continued and her body was in the room and Bosola was in the room and Cariola was in the room, and Cariola — Cariola who had witnessed the marriage and the births and the flight and the capture and the imprisonment and the hand and the figures and the masque — Cariola saw the Duchess's body on the floor and the seeing broke the faculty that had held Cariola through everything prior, broke it the way a final weight breaks a structure that has borne every weight before the final one, and Cariola did not kneel, Cariola did not go still, Cariola fought. Cariola screamed and twisted and bit and clawed and invoked every instrument of survival the body possesses when the mind has abandoned governance, when the mind has been broken by the sight of the one it served lying strangled on the stone floor, and the fighting was — the fighting was the other way to die, the way the Duchess had not chosen, the way that resists and by resisting confirms the killer's power, because the killer must overcome the resistance, must apply greater force, must subdue the body that refuses to submit, and the subduing is the killer's act in a way the Duchess's death had not been Bosola's act, the Duchess's death had been — hers. The kneeling. The stillness. The five words. Hers.

Cariola's was Bosola's.

The cord found Cariola's throat as it had found the Duchess's, but the finding was contested, was a struggle, was Cariola's hands at the cord and Cariola's voice — not words now but sound, the sound of a body that does not want to stop being a body, the most fundamental sound, the sound beneath speech and beneath music and beneath the madmen's cacophony, the sound of biological refusal — and the sound continued and then the sound stopped, and the stopping was Bosola's work, and Bosola's work was done, and the room contained what the room now contained: two women dead on the stone floor, one kneeling and one sprawled, one still and one stopped, and the difference between *still* and *stopped* was the difference between the Duchess and Cariola, the difference between choosing and being overcome, and the difference was everything, and the difference was nothing, because both women were dead, and the dead do not distinguish.

Bosola stood in the room with what he had done. The cord hung from his hand. The room was quiet in the way rooms are quiet after violence — not silent but emptied, the air evacuated of whatever the living put into air by the act of living, the room now containing only Bosola's breathing and the absence of two other breathings, and the absence was louder than the masque had been, louder than Cariola's screaming, the absence filling the room the way the eight dancers had filled it, filling it more completely because the absence had no gaps, no intervals, no rhythm to parse, just the unbroken continuity of *not*, and the *not* was what Bosola had made, and the making was what he now knew, and the knowing was the cord in his hand and the bodies on the floor and the grey light through the shutters falling on the face he would not yet cover.

The Duchess of Malfi lay on the stone floor of the room that had been her prison and was now her tomb, and the five words persisted in the room the way the masque's sounds had persisted after the dancers withdrew, the words reverberating in the stone the way sound reverberates, not diminishing but changing, becoming part of the room's material composition, becoming mineral, becoming permanent, becoming the thing the room would always contain regardless of what was done to the room or who entered or what orders were given or what cords were deployed, the words embedded in the stone:

*I am Duchess of Malfi still.*

Ferdinand entered the room the way a man enters a room he has built — with the proprietary confidence of the architect approaching his completed structure, the satisfaction already forming in the body before the eyes have confirmed the work, the satisfaction of design fulfilled, commission executed, the mechanism he had set in motion with Bosola's hiring arriving now at its terminal function, and his body carried that confidence through the door and three steps into the room before his eyes found what his eyes had come to find, and the finding was — 

Not what the finding was supposed to be.

The finding was supposed to be confirmation. The finding was supposed to be the architect's verification: the structure stands, the commission is discharged, the thing that needed doing is done, and the doing can be inspected and approved and then the architect can leave, can return to the distance from which he had designed, the distance that was his proper relation to the work, the distance from which he had written the letters to Bosola and conceived the hand and specified the waxwork figures and commissioned the madmen and ordered the cord, the distance that had made everything possible because the distance was the condition of the design, the distance was what permitted a man to design the strangling of his twin sister, the distance being — 

But the distance was gone. The distance had been in the room when Bosola was in the room with the cord; the distance had been maintained by the intermediary, by the instrument, by the mechanism of commission-and-execution that meant Ferdinand's hands remained clean while Bosola's hands held the cord, and the mechanism had functioned, had performed exactly as designed, and Bosola had withdrawn, and the room was empty of intermediaries, and Ferdinand was in the room, and his sister was on the floor, and there was no distance. There was no distance because there was no mechanism between his eyes and her body, no Bosola, no letter, no commissioned instrument, nothing between the designer and the design's product, and the product was — 

She was on the floor. She was on the stone floor in the grey light, and she was kneeling, or the body maintained the posture of kneeling, the posture she had assumed before the cord — he knew she had assumed it, he knew it the way the architect knows the load-bearing wall holds the roof though he was not present during construction, he knew because the posture was hers, was unmistakably hers, was the posture of a woman who governed, who administered, who signed documents and received diplomats and managed the revenues of a duchy, a woman who would kneel at her own execution because kneeling was a form and form was governance and governance was what she did, and the kneeling was — 

He looked at her face.

He had not intended to look at her face. He had intended to look at the room, at the fact of the completed work, at the abstract confirmation that his order had been fulfilled, and then to leave, and the leaving would restore the distance, and the distance would be permanent, and the permanence would be — but he looked at her face because the face was there and his eyes were there and the room contained nothing else that could hold his eyes, the room having been emptied of everything except the bodies and the light and the stone, and his eyes went to her face the way his eyes had always gone to her face, in the court at Amalfi, in the chamber where he had confronted her with the poniard, in every room they had shared across every year of their twinned existence, his eyes went to her face because her face was the face that answered his face, the face that had been formed in the same womb at the same hour from the same blood, and the face was — 

The face was still.

The face was still the way she had said she was still, and he did not know she had said it, he had not been in the room when the five words were spoken, he had maintained his distance, his architect's remove, but the face told him what the words had told Bosola — the face told him *still*, told him *continuing*, told him *persistent*, and the persistence was in the set of the jaw and the closed eyes and the arrangement of the features that were his features, that were the features he saw when he looked in mirrors, the features that were the twin's features, and the mirror was — 

The mirror was broken. The mirror was on the floor. The mirror would not reflect again.

Something moved in Ferdinand's chest. Something shifted — not the way a thought shifts, not the way a decision shifts, but the way a foundation shifts, the way the ground shifts beneath a structure when the ground has been undermined and the undermining has reached the critical point and the structure does not lean or tilt or gradually subside but simply — moves, all at once, the entire foundation, and the structure above the foundation does not yet know it has lost its ground, the structure still stands for one moment after the foundation has gone, stands on nothing, stands on the habit of standing, and then — 

"Cover her face."

The words came out of him. The words came out of him the way the cord had come out of Bosola's hand — as an instrument's discharge, as the final mechanical action of a mechanism that has completed its function and is now completing something else, something undesigned, something the architect did not draw, and the words were — he heard them as he spoke them, heard them the way a man hears his own voice in a room that has changed acoustics, the room having changed because the room now contained what it contained, and what it contained changed everything about how sound moved in the space, and his voice sounded wrong, sounded like a voice he did not recognize, sounded like — 

"Mine eyes dazzle."

The dazzle. The dazzle was not light. The dazzle was the opposite of light — the dazzle was the collapse of the faculty that processes light into meaning, the faculty that takes what the eyes receive and renders it into a world the mind can inhabit, and the faculty was collapsing, was collapsing the way Cariola's faculty had collapsed at the sight of the Duchess's body, but Ferdinand's collapse was not outward, was not screaming and clawing and fighting, Ferdinand's collapse was inward, was the ground giving way beneath the structure, was the foundation moving and the structure not yet falling but no longer standing on anything solid, and the dazzle was the moment between the foundation's departure and the structure's fall, the moment of suspension, the moment when the eyes still see but the mind behind the eyes has lost the capacity to bear what the eyes deliver — 

"She died young."

Three utterances. Three fragments. Cover — dazzle — young. And the three fragments were the recognition arriving not as a single illumination but as a fractured sequence, each fragment carrying a portion of the knowledge that was now entering Ferdinand and that Ferdinand could not refuse and could not absorb and could not survive, the knowledge being: *he had done this*. Not Bosola. Not the cord. Not the commission or the mechanism or the distance. He. Ferdinand. Her twin. The face on the floor was the face that answered his face, and he had killed the face, and the killing was not — the killing was not what he had designed. What he had designed was punishment. What he had designed was correction. What he had designed was the enforcement of the prohibition he and the Cardinal had delivered in the opening scene, the prohibition against remarriage, the prohibition he had framed as political interest, as dynastic governance, as the management of the Aragonian bloodline, and the framing had held, had held through the hiring of Bosola and the letters and the confrontation and the flight and the capture and the imprisonment and the hand and the figures and the madmen and the ordering of the cord, the framing had held because the framing was the distance, was the architect's remove, was the thing that permitted a man to do what Ferdinand had done without knowing what Ferdinand had done, and now the framing was gone because her face was on the floor and her face was his face and the mirror was broken and what he saw in the broken mirror was — 

Himself. What he had done. What he was.

The knowledge entered him the way the cord had entered the room — as an object whose meaning preceded its appearance, an object that was already what it was before it arrived. And the knowledge did what knowledge does when it exceeds the capacity of the vessel it enters: it broke the vessel. Not visibly. Not yet. The breaking was internal, was the foundation completing its shift, was the structure beginning its fall, the fall that would take — days, weeks, however long it took for the lycanthropy to surface, for the wolf to crawl out of the man, for the madness that was born in this room at this moment to reach the body's surface and become visible to others — but the fall began here. The fall began with the face on the floor and the eyes that dazzled and the three words — *she died young* — that were the first words Ferdinand spoke as a madman, though neither he nor Bosola nor anyone in the palace knew yet that the man speaking them was no longer the man who had entered the room.

He stood over her body. He stood over his own face on the floor. The grey light through the shutters fell on both of them — the standing twin and the kneeling twin, the living twin and the dead twin — and the light did not distinguish between them, the light fell equally on both, and the equality of the light was the last true thing in the room, the last thing that had not been deformed by what Ferdinand had done, and Ferdinand stood in the equal light and was no longer Ferdinand, was no longer the Duke of Calabria, was no longer the architect, was no longer the brother who had warned and prohibited and commissioned and designed, was no longer anything he had been before he entered the room, because what he had been before he entered the room was a man who had not yet seen what his design produced, and now he had seen, and the seeing had unmade him, and the unmaking was — 

He did not cover her face. He had said *cover her face* but he did not do it. He could not do it. To cover her face would require approaching her face, and approaching her face would reduce the distance further, and there was no further to reduce, and his hands — his hands that had held the poniard in her chamber, his hands that had never held the cord because the cord had been Bosola's, his hands that were clean, that had remained clean throughout the entire design — his hands could not touch what his design had produced, because touching would complete the circuit the distance had interrupted, would close the gap between designer and product, and the closing of the gap was what the recognition had already begun, and the recognition was already more than he could bear, and touching would be — 

He turned from the body. He turned from his own face on the floor. He turned and the turning was the first motion of the madness, the first gesture of the structure falling, because the turning was not a man leaving a room, the turning was a man leaving himself, leaving the self that had entered the room, leaving the self that had designed the killing, and the self he was turning toward was not yet formed, was still forming, was the wolf-self, the digging-up-dead-bodies self, the howling self that would emerge in the weeks to come, and the turning was the hinge between the two selves, the hinge between Ferdinand-the-designer and Ferdinand-the-mad, and the hinge turned and the door opened and the room remained and the body remained and the five words remained embedded in the stone — 

*I am Duchess of Malfi still.*

— and Ferdinand carried out of the room, in the space where his foundation had been, the six words that answered hers — *cover her face mine eyes dazzle* — and the six words were not governance, were not administration, were not the precision of a woman who understood form, the six words were breakage, were fracture, were the sound a man makes when knowledge arrives that exceeds him, and the sound would persist in him the way her words persisted in the stone, would persist and metastasize and become the lycanthropy, become the wolf, become the madness that was the only structure left to a man whose design had succeeded and whose success had destroyed him, and the destruction was complete, was already complete, had been complete from the moment his eyes found her face on the stone floor, the face that was his face, the face that died young.

Bosola came back into the room because the room was his. Not his by right or title or any claim a man could speak aloud, but his by labour — his by the cord his hands had held, his by the act his body had performed, his by the function he had discharged, and the room with its product was the wages of the function, and the wages were his to collect, and he came back into the room to collect them, and what he collected was —

The room was empty of Ferdinand. The room was full of everything else.

She was on the floor. She was on the floor where he had left her, in the posture he had left her in, and the posture had not changed because the dead do not rearrange themselves, and the dead do not rearrange themselves because the dead are finished, are completed, are the final form of whatever they were becoming, and what the Duchess of Amalfi had been becoming was this — this kneeling figure on the stone, this stillness that he had produced the way a craftsman produces a chair or a clerk produces a ledger entry, produced it with his hands and his cord and his commission, and the product was before him, and the product was —

He had killed people before. He had killed people in the galleys — not directly, not with his hands, but the galleys killed, the galleys were a mechanism for killing, and he had been in the mechanism, had been a component of the mechanism the way an oar is a component, and people died in the mechanism and the dying was distributed across all the components and no single component bore the dying, and that was the galleys, and that was serviceable, that was endurable, that was the way a man survived the thing he had done for the Cardinal without the thing consuming him, because the consumption was distributed too, was shared among all the oars.

This was not the galleys. This was not distributed. This was — 

He looked at her face. Ferdinand had said *cover her face* and Ferdinand had not done it and Bosola had not done it and her face was uncovered and her face was — 

Her face was the face of a woman who had said five words to him before the cord, five words he had heard and registered and filed in whatever compartment a man files such things when the man is performing a function, when the man is discharging a commission, when the man is being an instrument and the instrument does not have the luxury of hearing words as words, the instrument hears words as sounds and the sounds pass through the instrument the way wind passes through a doorway, without altering the doorway, and the five words had passed through him — *I am Duchess of Malfi still* — had passed through him and he had proceeded with the cord because the cord was the commission and the commission was the function and the function was what he was, what he had agreed to be when Ferdinand had offered the provisorship and Bosola had taken it, knowing — 

Knowing. He had known. He had known in the courtyard when Ferdinand's hand extended the office and Ferdinand's voice named the work and Ferdinand's eyes held the particular quality of a man purchasing a capacity he does not wish to exercise himself, the quality of a man hiring his own darkness out to cheaper hands, and Bosola had known what the hiring was and had taken it anyway, had taken it because — because the galleys. Because seven years in the galleys for the Cardinal's murder and the Cardinal's silence afterward and the world's indifference and the returning to a court where no one remembered the service and everyone remembered the crime, and the office was the only thing offered and the only thing a man of his position could take, and the taking was — 

The taking was the error. He knew it now. He knew it now the way a man knows the ground after he has fallen from a height — by impact, by the body's registration of what the body has struck, and what he had struck was the stone floor of this room and the body on the stone floor, and the impact was — 

He knelt beside her. He did not decide to kneel; his body knelt, his knees found the stone, and the kneeling placed his face near her face, placed his living face near her dead face, and the proximity was — intolerable and necessary. Intolerable because the face was the face he had killed. Necessary because the face was the face he had killed, and the killing required proximity now, required the closing of the distance that Ferdinand could not close, required Bosola to be where Ferdinand could not be, near the product, near the wages, near the thing the instrument had made.

Her lips. Something about her lips. The arrangement of them, the not-quite-closed quality, the — was there breath? Was there warmth? He placed his hand — the hand that had held the cord — near her mouth, and the hand felt — 

Nothing. Air. The ambient temperature of a room that contained a body that was no longer producing heat. His hand felt nothing because there was nothing to feel, because the function had been thorough, because Bosola was a thorough instrument, had always been a thorough instrument, thoroughness being the only professional virtue available to a man whose profession was — 

What was his profession? What was his profession *now*? The commission was discharged. The provisorship was held at Ferdinand's pleasure, and Ferdinand's pleasure had just walked out of this room in a condition Bosola recognized — the condition of a man whose structure is falling, the condition Bosola had seen in the galleys when a man's mind gave way under the oar and the man continued rowing because the body remembered rowing after the mind forgot everything else, and Ferdinand was rowing, was performing the motions of governance and departure and ducal authority while the mind behind the motions was — elsewhere, was falling, was already mad though the madness had not yet reached the surface.

Ferdinand would not pay. Ferdinand would not reward. Ferdinand would not complete the transaction that the commission had initiated, because Ferdinand was no longer the man who had initiated it, and the man who had initiated it was gone, was being replaced by whatever Ferdinand was becoming, and whatever Ferdinand was becoming did not remember debts, did not honour instruments, did not complete transactions.

Was that why? Was that the reason? Was the reason *payment*? Was Bosola kneeling beside this body because the body represented unpaid labour, because the killing was work and work demanded wages and the wages would not come? Was his anger — and there was anger, there was something in his chest that was anger or that resembled anger the way a fire in a building resembles a fire in a hearth, same material, same combustion, entirely different in implication — was his anger the anger of the hireling, the labourer denied his pay, the instrument discarded after use?

He looked at her face again. He looked at the face he had killed and the face looked back at him with closed eyes, and the closed eyes were — 

He had told her things. In the prison, between the tortures Ferdinand had designed and Bosola had delivered — the hand, the figures, the madmen — in the intervals between these deliveries, Bosola had told her things. Had told her she was in a world of suffering, that the stars were governed by nothing, that the cosmos was indifferent, had delivered these speeches as one delivers medication or poison, unable afterward to determine which, and she had received the speeches and she had replied, and her replies were — precise. Her replies were the replies of a woman who governed, who administered, who understood form, who heard a nihilist's inventory of the world's emptiness and responded with five words that were the opposite of empty, five words that were full, that were governance itself, that were — 

*I am Duchess of Malfi still.*

The words returned. The words returned the way a knife returns when the knife has been thrown and strikes the wall and does not embed but bounces back toward the thrower, and the words struck Bosola now, struck him in the place the wind had passed through, struck the doorway and the doorway was no longer open, the doorway had closed, and the words did not pass through but remained, embedded, and the embedding was — 

He was angry with himself. That was the knowledge. That was what the kneeling and the proximity and the looking at the face produced — not the anger of the hireling, not the resentment of the unpaid labourer, or not *only* that, but something else, something that included the hireling's resentment but exceeded it the way the Duchess's five words exceeded their occasion, and the something else was: he had done this thing. He, Bosola. Not as instrument. Not as oar. Not as mechanism. He, the man who had accepted the commission knowing the commission was corrupting, who had identified the pregnancy with the apricot, who had dropped the horoscope, who had written the letters to Ferdinand, who had intercepted her in the countryside and brought her here, who had delivered every torture and held the cord and been thorough, been professionally thorough, and the thoroughness was not the instrument's thoroughness but *his* thoroughness, because there was no instrument, there was only a man who told himself he was an instrument so the man would not have to bear what the man was doing, and the telling was — 

The telling was over. The telling ended here, in this room, on this floor, with this face.

He was awake. He had been asleep — asleep throughout the commission, asleep through the apricots and the letters and the capture and the tortures and the cord, asleep in the particular way a man sleeps who cannot afford to be awake, who has made the waking-cost too high by what he has done in his sleep, and now he was awake and the cost was — 

The cost was in front of him. The cost was on the floor. The cost had a face and the face had said five words and the words were embedded in him and would not come out.

What does an instrument do when the instrument wakes and discovers it is not an instrument? What does an instrument do when it discovers it is a man who chose to be an instrument, who made the choice in a courtyard with Ferdinand's hand extended and Ferdinand's voice naming the terms and Ferdinand's eyes purchasing his capacity for darkness, who made the choice knowing — *knowing* — and proceeded anyway, and the proceeding produced this, this room, this body, this stillness on the stone?

The instrument becomes something else. The instrument was not the first thing Bosola was and it was not the last thing Bosola would be, and the thing between — the thing Bosola was now, kneeling on the stone beside the body he had made — the thing between was the pivot, was the hinge, was the moment when the mechanism reversed its direction and the reversal could not undo what the mechanism had done in its forward motion but could — 

Could what? Could avenge. The word arrived in him the way the commission had arrived — from outside, from a source he could not entirely identify, from the same place that had produced the anger and the waking and the knowledge that the instrument-story was a lie he had told himself to survive the telling. The word *avenge* arrived and the word was — insufficient. The word was insufficient because the avenging could not restore the body on the floor, could not un-hold the cord, could not un-strangle the Duchess or Cariola or the children, could not return Bosola to the courtyard and make him refuse Ferdinand's hand, could not undo the choice that was the error, the choice that was Bosola's version of the Duchess's choice — she chose concealment, he chose commission, and both choices produced this room. But the word, though insufficient, was the only word available to a man kneeling beside the body of the woman he had killed for the man who would not pay and who was already descending into the wolf-madness that was Ferdinand's version of this room, and the word would have to serve, would have to carry what Bosola needed it to carry, even though what Bosola needed it to carry exceeded the word's capacity, the way the Duchess's five words exceeded their occasion, the way Ferdinand's six words exceeded his.

He stood. He stood up from the floor, and the standing was different from the kneeling the way waking is different from sleeping, and the standing man was not the kneeling man, and the kneeling man was not the instrument who had entered the room with the cord, and the instrument was not the galley-convict who had returned to court with nothing, and the galley-convict was not the young man who had killed for the Cardinal before the galleys, and none of these men were the man standing now, and the man standing now was — 

Bosola. Daniel de Bosola. Avenger. The word tasted like iron in his mouth, tasted like the cord had felt in his hands, tasted like the galleys, tasted like the apricots he had fed the Duchess to provoke the vomiting that confirmed the pregnancy that began the sequence that produced this room. The word tasted like everything he had done and would do, and the taste would not leave, and he did not want it to leave, because the taste was the waking, and the waking was what the Duchess's face on the floor demanded of him, and he would answer the demand, would answer it the way he had answered Ferdinand's commission — with thoroughness, with professional capacity, with the full exercise of whatever he was — but the answering would be his own this time, would not be commissioned, would not be purchased, would not be the instrument's function but the man's choice, and the choice would be — 

His. For the first time since the courtyard. His.

He looked at her one more time. The grey light had not changed. The body had not changed. The five words had not changed. Nothing in the room had changed except Bosola, and the change in Bosola was — everything, and nothing. Everything because the direction had reversed, because the instrument had broken and the man had emerged from the instrument's casing and the man had a direction now that was not Ferdinand's direction but his own. Nothing because the body was still on the floor and the body would remain on the floor and no reversal of direction, no waking, no vengeance, no word in any language Bosola possessed could alter the fact that the body was on the floor because Bosola's hands had put it there.

He left the room. He left the room carrying the five words and the iron taste and the direction and the knowledge — the knowledge that he was awake now, that he was angry with himself, that the anger was the only honest thing he had felt since the courtyard, and that the anger would have to serve as his compass through whatever was coming, through whatever *mine is another voyage* turned out to mean, and the meaning was not available to him yet, was still forming, was still in the future the way the wolf was still in Ferdinand's future, and the future would arrive, and when it arrived, Bosola would be — 

Awake. Awake, and thorough, and damned.

The light was wrong. The light had been wrong since the room — since the room with the body — since the room he had left, and the leaving had not corrected the light, and the not-correcting meant the error was not in the light but in the apparatus that received the light, and the apparatus was his eyes, and his eyes were — 

His eyes dazzled. He had said that. He had said that in the room. He had said *mine eyes dazzle* and the dazzling had not stopped, had continued past the room and through the corridor and down the stairs and into the courtyard and across the courtyard and into whatever chamber he was in now — some chamber, a chamber in Amalfi, his sister's palace, his sister's — his sister was — 

The dazzling was not in his eyes. The dazzling was behind his eyes, was in the place behind the eyes where a man assembles the world from what the eyes deliver, and the assembly was failing, the assembly was producing shapes that were not the shapes the eyes were delivering, and the shapes the assembly was producing were — 

Fur. The shapes were fur. The shapes had the quality of fur, the dense close-packed quality, the quality of a pelt, and the pelt was — was on the walls, was on his hands when he looked at his hands, was on the floor where the floor should be stone, and the fur was — his. The fur was his. The fur was growing from his hands or his hands were becoming the thing that grew fur or his hands had always been the thing that grew fur and the growing had been concealed and the concealing had ended and the thing underneath the concealing was — 

He held his hands in front of his face. His hands. The hands of Ferdinand, Duke of Calabria. The hands that had held a poniard in a bedchamber and told her — told *her* — told the woman who was his sister, his twin, the other half of whatever he was, told her *die* and *die* and *this was her judgment* and the hands had meant it, the hands had carried the meaning from wherever meaning originates in a man's body to the surface where meaning becomes act, and the act was — the act was accomplished, the act was behind him, the act was in the room he had left, the act was lying on the stone floor with its face uncovered because he had told Bosola to cover her face and Bosola had not covered her face and her face was — 

Her face was his face. Her face had always been his face. They were twins, they had shared the same darkness before birth, the same water, the same containment, and the sharing meant that her face was a version of his face and his face was a version of her face and when he looked at her dead face he was looking at his own face dead, and the looking was — 

The looking was what had broken the apparatus. The looking was where the dazzling had begun. And the dazzling had not stopped and would not stop because the thing the eyes had seen could not be un-seen and the apparatus that assembled the world from what the eyes delivered had received her dead face and his dead face simultaneously and the simultaneity had — cracked the apparatus, fractured it, and through the fracture something was coming, something was pushing through from underneath, and the something was — 

The wolf. 

He knew it was the wolf because he could feel the wolf's knowledge replacing his own, feel the wolf's way of assembling the world superimposing itself on Ferdinand's way of assembling the world, and the wolf's way was simpler, was cleaner, was a world of scent and sound and the particular sharpness of teeth, and the teeth were — his teeth. His teeth were sharper than they had been. His teeth were the teeth of — 

He crouched. He did not decide to crouch; his body crouched, his body found the posture that was lower than standing and different from kneeling, a posture that placed his hands — his furred hands, his clawed hands, his hands that had held the poniard and pointed at her belly where the children were and said *I could kill you now* — placed his hands on the floor, on the stone, and the stone was cold under the pads of his — under his hands, under his *hands*, they were hands, they were the hands of a duke, they were — 

They were not. They were paws. The transformation was not a decision but a discovery, the discovery that the thing Ferdinand had been before the room was a costume, a human costume worn over the wolf the way a wolf-skin is worn over a man in a masque, except the direction was reversed, the man-skin had been worn over the wolf, and the room — the room with the body, the room with her face, the room with *cover her face* — the room had torn the man-skin and the wolf was emerging through the tear, and the emerging was — 

Relief. That was the unspeakable thing. The emerging was relief. The wolf did not know what Ferdinand knew. The wolf did not know about the commission or the cord or the face on the floor. The wolf knew about hunger and pursuit and the particular quality of moonlight on a graveyard, and the graveyard was — 

He could smell the graveyard. He could smell earth and the particular sweetness that earth acquires when earth contains the dead, and the dead were — everywhere beneath him, beneath the stone floor of this chamber the dead were in the earth, and the dead included — 

The dead included her. She was dead now and she would be in the earth and the earth would acquire her particular sweetness and the sweetness would enter the soil and the soil would feed the roots and the roots would feed the grass and the grass would grow over her and the wolf would stand on the grass and the wolf would smell her through the grass, through the roots, through the soil, would smell the sister-twin-other-half through the layers of earth and the smelling would be — 

He needed to dig. The knowledge arrived with the wolf's authority, with the wolf's directness, without the mediation of reason or politics or ducal prerogative. He needed to dig. He needed to get into the earth where the dead were, needed to open the graves with his hands — his paws — his instruments of excavation that served no duke and no cardinal and no intelligencer but only the wolf's need, and the need was — 

To find her. Under the earth. To dig down through the layers of dirt and stone and containment and reach the place where she was, the place where he had put her by his commission, by his purchasing of Bosola's hands and Bosola's cord, and having put her there he needed to follow her there, needed to dig to where she was, because they were twins and the twin does not survive the division, the twin does not persist as a single thing after the other half is removed, the twin becomes — 

This. The twin becomes this. This crouching shape in a chamber in Amalfi, this creature whose hands are becoming paws and whose teeth are becoming sharper and whose eyes no longer dazzle because the wolf's eyes do not dazzle, the wolf's eyes function perfectly in darkness, the wolf sees best when the light is gone, and the light was going, the light was leaving Ferdinand the way Ferdinand's reason was leaving, and in the darkness the wolf was — 

Home.

He opened his mouth. He opened his mouth to speak, to say something — to say *I am Ferdinand, Duke of Calabria, I am a man, I am not this thing* — and what came out was not words. What came out was a sound that began in his chest and rose through his throat and emerged from his mouth as — 

Howling. The sound was howling. The sound was the sound a wolf makes when the wolf is alone and the aloneness is intolerable and the wolf calls into the darkness for the other wolf, the pack-mate, the twin, the other half that is gone, and the calling receives no answer because the other half is dead, is on the stone floor of a room, is being put in the earth, and the calling continues anyway because the wolf does not understand *dead*, the wolf understands only *absent* and *present*, and the twin is absent and the calling is for the twin's return and the return will not come and the calling will not stop.

The sound filled the chamber. The sound traveled through the corridors of the Duchess's palace — the Duchess's palace, the palace that belonged to the woman whose face he could not uncover because his eyes dazzled, the woman whose face was his face, the woman he had killed by commission and cord and Bosola and the long chain of instruments that began with his own possessive fury and ended with her kneeling and the cord tightening and the life — 

The wolf did not think about chains of instruments. The wolf howled. Ferdinand howled. The distinction between Ferdinand and the wolf was — narrowing. Had been narrowing since the room. Would continue to narrow until there was no distinction, until Ferdinand was the wolf entirely, until the duke was consumed by the animal the duke had always been, the animal that had commissioned the murder and designed the tortures and held the dead hand out to her in the dark and shown her the wax figures of her husband and children and watched — had *watched* — through the traverse, had watched her receive each instrument of suffering and had felt — 

What had he felt? What had Ferdinand felt, watching? The wolf did not know. The wolf could not access what Ferdinand had felt because what Ferdinand had felt was the thing that had torn the man-skin, was the thing the apparatus could not assemble, was the thing that exceeded the apparatus's capacity and broke it, and the breaking produced the wolf, and the wolf was the shape the breaking took, and the shape was — 

On all fours now. On all fours on the stone floor of a chamber in Amalfi, and his shadow on the wall was — he did not look at his shadow on the wall. He did not look because the wolf does not look at shadows, the wolf is not interested in its own reflection, the wolf is interested only in the dig and the hunt and the howl and the absent twin, and the absent twin was — 

Dead. She died young.

The howling resumed. The howling was louder now, was fuller, was the howling of a wolf that has found its register, that has located the precise pitch at which the aloneness becomes audible, and the pitch was — 

The pitch was the pitch of a man screaming, transposed into a key that was not human, rendered in a register that no duke and no brother and no twin could produce, only the wolf, only the thing underneath the man-skin, only the truth of Ferdinand emerging through the torn costume of Ferdinand, and the truth was furred and clawed and its teeth were sharp and its eyes did not dazzle and it would dig, would dig in the graveyards of Amalfi, would dig with its paws through the earth that contained the dead, would dig until it found her or until the digging killed it, and the killing would be — 

Another voyage. The phrase surfaced from wherever Ferdinand's language was sinking to, surfaced briefly like a drowning man's hand above water, and then submerged again, and the wolf reclaimed the surface, and the surface was fur and claw and the long howl that had no words in it, and the no-words were — 

Sufficient. The no-words were sufficient. The no-words said everything Ferdinand's words had failed to say — said *I did this*, said *I commissioned this*, said *I am the thing that produced the room and the body and the cord*, said *cover her face* without covering her face, said *mine eyes dazzle* without the eyes, said *she died young* without the dying, because the dying was not hers alone, the dying was his too, the dying was the twin's dying, and the twin was dying now, was dying into the wolf, and the wolf was — 

Alive. The wolf was alive in the way that only the wolf could be alive — alive without conscience, alive without the knowledge that kills, alive without the face on the floor and the five words and the six words and the cord and the commission and the error. The wolf was alive, and Ferdinand was dying into the wolf, and the dying was — not relief, not punishment, not performance, not symbol, but all of these and none of these, the way a man who becomes a wolf is all of these and none of these, the way a brother who kills his twin sister and discovers that the killing has killed the part of him that was human discovers the discovery in a register that is not available to medicine or theology or moral philosophy but only to — 

The howl. Only to the howl.

Bosola heard it. Bosola, who had left the room carrying the five words and the iron taste, Bosola who was awake now, who was an avenger now, who was walking the corridors of the palace toward whatever his new direction would require of him — Bosola heard the howling and stopped, and the stopping was the stopping of a man who recognizes the sound he is hearing, who identifies the source and the meaning simultaneously, and the source was Ferdinand, and the meaning was — 

The meaning was that Ferdinand was gone. Not dead — the body persisted, the ducal body continued to crouch and howl and claw at the stone — but gone in the way that mattered, gone in the way that meant the commission-giver had departed and left behind only the wolf, and the wolf would not pay and would not explain and would not justify and would not complete the transaction, because the wolf was not party to the transaction, the wolf was what remained after the party to the transaction had been consumed by his own act, and the consumption was — audible, was traveling through the corridors of Amalfi, was filling the palace of the dead Duchess with the sound of her twin brother's disintegration.

Bosola listened. Bosola filed the sound in the place where he filed such things — not the place where the wind passed through, that place was closed now, sealed by the five words — but a new place, a place that was forming as the avenger was forming, a place that catalogued the evidence, that registered the debts, that mapped the terrain of what was owed and to whom and by whom and the howling was — evidence. The howling was evidence that the machine was breaking, that the brothers' machine — Ferdinand's obsession, the Cardinal's politics, Bosola's hands — was breaking apart, and the breaking would produce opportunities, and the opportunities would serve the direction, and the direction was — 

His. Still his.

He walked on. Behind him, below him, in whatever chamber Ferdinand had found or the wolf had claimed, the howling continued, and the howling would continue, and the howling was the sound of a man who had ordered his sister strangled and discovered too late that the ordering had strangled whatever in him was not wolf, and the discovery took the form of fur and claw and the long sound that carried through the palace of the dead Duchess, through the corridors her governance had once filled, through the rooms where she had administered and loved and concealed and borne children and been Duchess of Malfi still, carried through all of it and found no wall that stopped it, no door that contained it, because the sound was not containable, the sound was the sound of the thing underneath, and the thing underneath was — 

The wolf. Only and always the wolf. Ferdinand had carried it since birth, since the shared darkness, since the twinning, had carried it beneath the duke-skin and the brother-skin and the man-skin, and the carrying was over, and the wolf was out, and the wolf would not go back.

The howling filled Amalfi. The howling would fill Amalfi until there was no Ferdinand left to howl, and then there would be silence, and the silence would be worse.

The book lay on the table between them. It was a book of hours, vellum-bound, the gilt edges catching the candlelight in a manner the Cardinal found satisfactory — the gilt was recent, applied that morning by a craftsman who did not ask questions about the paste he was told to work into the binding, the paste that was not paste but something derived from a Neapolitan apothecary's particular knowledge, the knowledge that certain compounds enter the body through the skin of the hands, through the oils of the fingertips, through the moisture that gathers where flesh presses against surface, and the entering is — slow. Slow enough for a conversation. Slow enough for what the Cardinal required.

Julia had come to Rome because Julia always came to Rome. Castruchio's wife, the Cardinal's mistress, a woman whose uses had been — the Cardinal considered the word — *multiple*. Her uses had been multiple and her liabilities had been manageable and the ratio of use to liability had been favorable for the duration of their arrangement, and the duration had been considerable, and the consideration had produced — affection was not the word. The Cardinal did not traffic in affection. The consideration had produced *familiarity*, and familiarity in a woman of Julia's particular appetites produced a dangerous thing, which was the conviction that familiarity entitled one to knowledge, and the conviction had led Julia to Bosola, and Bosola had led Julia to — 

She knew. That was the difficulty. She knew about the Duchess. She knew because she had extracted it — extracted it from Bosola or from the Cardinal's own unguarded speech or from the particular mathematics of inference that women of Julia's intelligence performed upon the silences of their lovers — and the knowing made her dangerous, and the dangerous made her — 

Disposable.

The Cardinal watched her from across the table. She was beautiful in the particular way that Roman candlelight made women beautiful — the shadows generous, the angles softened, the skin acquiring a warmth that daylight would correct. She was speaking. She had been speaking for some time. The Cardinal had been listening with the portion of his attention that was not occupied with the book, with the binding, with the question of when her fingers would find the gilt edges.

"You are distracted," Julia said.

"I am puzzled in a question about hell," the Cardinal said.

This was true. Not in the way Julia would receive it — she would hear it as abstraction, as theology, as the kind of ornamental melancholy a churchman affects when his mind is elsewhere — but in the way the Cardinal meant it, which was: the question of hell had become, in recent weeks, less abstract than it had been. The question had acquired specificity. The question had acquired his sister's face, though the Cardinal had not seen his sister's face since Loretto, since the banishment ceremony he had performed in his vestments before the shrine, the ceremony that had stripped her of name and title and sent her onto the road where Bosola would find her, and the finding had led to — 

He did not rehearse the sequence. The sequence was known to him. The sequence was his sequence, co-authored with Ferdinand, executed by Bosola, and the execution was — accomplished. The Duchess was dead. The children were dead. Cariola was dead. Ferdinand was — the reports from Amalfi described howling, described a duke who crouched on all fours and clawed at stone, described something the Cardinal declined to credit as lycanthropy because the Cardinal did not believe in lycanthropy, the Cardinal believed in madness and the political inconveniences madness produced, and the inconvenience was considerable, and the consideration was — 

Later. The consideration would be later. Now there was Julia. Now there was the book. Now there was the particular management of a liability that had exceeded its usefulness.

"A question about hell," Julia repeated, and the Cardinal could hear in her voice the thing he had always heard — the curiosity that served as appetite, the appetite that served as intelligence, the intelligence that had made her valuable and was now making her lethal. "You are the Church's prince. You should have the answer."

"The Church provides the catechism. The catechism is not the answer."

"What is the answer?"

"That hell is a place one constructs without knowing one is constructing it. One makes decisions — political decisions, administrative decisions, decisions about the disposition of persons and the management of estates and the silencing of inconvenient relations — and each decision is a brick, and the bricks accumulate, and the accumulation is not visible from the inside because from the inside one sees only the next decision, and the next, and the architecture only becomes apparent when the building is complete and one discovers one is inside it and the door has — "

He stopped. He had said more than he intended. He had said more than was characteristic. The Cardinal did not soliloquize; the Cardinal calculated, and the calculation was clean, and the cleanliness was the thing that distinguished him from Ferdinand, whose calculations had been contaminated by — whatever Ferdinand's contamination was. The word for it existed in medical Latin and in theological Latin and in the particular Latin that described the relation of twins, and the Cardinal declined all three vocabularies because all three led to places the Cardinal did not go.

Julia was watching him with the expression she wore when she believed she was seeing through him. The expression was wrong — she was not seeing through him; she was seeing a surface that resembled transparency but was in fact another surface, a surface the Cardinal maintained for the purpose of being seen through, so that persons who believed they were seeing through him would stop looking and the thing behind the second surface would remain — 

His. The thing behind the second surface was his.

"I know about your sister," Julia said.

The Cardinal did not move. The Cardinal's face did not alter. The Cardinal's hands remained where they had been — folded, still, the hands of a churchman accustomed to being folded and still during the portions of the Mass that required folding and stillness.

"You know what about my sister."

"That she is dead. That you and Ferdinand arranged it. That Bosola was the instrument. That the children — "

"You have been speaking to Bosola."

"Bosola speaks to anyone who asks the right questions. Bosola is — altered. Something has changed in him. He came to Rome carrying something heavy, and the heaviness makes him — talkative."

"Talkative." The Cardinal considered the word. Bosola talkative was Bosola dangerous, and Bosola dangerous was — a problem the Cardinal would address, would address after Julia, would address in the sequence that the Cardinal's mind arranged such problems, the sequence being: immediate liabilities first, structural liabilities second, and Julia was immediate and Bosola was structural and the immediate preceded the structural in the Cardinal's private logic, and the logic was —

He understood it not because the room told him — the room told him about the Cardinal, about Ferdinand, about Bosola — but because the room's contents implied the Duchess's death the way an effect implied its cause, the effect being Ferdinand's madness-scarred face on the floor and Bosola's sword in the Cardinal's body and the whole terrible architecture of the chamber's carnage, and the cause — the cause that had set the machinery in motion, the cause that had produced the commission and the spying and the capture and the imprisonment and whatever followed the imprisonment — the cause was the Duchess. The cause was the Duchess's marriage. The cause was the Duchess's concealment. And the fact that the machinery had run to its conclusion — had consumed the machinists — meant that the machinery had first consumed — her.

Delio returned to the gallery.

He returned to the gallery where the boy stood, the boy who stood near his father's body with the stillness that children achieved when children were surrounded by things they did not understand and the not-understanding produced not panic but — quiet. The boy was quiet. The boy was quiet the way the palace was quiet, the way the dead were quiet, and the quietness was the boy's inheritance — the boy who would inherit whatever was left of the Duchess's estate, whatever was left of the duchy that the brothers had interdicted and that the interdiction had not erased, the estate being — legal. The estate being the legal fact that per verba de praesenti marriages produced, the fact of legitimacy, the fact of succession, the fact that this child — this small, tired, cold child standing in a gallery beside his dead father — was the Duchess of Amalfi's heir, was the son of Antonio Bologna and the Duchess of Amalfi, was the continuation of the line that the brothers had attempted to sever and that the severing had not — entirely — severed.

Delio took the boy's hand.

He took the hand and the hand was warm. The hand was warm because the hand was alive, because the boy was alive, because the boy was the one thing the catastrophe had not consumed, the one remainder, the one surviving digit in the catastrophe's accounting, and the surviving was — accidental. The surviving was the product of the Duchess's decision to send Antonio and the eldest son ahead to Milan, the decision being the decision that a mother made when a mother assessed the dangers and distributed the children according to the assessment, and the assessment was — correct. The assessment was correct in the way that the Duchess's assessments had been correct at the level of principle and catastrophic at the level of execution, but this one assessment — this one decision to separate the eldest from herself — had produced — survival. Had produced this hand. This warmth. This boy.

Delio led the boy through the palace. Delio led the boy away from the gallery and away from the Cardinal's chamber and away from the bodies that the palace contained, the leading being the function that survivors performed, the function of conducting the heir away from the catastrophe and toward — what. Toward whatever followed catastrophes. Toward the court, toward Pescara, toward the men who would receive the boy and acknowledge the boy and do for the boy what the dead could not do, which was — raise him. Which was tell him. Which was make of him whatever the future made of the material that the past deposited in the future's custody, the material being — this child.

These wretched eminent things. These brothers and this Cardinal and this instrument and this steward and this Duchess — these wretched eminent things that had burned through the play's duration with the particular incandescence that eminence produced and that the burning consumed, leaving — what. Leaving no more fame behind them than should one fall in a frost and leave his print in snow. The print in snow. The impression that the fallen body made in the frost, the impression that lasted until the frost renewed itself and the renewal erased the impression and the erasure was — complete. The Aragonese name erased. The Cardinal's office vacant. Ferdinand's duchy lapsed. Bosola's commission — terminated, by the commissioner and by the commissioned, simultaneously, on the same floor.

And the boy.

And the boy, who was not snow, who was not frost, who was not an impression but a body — a small body, a warm body, a body that breathed and whose breathing was the play's final sound, the sound that replaced the silence that the dead produced, the breathing of a child who did not know what he inherited or what the inheritance cost or what the cost had been paid in, the currency being — lives. The currency being the lives of his mother and his father and his uncle and his other uncle and the man who strangled his mother and the woman who witnessed his parents' marriage and the woman who was his mother's maid and the children who were his siblings, the younger ones, the ones who did not survive the catastrophe because they were not sent ahead, because the sending was selective, because the Duchess could send one and keep two and the keeping was — fatal.

The boy did not know these things. The boy knew Delio's hand. The boy knew the walking. The boy knew the cold and the tiredness and the particular quality of the night that the palace contained, the night being late, the night being the night in which everything had happened and in which the happening had concluded and the conclusion was — walking. The conclusion was a man and a boy walking through a palace in Rome, the man holding the boy's hand, the boy holding the man's hand, the holding being mutual, the mutuality being the play's final mutuality — not the mutuality of wounds, not the mutuality of killing, but the mutuality of hands. Of a surviving man and a surviving child. Of the past's custodian and the future's vessel. Of Delio, who would tell the story, and the Duchess's eldest son, who would — live.

Who would live.

In all our quest of greatness — like wanton boys, whose pastime is their care — we follow after bubbles blown in the air. And the bubbles burst. And the bursting is the play. And after the bursting, the air remains, and in the air, the boy breathes, and the breathing is — 

The breathing is the end.
