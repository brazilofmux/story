# Freytag's Pyramid

## One-line summary

A five-part structural model of tragic drama — exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, catastrophe — presented as a triangular diagram, derived by analysis of Greek tragedy and Shakespeare.

## Origin

Gustav Freytag, *Die Technik des Dramas* (1863), translated into English as *Technique of the Drama* (MacEwan, 1895). German critic and novelist; the book was intended as a practical manual for dramatists, not a universal theory of story.

## Core claim

A well-formed five-act tragedy has a triangular shape: dramatic intensity rises from an introduction, crests at a central turning point (*peripeteia*), and falls toward a catastrophic resolution. The apex of the triangle is not the end but the middle.

## Structural elements

1. **Exposition / Introduction** — setting, characters, initial situation established.
2. **Rising action / Complication** — an inciting event sets conflict in motion; tension accumulates.
3. **Climax** — the *central* turning point (not the emotional peak near the end, which is a modern misreading). The protagonist's fortunes pivot here.
4. **Falling action / Reversal** — consequences of the climax play out; the opposing force gains strength.
5. **Catastrophe** — final resolution; in tragedy, the protagonist's destruction.

Freytag also identified "moments" attached to these phases (e.g. the *exciting force* that launches rising action; the *tragic force* that drives falling action; the *moment of last suspense* just before catastrophe).

## What it explains well

- Five-act Elizabethan tragedy and classical Greek tragedy — the works it was derived from.
- The shape of dramatic irony: the audience sees the turning point and watches the protagonist fall.
- Why the *middle* of a play matters as much as its climax in the modern sense.

## What it doesn't explain / gaps

- **Comedy.** Freytag addresses it only glancingly; the pyramid is explicitly built for tragedy.
- **Novels and long-form prose fiction.** Freytag was a dramatist writing about the stage.
- **Modern narrative shapes** — non-linear, episodic, ensemble, or deconstructed structures.
- **The "climax in the middle" claim is routinely inverted in modern usage.** Most contemporary writing pedagogy places the climax near the *end* of the third act. This is not Freytag; it is a later bowdlerization.
- No account of theme, character interiority, or reader response — purely structural.

## Relationship to other theories

- Derived from Aristotle's *Poetics* (beginning, middle, end; reversal and recognition) but more prescriptive and more diagrammatic.
- Direct ancestor of the modern **three-act** model (merge exposition + rising action, treat falling action + catastrophe as act three). Three-act is essentially Freytag collapsed and re-centered on a late climax.
- Sibling of **five-act** teaching frameworks still used for Shakespeare, which are partly Freytag-inflected even when not cited.
- The shape survives, stripped of the "climax in the middle" claim, in countless diagrams of "the story arc."

## Formalizability

High, on its own terms, low as a universal theory.

- The five phases are nameable states. Transitions between them are event-triggered (*exciting force* → rising action; *tragic force* → falling action).
- An engine could label a sequence of scenes with a phase and check that required transition events occur.
- **But:** what counts as "the climax" vs. "rising action" is judgment-laden unless tied to a specific event type (e.g. protagonist's decision that forecloses the prior goal). Freytag himself defines these loosely.
- Formalizing Freytag properly requires first deciding *which* Freytag: the original "climax in the middle" model, or the degraded modern "climax near the end" version. They are different shapes.
