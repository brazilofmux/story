## Cross-cutting notes

### The descriptive–prescriptive axis

- **Descriptive:** Aristotle (mostly), Propp, Greimas, Barthes — analyzing what stories *are*.
- **Prescriptive:** Freytag, Field, McKee, Truby, Save the Cat — telling writers what to *do*.
- **Both at once:** Aristotle, Dramatica — claiming that the descriptive structure is also normative for well-formed stories.

Prescriptive theories are easier to formalize but narrower. Descriptive theories tend to be either so narrow (Propp: Russian wonder tales) they cover a subgenre exactly, or so broad (Aristotle) they underdetermine actual decisions.

### Plot vs. character primacy

Aristotle and Propp make plot primary. Modern novelistic theory (James, Forster) and much post-1970 screenwriting theory (Truby, McKee) argue character-driven plot. Dramatica attempts to collapse the distinction (the story is the mind, the mind contains both). No theory yet surveyed gives a clean account of how plot-primacy vs. character-primacy should be chosen by the author for a given work.

### What the theories collectively under-specify

- **Reader interiority.** Most theories say little about what the reader *experiences*. Sternberg's split of suspense / curiosity / surprise is an exception and deserves its own entry.
- **Theme in an implementable sense.** Dramatica tries hardest; its success is disputed.
- **Rhythm and pacing at scene level.** Structure is specified at act level, often at beat level, rarely at scene-transition level where most working writers actually plan.

## Open questions

- Is there a *substrate* (event log with knowledge projections, emotional dynamics, tension curves) that multiple theories can all be expressed *over*, rather than each theory being its own ontology?
- When theories disagree irreconcilably (climax-in-middle vs. climax-at-end, plot-primacy vs. character-primacy, GAS completeness vs. episodic legitimacy), do the disagreements reflect different *kinds of story*, different *evaluation criteria*, or genuine theoretical error?
- Which theories, if any, say anything non-trivial about stories longer than a film — multi-novel sagas, serialized TV, life-of-a-character epics?
