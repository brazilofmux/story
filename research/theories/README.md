## Cross-cutting notes

### The descriptive–prescriptive axis

- **Descriptive:** Aristotle (mostly), Propp, Sternberg, Greimas, Barthes — analyzing what stories *are*.
- **Prescriptive:** Freytag, Field, McKee, Truby, Save the Cat, Vogler — telling writers what to *do*.
- **Both at once:** Aristotle, Dramatica, Campbell — claiming descriptive structure is also normative.

Prescriptive theories are easier to formalize but narrower. Descriptive theories tend to be either so narrow they cover a subgenre exactly (Propp: Russian wonder tales) or so broad they underdetermine decisions (Aristotle).

### Plot vs. character primacy

Aristotle and Propp make plot primary. Modern novelistic theory (James, Forster) and much post-1970 screenwriting theory (Truby, McKee) argue character-driven plot. Dramatica attempts to collapse the distinction. No theory yet surveyed gives a clean account of how plot-primacy vs. character-primacy should be chosen for a given work.

### The reader axis is mostly empty

Of the theories surveyed, only **Sternberg** is genuinely reader-facing. Most others either embed assumptions about the reader (Aristotle's pity and fear; Campbell's psychological resonance; Save the Cat's emotional beats) or ignore the reader entirely (Propp, most formalist narratology). Sternberg's suspense / curiosity / surprise framework looks load-bearing for the engine precisely because **the substrate it requires (per-agent knowledge states over time, with the reader as an agent) is the same substrate required for dramatic irony, mystery, and reveal.** This is probably our single most actionable early architectural commitment.

### Template vs. substrate

The prescriptive theories (three-act, Campbell, Freytag, Save the Cat) are most useful as **templates in a library**, not as substrates at the root of the engine. Embedding any one of them at the root turns the engine into a generator for the subgenre it describes. The substrate should be at the level Aristotle and Sternberg operate at: events with causality, characters with knowledge, readers with interests.

### What the theories collectively under-specify

- **Reader interiority** — except in Sternberg.
- **Theme in an implementable sense** — Dramatica tries hardest; its success is disputed.
- **Rhythm and pacing at scene level.** Structure is specified at act level, sometimes at beat level, rarely at scene-transition level where most working writers actually plan.
- **Long forms beyond a film.** Almost all prescriptive theories assume a two-hour movie or a short-to-medium novel. Sagas, serialized TV, life-of-a-character epics are largely unexplained territory.
- **Non-Western forms.** Kishōtenketsu, various oral traditions, and forms outside the Mediterranean/Hollywood axis are underrepresented in this survey so far and in the field generally.

## Open questions

- Is there a **substrate** (event log with knowledge projections, emotional dynamics, tension curves) over which multiple theories can be expressed rather than each being its own ontology? Current working hypothesis: yes, and it is approximately the Sternberg-compatible substrate described above.
- When theories disagree irreconcilably (climax-in-middle vs. climax-at-end, plot-primacy vs. character-primacy, GAS completeness vs. episodic legitimacy), do the disagreements reflect different *kinds of story*, different *evaluation criteria*, or genuine theoretical error?
- Which theories, if any, say anything non-trivial about stories longer than a film?
- Is there a rigorous account of theme-as-argument that survives contact with Dramatica's opacity?
