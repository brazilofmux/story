# Three-Act Structure (Field and Descendants)

## One-line summary

The dominant prescriptive structural model of twentieth- and twenty-first-century Hollywood screenwriting: three acts (Setup, Confrontation, Resolution) separated by two major turning points called "plot points," with strong page-count conventions — a simplification and relocation of Freytag's pyramid that is culturally ubiquitous and, in its standard form, a craft heuristic rather than a theory.

## Origin

The three-act label has been applied to Western drama for centuries, but the *modern specific form* traces to:

- **Syd Field**, *Screenplay: The Foundations of Screenwriting* (1979). The foundational text. Defined the now-standard three-act screenplay paradigm with explicit page counts and plot-point placements. Field's own contribution was less inventing three-act structure than codifying it into a teachable, measurable Hollywood norm.
- **Precursors:** classical Aristotelian beginning/middle/end; the Freytag pyramid collapsed to three phases; neoclassical stage-play act divisions. Field synthesized and made the synthesis concrete.

## Core claim

A well-formed screenplay (and by extension a well-formed film story) has three acts with specific proportional durations and explicit transition events:

- **Act One (Setup, ~25%).** Establishes protagonist, world, status quo, and inciting incident. Ends with **Plot Point 1**, a decision or event that locks the protagonist into the main story problem.
- **Act Two (Confrontation, ~50%).** The protagonist pursues the goal against escalating obstacles. Usually contains a **Midpoint** (a major reversal, revelation, or escalation of stakes, added or emphasized by successors to Field such as Blake Snyder). Ends with **Plot Point 2**, the all-is-lost moment that forces the final choice.
- **Act Three (Resolution, ~25%).** Climax and denouement. The protagonist succeeds, fails, or transforms; the story's question is answered.

For a standard ~110-page American screenplay Field originally gave page-count conventions (e.g. Plot Point 1 around pages 25–27; Plot Point 2 around pages 85–90). These numbers became craft folklore.

## Structural elements

- **Three acts.**
- **Inciting incident** (early in Act One; sometimes identified as the opening image + theme stated + catalyst triplet in Save-the-Cat-derived traditions).
- **Plot Point 1 / First Act Break** — the protagonist's commitment to the journey; the door closes behind them.
- **Midpoint** — not in Field's original as a primary beat, but universally added by successors (Snyder, McKee, Truby). Reversal, revelation, or raising of stakes.
- **Plot Point 2 / Second Act Break** — low point, all-is-lost, dark night of the soul.
- **Climax** — confrontation with the central opposition.
- **Resolution / denouement** — new equilibrium.

## What it explains well

- **Hollywood feature films of the last 50 years.** The theory is self-fulfilling in a sense: writers trained on Field write screenplays that fit the paradigm.
- **Why Act Two feels like it sags** in so many films. The paradigm does not natively supply a midpoint; films without one hit a structural dead zone around page 55.
- **Pacing expectations for a paying audience.** The page-count conventions roughly track attention, investment, and rhythm for a two-hour narrative.

## What it doesn't explain / gaps

- **Why the climax is late.** Field places the climax in Act Three, unlike Freytag's original (climax in middle). The paradigm does not justify this structurally — it is a craft convention, not a derived result.
- **Television.** Episodic television, serialized streaming, and anthology forms do not map to three acts; attempts to force them (seven-act TV, four-act procedural) multiply the paradigm without explaining anything new.
- **Novels.** Novels are not screenplays. The page-count conventions are inapplicable. The *shape* sometimes holds; the *beats* rarely do.
- **Non-Western forms.** Kishōtenketsu (Japanese four-part structure) and other traditions do not fit and are not illuminated by the three-act template.
- **Why it works when it works.** The paradigm is descriptive-of-practice more than explanatory. It tells you what successful Hollywood screenplays tend to look like; it does not tell you why those shapes produce the effects they do.

## Relationship to other theories

- **Freytag, collapsed and reweighted.** Exposition + rising action → Act One + first half of Act Two; climax → Plot Point 2 and the Act Three climax; falling action + catastrophe → rest of Act Three. The "climax in the middle" of original Freytag is gone; the midpoint is a nod to it.
- **Successor frameworks** — **Save the Cat** (Blake Snyder, 2005, with a 15-beat sheet that tiles onto three acts), **Story Grid** (Shawn Coyne, focus on obligatory scenes and genre conventions over pure act structure), **McKee's *Story*** (act structure plus more attention to controlling idea and character transformation), **Truby's *22 Steps*** (rejects the three-act paradigm as superficial; offers a longer character-driven sequence instead) — all engage with, extend, or explicitly oppose the Field paradigm.
- **Orthogonal to** narratological theory (Genette, Propp, Sternberg): three-act is a writer's heuristic, not a descriptive analysis of narrative form.

## Formalizability

High, **but shallow**. Three-act is the easiest theory in this survey to encode and the least theoretically ambitious.

- **Encoding.** Three named phases, a small set of transition events (inciting incident, plot point 1, midpoint, plot point 2, climax). An engine can check whether a generated story has these beats and roughly where.
- **As a generation target.** Many existing LLM-era story pipelines (Dramatron among them) explicitly use three-act scaffolds. The paradigm's commercial ubiquity makes it a natural default.
- **Danger.** Three-act is so easy to check that it can become a false test of story quality. A story can satisfy every three-act beat and still be dead; a story can violate the paradigm and be alive. Using three-act as the primary structural constraint embeds Hollywood conventions into a putatively general engine. For a general story engine, three-act belongs in the *library* of structural templates, not at the root.
