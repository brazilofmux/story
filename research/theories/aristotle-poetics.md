# Aristotle's *Poetics*

## One-line summary

The foundational Western treatise on narrative art (c. 335 BC), analyzing tragedy as a structured imitation of action with six essential elements and organizing principles — plot supremacy, unity of action, reversal, recognition, and catharsis — from which most later Western story theory descends.

## Origin

Aristotle, *Peri Poietikēs* (*On Poetry*), c. 335 BC. Surviving text is fragmentary and almost certainly a set of lecture notes rather than a polished treatise; the promised second book, on comedy, is lost. Principal surviving concern is tragedy; epic and comedy are treated briefly or by comparison.

## Core claim

A tragedy is an *imitation* (*mimēsis*) of a serious, complete action of sufficient magnitude, in heightened language, performed rather than narrated, effecting through pity and fear the *catharsis* of such emotions. Plot — the arrangement of events — is the most important element; character serves plot, not the reverse.

## Structural elements

Six constitutive parts of tragedy, ranked by Aristotle in rough order of importance:

1. **Plot (mythos)** — the arrangement of the incidents. "The soul of tragedy."
2. **Character (ēthos)** — the moral qualities of the agents.
3. **Thought (dianoia)** — what characters think, argue, reveal through speech.
4. **Diction (lexis)** — the verbal expression.
5. **Song / melody (melos)** — the choral music.
6. **Spectacle (opsis)** — the visual production. Ranked last; Aristotle considers it extrinsic to the art.

Structural principles of plot:

- **Unity of action.** A whole has a beginning, a middle, and an end; removing any part damages the whole. Plot is not a life-story; it is *one action*.
- **Magnitude and order.** Length must allow the change from good to bad (or bad to good) fortune to occur *kata to eikos ē to anagkaion* — according to probability or necessity.
- **Complex plots** include **peripeteia** (reversal — a change of fortune to the opposite) and **anagnorisis** (recognition — a change from ignorance to knowledge, often of identity). The best tragedies combine both in the same stroke (Aristotle's exemplar: *Oedipus Tyrannus*).
- **Hamartia** — the protagonist's error or missing-the-mark that brings about the reversal. Often mistranslated as "tragic flaw"; more accurately a mistake, often one made in ignorance.
- **Catharsis** — the purgation, purification, or clarification of pity and fear in the audience. The term is contested and Aristotle defines it nowhere fully.

## What it explains well

- Greek tragedy as a unified art form, on its own terms.
- Why *Oedipus Tyrannus* feels structurally perfect: the anagnorisis and peripeteia coincide; all prior action converges on a single recognition.
- The enduring intuition that narrative coherence is a matter of *probability or necessity* rather than literal truth ("that which is convincing though impossible is preferable to that which is possible but unconvincing").
- The basic form underlying almost every later Western structural theory: something changes, and the change is causally earned.

## What it doesn't explain / gaps

- **Comedy.** The relevant book is lost. What survives is a short list of contrasts with tragedy.
- **Long-form prose.** The novel did not exist as a form. Unity-of-action prescriptions strain against the digressive, polyphonic novel.
- **Character as the engine of story.** Aristotle subordinates character to plot; much modern writing theory inverts this. Both may be right about different kinds of story.
- **Theme** in the modern sense (*what the story is about*) is treated under *dianoia* (thought) and only at the level of what characters say, not what the work means.
- **Reader/audience interiority** beyond pity and fear. No account of curiosity, surprise, or immersion as separable effects (Sternberg would much later split these apart).
- **The "three unities"** (action, time, place) often attributed to Aristotle are **mostly a Renaissance fabrication.** Unity of action is Aristotelian. Unity of time is an observation (tragedy "tries to confine itself to a single revolution of the sun or nearly so"), not a rule. Unity of place is absent from the *Poetics*.

## Relationship to other theories

- **Ancestor of essentially every Western structural theory of story.** Freytag, three-act, Campbell, McKee, Truby, Story Grid — all inherit plot supremacy, unity of action, and causal reversal from Aristotle, whether they cite him or not.
- **Early opposition** from Horace's *Ars Poetica* (more prescriptive, more decorum-focused) and later Renaissance neoclassicists (who over-systematized Aristotle into the "three unities").
- **Opposed by** Brechtian theory (rejects catharsis as politically narcotizing) and by structuralist narratology (Propp, Barthes, Genette — which bracket questions of dramatic effect to look at underlying form).
- **Direct engagement with plot-vs-character primacy** continues to divide craft theory: Aristotle (plot), Henry James / E. M. Forster (character), McKee (plot-character as inseparable), Truby (character-driven plot).

## Formalizability

Medium. The structural vocabulary is nameable; the judgments are not.

- **Nameable:** unity of action (one goal-thread), beginning/middle/end, peripeteia (a reversal event), anagnorisis (a knowledge-state change in the protagonist), hamartia (a mistaken action attributable to a specific ignorance).
- **Implementable checks:** does the plot reduce to one action? Is there a peripeteia? Does it coincide with anagnorisis? Is the protagonist's reversal traceable to hamartia and not mere accident?
- **Resists formalization:** catharsis (defined nowhere fully, effect on a reader), "probability or necessity" (requires a model of the reader's world-expectations), *megethos* — the right magnitude of a plot.
- **Useful sub-primitive:** anagnorisis is a pure knowledge-state change, which maps directly onto the event-log + per-agent-knowledge-projection substrate already discussed. Aristotle's best structural device is, in our terms, a query on the knowledge graph.
